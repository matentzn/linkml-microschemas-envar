## Conceptual Foundations: Environmental Exposure Data and Metadata Requirements

### Environmental-exposure data and the analytic stack

Environmental-exposure data can be understood as observations or estimates of environmental conditions—temperature, particulate matter concentrations, ozone levels, wildfire smoke plumes, noise, green space accessibility—linked to human or population units over space and time.[1] Most exposure datasets are not raw measurements but results of multistep analytic pipelines that start from primary environmental observations (satellite retrievals, station readings, model outputs) and end with exposure metrics assigned to individual addresses, census tracts, or patient records through geocoding, spatial joins, temporal aggregation, and sometimes complex statistical modeling.[18][20] Fowler et al. propose an “analytic stack” perspective on reproducible computational research, distinguishing metadata that describes input data, tools, pipelines, intermediate results, and final outputs, and emphasizing that reproducibility demands metadata across all these layers.[1] This framework maps naturally onto environmental exposure workflows, where the metadata required to interpret a PM\(_{2.5}\) exposure estimate includes not only the pollutant dataset and coordinate reference system but also the geocoder, spatial buffer, temporal aggregation window, and health-data linkage strategy used.[18][20]

The analytic stack framing underscores why traditional dataset-centric metadata alone is insufficient for exposure science. ISO 19115-style geospatial metadata focuses on identifying datasets, describing spatial and temporal extent, quality, and distribution, but typically treats datasets as static products without fully modeling the analysis steps used to derive them.[11][2] Even climate-focused conventions like the Climate and Forecast (CF) metadata standard and Attribute Convention for Data Discovery (ACDD) emphasize variable semantics, coordinate systems, and discovery-oriented attributes rather than instance-level provenance or pipeline semantics.[14][15] As Fowler et al. argue, reproducible computational research requires metadata that documents both scientific concepts and the underlying computing environment, including software versions, configuration parameters, and workflow structure.[1] Environmental-exposure pipelines are subject to all these challenges and add additional complexity through geocoding, spatial aggregation, and linkage to health outcomes, making provenance and process metadata even more critical.

A robust conceptual model for environmental-exposure metadata must therefore address at least four levels. First, **dataset-level metadata** describing the environmental datasets used (e.g., satellite aerosol optical depth products, reanalysis temperature grids) and any intermediate exposure surfaces generated. Second, **variable-level metadata** defining exposure metrics, their physical units, spatial and temporal resolutions, and statistical properties. Third, **instance-level metadata** documenting how exposure values were assigned to specific individuals, addresses, or population units, including geocoding details and linkage rules.[18][20] Fourth, **provenance metadata** describing the analytic pipeline, software environment, and parameterization used to generate exposure estimates, ideally in a machine-interpretable form suitable for reproducibility and meta-analysis.[1][10] Many existing standards cover one or two of these levels well but rarely all four, which is a central limitation highlighted throughout this survey.

### FAIR principles, reuse, and meta-analysis in exposure science

The FAIR (Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, Reusable) principles have become a benchmark for data-management practice across scientific disciplines, and environmental exposure datasets are increasingly expected to conform to them.[1] For such datasets to be findable and accessible, catalog-level standards like DCAT and schema.org Dataset play a key role, allowing exposure collections to be indexed by general-purpose and domain-specific data portals.[4][5][6] Interoperability and reuse, however, demand not only identifiers and high-level descriptions but also shared semantics for variables, units, coordinate systems, and observational context, which standards like CF metadata conventions, ISO 19156/OGC Observations and Measurements, and domain-specific ontologies attempt to provide.[3][13][14] Yet as Fowler et al. note, the mere existence of standards does not guarantee their consistent implementation; significant heterogeneity in how metadata fields are populated can undermine interoperability even when nominally standard-compliant schemas are used.[1]

From the perspective of exposure meta-analysis, the bar for **reusability** is especially high. Meta-analysts must not only know what exposures were measured or estimated but also how they were computed, including specific modeling choices, aggregation windows, and linkage strategies that can substantially affect effect estimates.[18][19][20] Most general metadata standards were not designed with this level of detail in mind, focusing instead on discovery and citation. DataCite metadata, for example, provides rich descriptive elements for identifying and citing datasets but does not attempt to model analytic pipelines or instance-level derivations.[9] W3C PROV offers a generic framework for representing provenance but is rarely used systematically in environmental exposure pipelines, and even when applied, tends to capture only parts of the workflow.[10] Consequently, many exposure datasets advertised as FAIR still fall short of supporting robust meta-analysis, because key provenance information survives only in prose descriptions, code repositories, or pipeline sidecars not integrated into formal metadata standards.[1][10][18][19][20]

A skeptical reading of the FAIR discourse suggests that environmental-exposure metadata efforts risk being over-focused on openness and discoverability while neglecting the harder problem of **computational reproducibility**.[1] Fowler et al. explicitly warn that reproducible research requires extensive metadata about software environments and workflows, not just data descriptors.[1] Yet few exposure datasets provide machine-readable records of the exact pipeline used to generate exposures, even when geospatial standards like ISO 19115 and CF are employed.[11][14][15] This gap reflects both technical challenges and social incentives: investing effort in detailed provenance documentation yields little visible reward compared to publishing new exposure-outcome associations, and funding structures rarely prioritize metadata development.[1] These limitations must be confronted directly when designing an environmental-exposure metadata registry, lest the registry replicate existing standards’ focus on static data products while ignoring the dynamic processes that produce exposure estimates.

## Geospatial and Earth-Science Metadata Standards Relevant to Environmental Exposures

### ISO 19115 and EPA metadata specifications

ISO 19115 is the canonical international standard for geospatial metadata, defining a comprehensive schema for describing geographic information and services.[11] ISO 19115-1:2014 specifies metadata classes for identification, extent, quality, spatial and temporal characteristics, content, spatial reference, portrayal, distribution, and other properties of digital geographic data and services, and has been widely adopted by national mapping agencies and environmental data providers.[11] The United States Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) Metadata Technical Specification is explicitly based on ISO 19115 and related standards, providing guidance on how EPA geospatial data should be described, including topic categories, keywords, spatial and temporal extent, and data quality indicators.[2] The EPA specification aims to ensure consistent metadata across the agency, facilitating dataset discovery and integration through shared ISO-based descriptors.[2]

In the context of environmental-exposure data, ISO 19115 and the EPA specification primarily address **dataset-level metadata**. They enable cataloging of base environmental datasets—such as air quality rasters, temperature grids, land cover maps—that feed exposure pipelines, and can also describe exposure surfaces generated by aggregating such data to population units.[2][11] Key elements relevant to exposures include MD_DataIdentification (title, abstract, purpose), MD_Keywords (including CF or GCMD keywords), EX_Extent (bounding boxes and temporal periods), and DQ_DataQuality (lineage statements describing production processes).[11][2] However, the standard is largely silent on instance-level exposure assignments; it does not natively model the linkage between environmental datasets and specific individuals or addresses, nor the geocoding and spatial join processes used.[11] Lineage metadata can be used to outline workflows, but in practice, lineage fields are often populated with brief narrative descriptions rather than formal process graphs, limiting their usefulness for computational reproducibility.[1][11]

A recurrent criticism of ISO 19115 and related geospatial metadata standards, especially from smaller research groups, is their **complexity and implementation burden** (unverified). The standard defines hundreds of optional elements and multiple profiles, making full compliance challenging without dedicated tooling and expertise.[11] As a result, many geospatial datasets in practice have sparse or inconsistent ISO metadata, or rely on minimal profiles that emphasize identification and extent over lineage and quality.[2][11] Fowler et al. highlight that incomplete or inconsistent metadata is a major barrier to reproducible computational research, and geospatial datasets are no exception.[1] From an environmental-exposure standpoint, ISO 19115 thus provides essential building blocks for dataset discovery but cannot, by itself, guarantee that exposure pipelines are transparently documented; its lineage constructs are underused and too free-form to support instance-level reproducibility in a standardized way.[1][11]

The canonical reference for ISO 19115-1 is the ISO catalogue entry at https://www.iso.org/standard/53798.html.[11] The EPA Metadata Technical Specification is published at https://www.epa.gov/geospatial/epa-metadata-technical-specification and functions as a national profile of ISO 19115 with additional agency-specific rules.[2] Both are **general-purpose geospatial standards** rather than environmental-health-specific formats, and their primary purposes are dataset discovery and cataloging. They carry limited provenance metadata through lineage elements but do not structure analytic pipelines in detail, and thus they support reproducibility only at a high level. They are clearly **general standards**, not pipeline sidecars.

### ISO 19156 and OGC Observations and Measurements

ISO 19156:2011, titled “Geographic Information — Observations and Measurements,” defines a conceptual schema for observations and sampling features, intended to model observation acts and their results across scientific domains.[3] The standard provides classes for OM_Observation (linking an observed property, feature of interest, procedure, and result), OM_Process (describing the measurement or model), and various sampling features representing the spatial entities involved in observation.[3] The Open Geospatial Consortium’s Observations, Measurements, and Samples (OM) standard builds on the same conceptual foundations, specifying models for the exchange of information describing observation acts and their results across scientific and technical communities.[13] Together, ISO 19156 and OGC OM offer a rich, generic framework for modeling environmental observations, including environmental exposures conceived as observations of environmental conditions relative to human or population units.[3][13]

Unlike ISO 19115, which focuses on datasets, ISO 19156 and OGC OM are oriented toward **observation instances**. An OM_Observation can describe a single temperature reading at a sensor, a modeled PM\(_{2.5}\) concentration at a grid cell, or an exposure estimate for a specific address, by specifying the observed property (e.g., “air temperature”), the feature of interest (sensor location or residential address), the procedure (thermometer, model, or exposure assignment algorithm), and the result (a numeric value with units and time).[3][13] This structure is highly relevant for environmental exposure data, as it can potentially capture instance-level exposure values and their derivation context. For example, an exposure assignment algorithm that interpolates gridMET temperature data to patient addresses and aggregates to daily averages could be modeled as an OM_Process, with each patient-day exposure represented as an OM_Observation referencing that process.[3][13] In principle, this supports **instance-level provenance**, as each exposure value is linked to the procedure that produced it and the feature of interest it pertains to.

In practice, however, adoption of ISO 19156/OGC OM in exposure science appears limited (unverified). Many environmental data providers and geospatial systems focus on ISO 19115 metadata and CF/ACDD conventions for gridded data, while OM is more commonly used in sensor networks and hydrological data systems.[3][13] The standard is also conceptually demanding; implementing OM_Observation structures across large exposure datasets would require substantial modeling effort and tooling, which few exposure-focused projects have invested in (unverified). The standard is further complicated by its withdrawal status in the ISO catalogue—ISO 19156:2011 is marked as withdrawn, although OGC continues to maintain its Observations and Measurements specification.[3][13] This regulatory ambiguity may discourage new adopters, despite the conceptual model remaining sound for exposure provenance.

The canonical ISO entry for ISO 19156 is https://www.iso.org/standard/32574.html.[3] The OGC OM standard is documented at https://www.ogc.org/standards/om/.[13] These are **general standards**, domain-neutral and focused on modeling observations rather than environmental health specifically. Their primary purpose is to define observation structures for interoperability and exchange, with strong support for **instance-level provenance** via OM_Observation and OM_Process. However, uptake in environmental exposure pipelines is weak, and the complexity of full OM implementation has been criticized by practitioners (unverified). Consequently, while OM offers a powerful path toward instance-level exposure metadata, it remains more a conceptual template than a widely deployed solution in the exposure community.

### CF Metadata Conventions and Attribute Convention for Data Discovery (ACDD)

The Climate and Forecast (CF) metadata conventions are a community standard for describing variables, coordinates, and ancillary attributes in NetCDF files containing Earth science data, including in situ, airborne, satellite, and model outputs.[14] CF conventions specify how to represent geolocations and times of Earth science data, physical quantities that data represent, and other ancillary information useful in interpreting data or comparing it with other sources.[14] CF includes standard names for physical quantities, rules for coordinate variables, and attributes that convey variable semantics, units, and metadata about cell methods (aggregation techniques). CF has been nominated as a NASA Earth Science Data Systems community standard and is widely used in climate and atmospheric science data.[14] The Attribute Convention for Data Discovery (ACDD), developed under the Federation of Earth Science Information Partners (ESIP), describes recommended attributes for NetCDF datasets to facilitate discovery in digital libraries and data portals.[15] ACDD defines dataset-level attributes like title, summary, keywords, geospatial bounds, time coverage, and use constraints, complementing CF’s variable-level semantics.[15]

For environmental-exposure data, CF is crucial wherever exposures are derived from gridded climate or air-quality datasets stored in NetCDF. The CF standard names define the physical meaning of variables (e.g., “air_temperature”, “mass_concentration_of_pm2p5_in_air”), while coordinate attributes specify spatial and temporal frameworks, enabling consistent interpretation across datasets.[14] CF’s cell_methods attribute can indicate how variables have been aggregated (e.g., temporal mean, maximum), which is directly relevant to exposure metrics that use daily maxima or long-term averages.[14] ACDD’s discovery attributes make environmental datasets findable and provide high-level context about geospatial and temporal coverage, crucial for identifying datasets suitable for exposure estimation.[15] Together, CF and ACDD can describe both base environmental datasets and derived exposure surfaces, though they do not explicitly model exposure assignment to individual humans or populations.[14][15]

From the perspective of provenance and reproducibility, CF and ACDD are **limited**. CF focuses on variable semantics and units but does not standardize how to document specific processing pipelines or software environments; its cell_methods attribute can capture some aggregation semantics, but not full workflows.[14] ACDD includes a “history” attribute where producers often record processing steps as free text, along with “source” information, but these fields are not formally structured and are not consistently populated.[15] Fowler et al. emphasize that reproducible computational research demands rich, structured metadata about computational environments, which CF and ACDD do not aim to provide.[1] As a result, even when exposure datasets are CF/ACDD-compliant, meta-analysts may lack detailed information on how exposures were computed, particularly when complex modeling or geospatial linkage is involved.[1][14][15]

CF metadata conventions are documented at https://cfconventions.org, with NASA Earthdata providing a community standard nomination at https://www.earthdata.nasa.gov/about/esdis/esco/standards-practices/climate-forecast-metadata-conventions.[14] ACDD is described on the ESIP wiki at https://wiki.esipfed.org/Attribute_Convention_for_Data_Discovery_1-3.[15] Both are **general Earth-science standards**, not environmental-health-specific. They primarily describe dataset-level and variable-level metadata for gridded environmental datasets, with a strong orientation toward discovery and interpretation rather than instance-level exposure assignments. Provenance support is minimal and mostly free-text, so they cannot, by themselves, support instance-level reproducibility of exposure values, although they are essential foundations for any exposure pipeline built on NetCDF data.[14][15]

### SpatioTemporal Asset Catalogs (STAC)

SpatioTemporal Asset Catalogs (STAC) are a specification for describing and cataloging geospatial assets—imagery, raster grids, vector features—as spatiotemporal items within interoperable catalogs.[7] The STAC specification defines JSON-based schemas for Items, Collections, and Catalogs, with core fields for identifiers, geometry, datetime, properties, and links, and extensions for specific domains such as EO (Earth observation) and processing levels.[7] STAC has gained significant traction as a way to publish large Earth-observation datasets on the web, enabling standardized discovery and access via APIs.[7] The specification is explicitly designed as a “common language to describe geospatial information” and a standardized way to expose collections of spatiotemporal data.[7]

In environmental-exposure contexts, STAC can serve as a cataloging layer for both raw environmental data and precomputed exposure surfaces. For example, a STAC Item might represent a daily PM\(_{2.5}\) raster covering a region, with properties describing sensor type, processing level, CF-compliant variable names, and links to provenance information.[7][14] Exposure surfaces generated by aggregating such rasters to census tracts or other population units could also be published as STAC Items, making them discoverable and accessible via standardized geospatial APIs.[7] STAC’s extension mechanism allows encoding domain-specific metadata, such as processing steps or quality flags, although there is currently no established STAC extension specifically for environmental exposures (unverified). Nonetheless, STAC presents a flexible substrate on which exposure-focused profiles could be built.

From a provenance standpoint, STAC’s core specification is modest. It includes fields such as “processing:level” in some extensions and allows links to external provenance documents, but does not natively define a complete process model.[7] Provenance can be expressed as properties or via linked PROV documents or RO-Crates, but this depends on community conventions.[7][8][10] As with DCAT and schema.org, STAC is fundamentally a **cataloging standard**, prioritizing discoverability and access over detailed instance-level derivation metadata. Without additional layers, STAC alone cannot guarantee reproducible re-computation of exposure values; it can only indicate where exposure datasets exist and provide some high-level processing metadata.[7] Furthermore, the STAC ecosystem is still evolving quickly, and stability of extensions may be a concern for long-term archival of exposure metadata (unverified).

The canonical STAC specification is available at https://stacspec.org.[7] STAC is a **general geospatial standard** whose primary purpose is dataset-level discovery and access. It can model collections and items that represent environmental datasets or exposure surfaces, but it does not directly represent exposure assignments to individuals or analytic pipelines. Provenance support is limited to properties and links, and instance-level reproducibility requires integration with additional standards such as W3C PROV or RO-Crate.[7][8][10]

## Cross-Domain Data Catalog, Dataset, and Packaging Standards

### DCAT: Data Catalog Vocabulary

The W3C Data Catalog Vocabulary (DCAT) is an RDF vocabulary designed to facilitate interoperability between data catalogs published on the web.[4] DCAT defines classes such as dcat:Catalog, dcat:Dataset, and dcat:Distribution, with properties for titles, descriptions, themes, keywords, spatial and temporal coverage, and access URLs.[4] DCAT was originally developed in the context of government open-data portals such as data.gov and data.gov.uk, but the standard is domain-agnostic and widely used in other contexts.[4] The DCAT 3 specification extends earlier versions with improved alignment to schema.org Dataset and additional provisions for data services and distributions.[4]

For environmental-exposure data, DCAT plays a central role in making datasets **findable** across institutional catalogs. Exposure datasets—whether raw environmental rasters, exposure surfaces, or linked exposure-health datasets—can be described as dcat:Dataset entities with metadata about spatial extent, temporal coverage, themes (e.g., environment, health), and distributions (e.g., NetCDF, GeoTIFF, CSV files).[4] DCAT’s alignment with ISO 19115 and other geospatial metadata standards allows integration with geospatial catalogs, while its RDF basis supports semantic queries across multiple catalogs.[4][11] DCAT does not prescribe domain-specific fields for exposures but can be combined with other vocabularies to declare relationships between datasets (e.g., “this exposure dataset is derived from that environmental dataset”).[4]

From a provenance and reproducibility perspective, DCAT’s scope is intentionally limited. It focuses on catalog-level descriptions of datasets and distributions, not on modeling analytic workflows or instance-level derivations.[4] Provenance can be represented by linking dcat:Dataset to PROV entities or RO-Crates, but DCAT itself includes only high-level properties such as dct:source and dct:provenanceStatement.[4][10][8] Fowler et al. emphasize that catalog-level metadata is necessary but insufficient for reproducible research; DCAT exemplifies this tension.[1] It excels at discoverability but provides little structure for documenting how exposure values were computed, so relying solely on DCAT for metadata would leave meta-analysts with significant gaps.[1][4]

The canonical DCAT 3 specification is at https://www.w3.org/TR/vocab-dcat-3/.[4] DCAT is a **general-purpose catalog standard** under W3C governance, mature and widely implemented. Its primary unit of description is the dataset (dcat:Dataset), and its primary purpose is to make datasets findable and interoperable across catalogs. It carries minimal provenance metadata and does not support instance-level reproducibility by itself, placing it firmly in the **general standards** category rather than as a pipeline sidecar.[4]

### schema.org Dataset and Science-on-schema.org guidance

schema.org is a collaborative initiative to develop structured vocabularies for describing web resources, used extensively in search-engine optimization and rich snippets.[5] The schema.org Dataset type represents “a body of structured information describing some topic(s) of interest” and includes properties such as name, description, creator, spatialCoverage, temporalCoverage, and distribution URLs.[5] Recognizing its potential for scientific data, the Earth Science Information Partners (ESIP) and collaborators have developed “science-on-schema.org” guidance, endorsed by ESIP, to show how scientific datasets and related resources should be described using schema.org terms.[6] The guidance aims to make earth and environmental science data more easily discoverable via general web search and to connect institutional catalogs with broader web ecosystems.[6]

For environmental-exposure data, schema.org Dataset and science-on-schema.org patterns offer a light-weight, web-native way to describe exposure datasets on landing pages. A dataset describing neighborhood-level heat exposure, for example, could be marked up with schema.org Dataset properties indicating its spatialCoverage, measurementTechnique, variableMeasured (possibly using CF standard names), and distribution URLs.[5][6][14] The science-on-schema.org guidance suggests how to align schema.org Dataset with DCAT and DataCite, enabling consistent representation across different metadata ecosystems.[6][4][9] This bridging role is particularly important when exposure datasets are published outside traditional scientific repositories, such as on project websites or institutional portals without ISO 19115 support.

However, schema.org’s **semantic depth** is limited relative to domain-specific metadata standards. Its properties are intentionally generic, and while microdata or JSON‑LD markup can express basic dataset descriptors, they do not encode detailed provenance or analytic pipeline information.[5][6] The science-on-schema.org guidance offers conventions for linking to more expressive provenance representations (e.g., PROV documents, RO-Crates), but these are not enforced by schema.org itself.[6][8][10] Moreover, adoption of science-on-schema.org remains uneven across scientific data providers (unverified), and many exposure datasets published on the web lack any structured schema.org metadata, limiting their visibility to search engines.[6] This underscores the broader challenge noted by Fowler et al.: metadata standards can only improve reproducibility and discoverability when actually implemented.[1]

The canonical schema.org Dataset definition is at https://schema.org/Dataset.[5] The science-on-schema.org guidance endorsed by ESIP is available at https://www.esipfed.org/esip-endorses-guidance-for-science-on-schema.org-metadata.[6] These are **general standards** under the schema.org collaboration and ESIP community, focused on dataset-level web markup. Their primary purpose is dataset discovery via web search, and they carry minimal provenance information unless combined with other standards. They do not support instance-level reproducibility on their own but can serve as a gateway to richer metadata.[5][6]

### DataCite Metadata Schema

The DataCite Metadata Schema is designed to support accurate and consistent identification of research resources for citation and discovery.[9] The current version, described in DataCite Metadata Kernel v4.0, defines core properties such as identifier (DOI), creators, titles, publisher, publication year, resource type, and optional elements like subjects, contributors, dates, language, alternate identifiers, related identifiers, sizes, formats, version, rights, descriptions, geo-location, and funding references.[9] DataCite is widely used by repositories to mint DOIs for datasets, software, and other research outputs, making DataCite metadata central to the scholarly data ecosystem.[9]

In environmental-exposure contexts, DataCite metadata primarily supports **citation and high-level discovery** of exposure datasets. When a repository assigns a DOI to a dataset containing exposure values, DataCite metadata can capture its basic descriptive information and relationships to other resources (e.g., “is derived from” an underlying climate dataset).[9] Geo-location elements can describe the spatial footprint, while related identifiers can link to publications or other datasets that use the exposures.[9] However, DataCite metadata is intentionally generic and minimal; it does not attempt to encode variable-level semantics, analytic pipelines, or instance-level exposure assignments.[9] Its strength lies in facilitating persistent identification and attribution, not in describing methodological detail.

From a skeptical perspective, reliance on DataCite metadata as the primary descriptor of environmental-exposure datasets would be problematic. Fowler et al. note that bibliographic metadata alone cannot ensure reproducibility or even correct reuse, because methodological detail is absent.[1] DataCite metadata may make exposure datasets easier to cite and track, but without complementary domain-specific and provenance standards, meta-analysts will still lack critical information about how exposures were calculated.[1][9] There is also a risk that DataCite’s generic “descriptions” element becomes a dumping ground for unstructured narrative about methods, which, while better than nothing, is difficult to process systematically.[9] This underscores the need to treat DataCite as a component of a broader metadata stack rather than as a standalone solution.

The canonical DataCite metadata schema documentation is available at https://schema.datacite.org/meta/kernel-4.0/doc/DataCite-MetadataKernel_v4.0.pdf.[9] DataCite is a **general scholarly metadata standard**, governed by DataCite, with dataset-level units of description and a primary purpose of citation and discovery. It includes minimal provenance elements through related identifiers and descriptions but does not support instance-level reproducibility of exposure values. It is clearly a **general standard**, not a pipeline sidecar.[9]

### Frictionless Data Package

The Frictionless Data initiative defines a “Data Package” as a simple container format for describing a coherent collection of data in a single package.[16] A Data Package typically includes a descriptor file (datapackage.json) that lists one or more resources (files), along with metadata about their structure (fields, types), sources, and additional descriptive information.[16] The specification aims to provide a convenient basis for delivery, installation, and management of datasets, with a focus on tabular data but extensible to other formats.[16] Frictionless Data tools can validate packages, enforce schemas, and promote lightweight, machine-readable metadata for data collections.[16]

For environmental-exposure data, Data Packages offer a way to bundle exposure tables and environmental datasets with structural metadata, such as field names, types, and CSV dialect. An exposure dataset with rows representing individuals or addresses and columns for exposure values, time windows, covariates, and identifiers could be encapsulated in a Data Package with a schema describing each field.[16][18][20] This supports data validation and reduces ambiguity about formats, an important aspect of reproducibility. Additional metadata fields (e.g., title, description, sources) can describe dataset origins, though the core specification remains relatively minimal.[16] Data Packages thus sit at the interface between **pipeline sidecars** and **general standards**, as they are widely applicable but often used at project or workflow scope.

From a provenance perspective, Frictionless Data Package is limited. The specification does not natively define a full process model; it focuses on descriptive and structural metadata for static data resources.[16] Provenance can be included as custom properties or through links to external documents, but this is not standardized.[16] Fowler et al.’s emphasis on documenting the computing environment and analytic steps suggests that Data Packages, while useful, must be complemented by provenance frameworks like W3C PROV or RO-Crate to support reproducible exposure workflows.[1][8][10] There is also a question of adoption: while Frictionless Data has gained traction in some data science communities, its uptake in geospatial and clinical exposure pipelines appears limited (unverified), reducing its impact on exposure metadata standardization.

The canonical Data Package specification is at https://specs.frictionlessdata.io/data-package/.[16] Frictionless Data Package is a **general structured-metadata format** for describing collections of data resources, with resource-level units and a primary purpose of data delivery, validation, and management. It carries basic dataset-level metadata and detailed structural metadata but minimal provenance. It does not inherently support instance-level reproducibility of exposure values, though it can contain exposure tables and be extended with provenance fields. It is best considered a **general standard**, albeit one frequently implemented at pipeline or project scope, blurring the line with sidecars.[16]

### RO-Crate: Research Object packaging

RO-Crate is a community-driven specification for packaging research objects—data, code, workflows, and related artifacts—into a machine-readable, self-describing bundle.[8] An RO-Crate provides an integrated view of a research project, recording methods, data, outputs, and outcomes, with metadata expressed in JSON-LD and based on schema.org terms.[8] The goal is to make research objects portable, inspectable, and reusable, capturing both basic descriptive metadata and structured links between entities, including provenance relationships.[8] RO-Crate can embed or reference W3C PROV, workflow descriptions, and additional domain-specific metadata, serving as a container for rich metadata ecosystems.[8][10]

In environmental-exposure research, RO-Crate is particularly promising as a means to package entire exposure pipelines, from raw environmental datasets through geocoding scripts to final exposure tables and analysis outputs. A well-constructed RO-Crate could include exposure datasets (as DataCite-like resources), code repositories, container images, workflow definitions (e.g., CWL), and PROV records describing how exposure values were computed, all linked via JSON-LD metadata.[8][1][10] This aligns closely with Fowler et al.’s analytic stack concept, where metadata spanning input data, tools, pipelines, and outputs is required for reproducible computational research.[1] RO-Crate explicitly targets such holistic provenance, making it suitable for instance-level exposure metadata when implemented fully.

Nevertheless, RO-Crate’s power comes with complexity. Constructing comprehensive RO-Crates demands careful modeling and often specialized tooling, and adoption remains uneven across disciplines (unverified). Some projects use RO-Crate primarily for data packaging, without fully leveraging its provenance capabilities, leading to crates that capture existence of artifacts but not the detailed process that links them.[8] In exposure science, there are few public examples of RO-Crate-based pipelines, suggesting that while the standard is conceptually aligned with reproducibility needs, social and technical barriers hinder its widespread use (unverified). Moreover, RO-Crate is a **general research-object standard**, not tailored to environmental exposures, so domain-specific semantics must be added via external vocabularies and schemas.[8]

The canonical RO-Crate documentation is at https://www.researchobject.org/ro-crate/about_ro_crate.[8] RO-Crate is a **general metadata and packaging standard**, governed by the Research Object community, with research-object-level units and a primary purpose of comprehensive packaging and provenance. It supports detailed provenance and can enable instance-level reproducibility when used to describe workflows and exposure assignments; thus, it sits firmly in the **general standards** category, with strong provenance support compared to most other standards discussed.[8][10]

### Croissant: high-level format for machine learning datasets

Croissant is a high-level format for machine learning datasets that combines metadata, resource file descriptions, data structure, and default ML semantics into a single file.[17] Developed under MLCommons, Croissant builds on schema.org and its Dataset vocabulary to represent datasets on the web and make them searchable.[17] A Croissant file encapsulates metadata about the dataset, resources (files or data sources), its internal structure (tables, fields), and machine learning semantics such as typical input and target variables.[17] The format is intended to work with existing datasets, making them easier to find, use, and support with tools, and incorporates concepts related to responsible ML.[17]

For environmental-exposure data, Croissant has potential when exposure datasets are used in machine-learning models—for example, predicting health outcomes from exposures or inferring exposures from environmental signals. A Croissant description could model an exposure dataset’s fields, their roles (features, labels), and relationships to other datasets, facilitating automated ingestion into ML pipelines.[17] It offers more semantics about ML usage than Frictionless Data Package while leveraging schema.org Dataset for discoverability.[17][5] However, Croissant is not designed to encode detailed provenance of how exposure values were computed; its focus is on dataset structure and ML semantics.[17] Thus, while it can make exposure datasets more ML-ready, it does not address the deeper reproducibility needs identified by Fowler et al.[1]

Croissant is relatively new, and its adoption in environmental and health domains is in early stages (unverified). MLCommons’ governance and emphasis on ML may bias the format toward computer-vision and NLP datasets (unverified), and specific patterns for environmental exposures remain to be developed. As such, Croissant is best seen as a complementary standard: useful for structuring exposure datasets for ML applications but not a replacement for geospatial or provenance standards needed for exposure science.

The canonical Croissant specification and repository are at https://github.com/mlcommons/croissant.[17] Croissant is a **general ML dataset format**, governed by MLCommons, with dataset-level units and a primary purpose of making ML datasets findable and usable. It carries structural and ML semantics but minimal provenance, and does not support instance-level reproducibility of exposure values on its own. It belongs in the **general standards** class, with a specific ML orientation.[17]

## Clinical, Health, and Exposome-Oriented Metadata Standards

### OMOP CDM GIS and external_exposure extensions

The Observational Medical Outcomes Partnership (OMOP) Common Data Model (CDM), maintained by the OHDSI community, is a widely used standard for structuring observational health data across institutions.[18] Recognizing the growing need to contextualize healthcare data with environmental and societal factors, OHDSI has developed the OMOP GIS Vocabulary Package and an external_exposure table schema that extend the CDM to incorporate spatial, environmental, behavioral, socioeconomic, phenotypic, and toxin-related determinants of health into standardized data structures.[18] The GIS vocabulary defines concepts for environmental exposures, spatial identifiers, and measurement units, supporting linkage of patient locations to external environmental datasets.[18]

The OMOP GIS and external_exposure extensions are directly relevant to environmental-exposure metadata. The external_exposure table is designed to store exposure measurements or assignments for each patient, linked via spatial identifiers such as GEOID (census tract), ZIP codes, or latitude/longitude, and mapped to exposure_concept_id entries in the GIS vocabulary.[18] Guidance from the OHDSI GIS working group describes validation objectives, acquisition and preprocessing of authoritative GIS datasets (e.g., EPA AirNow, WHO noise pollution index), normalization of spatial granularity, crosswalking of coordinates to GEOID using reverse geocoding, and hierarchical matching strategies between GIS data and OMOP locations.[18] Measurement units are mapped to unit_concept_id via OHDSI Athena, and mapping tables connect source environmental variables to standardized concept IDs.[18] This architecture supports the representation of environmental exposures in a clinically oriented data model, enabling analysis of health outcomes in relation to exposures.

From a provenance perspective, the OMOP external_exposure schema provides some structure but leaves much to be documented externally. The schema ensures that each patient has a geospatial identifier that can be linked to GIS datasets, and that exposure_start_date, value_as_number, unit_concept_id, and exposure_concept_id are recorded.[18] However, details of the pipeline used to derive exposures—such as the specific GIS dataset version, model parameters, or spatial buffers—are not explicitly modeled in the core schema.[18] These must be captured either in separate tables, via concepts in the GIS vocabulary, or in external documentation. Consequently, while OMOP external_exposure supports **instance-level storage** of exposure values and standardized concept mapping, it does not fully encode analytic provenance, limiting reproducibility of exposure assignment unless additional metadata is provided.[18]

The canonical documentation for the OMOP GIS vocabulary and external_exposure guidance is at https://ohdsi.github.io/GIS/vocabulary.html.[18] These extensions are **domain-specific standards** within the clinical/health domain, governed by OHDSI. Their primary unit of description is the patient-level exposure record, and their purpose is to support environmental exposure linkage and analysis within OMOP-based health databases. They provide strong support for standardized terminology and some provenance via concept mapping, but detailed process metadata must be recorded elsewhere. As part of the OMOP CDM, they are **general standards** within the observational health-data community, not pipeline sidecars.[18]

### FHIR environmental profiles and FHIR PIT

HL7 FHIR (Fast Healthcare Interoperability Resources) is a widely adopted standard for exchanging healthcare information electronically, with resources representing clinical concepts such as patients, observations, medications, and procedures.[19] Environmental exposures can be represented in FHIR using Observation resources and domain-specific profiles, though no single global profile for environmental exposures has yet achieved consensus (unverified). The NIEHS CHORDS Training and Use Cases Toolkit describes FHIR PIT (FHIR-based Patient-Exposure Integration Tool), an open-source tool that links electronic health record data in FHIR format with environmental exposure data derived from public sources.[19] FHIR PIT operates by ingesting FHIR EHR data, geocoding patient addresses, and joining them to environmental exposure datasets, producing integrated datasets suitable for exposure-health analyses.[19]

FHIR PIT’s metadata practices exemplify **pipeline sidecar** behavior. The tool links patient records to exposures via geocoding and spatial-temporal joins, and its documentation describes inputs (EHR FHIR bundles, exposure datasets), processing steps (geocoding, linkage), and outputs (patient-level exposure assignments).[19] However, the structured metadata about these processes is primarily embedded in code, configuration files, and project-specific documentation rather than in a general standard. FHIR itself provides a rich schema for clinical observations, and exposure values could be represented as FHIR Observation resources with codes indicating environmental exposures, but the specifics of how exposures are computed—geocoder used, environmental dataset version, aggregation rules—are not mandated by FHIR.[19] Thus, FHIR PIT yields integrated datasets with patient-level exposures but without standardized provenance metadata capturing the full pipeline.

From a reproducibility perspective, this is problematic. Fowler et al. emphasize that reproducible computational research requires extensive metadata about the computing environment and analytic stack.[1] In FHIR PIT, reproducing exposure assignments would require access to the same tool, configuration, environmental datasets, and geocoding procedures; yet these are not fully described in a machine-readable metadata standard.[19] The tool’s documentation provides guidance, but there is no FHIR-native representation of exposure pipelines. As a result, FHIR PIT must be classified as a **pipeline sidecar** rather than a general metadata standard, and its outputs, while valuable, are difficult to integrate into a broader exposure metadata registry without additional modeling.

The canonical description of FHIR PIT is in the CHORDS Training and Use Cases Toolkit at https://niehs.github.io/PCOR_bookdown_tools/chapter-fhir-pit.html.[19] FHIR itself is documented at https://www.hl7.org/fhir, but environmental exposure modeling in FHIR is an emerging practice with no single authoritative profile (unverified). FHIR PIT illustrates the potential for linking clinical and environmental data using FHIR, but also the limitations of current metadata practices around exposure pipelines, underscoring the need for more structured provenance in health-exposure integration workflows.[19][1]

### Other clinical and exposome initiatives (brief overview)

Beyond OMOP and FHIR-based tools, a variety of exposome and environmental health projects have developed local schemas for exposure data, often using project-specific relational models or JSON schemas (unverified). Many of these schemas encode exposure variables, measurement methods, and linkage strategies but lack formal standardization, making them difficult to generalize.[1] CODATA’s “Essential Variables” framework, for example, identifies key environmental variables relevant to climate and societal impacts, which could serve as a basis for exposure-variable semantics, but does not itself define instance-level exposure schemas (unverified). Similarly, some cohort studies employ bespoke exposure tables with rich metadata describing measurement protocols, yet these schemas remain confined to single projects.[1]

These efforts highlight a broader problem: while clinical and exposome communities recognize the importance of environmental exposures, they lack shared metadata standards comparable to CF or ISO 19156 in Earth science. OMOP’s external_exposure and FHIR-based profiles represent promising steps, but their adoption is still limited and their provenance semantics incomplete.[18][19] Without stronger cross-project standardization, exposure meta-analysis across cohorts will continue to face challenges in harmonizing exposure definitions, units, and derivation methods, even if basic dataset-level metadata is available.[1] This exposes a critical gap for schema engineers designing an environmental-exposure metadata registry: standards exist in geospatial and climate domains, but clinical exposure metadata remains underdeveloped and highly heterogeneous (unverified).

## Provenance Standards and Pipeline Sidecars

### W3C PROV and geospatial provenance applications

The W3C PROV family of specifications defines a generic framework for representing provenance information—what entities, activities, and agents are involved in producing data, and how they are related.[10] PROV-DM (data model) introduces core concepts such as Entity, Activity, and Agent, and relations like used, wasGeneratedBy, and wasDerivedFrom.[10] These primitives can express complex workflow provenance across domains. A paper published in Computers & Geosciences presents an application of W3C PROV for representing geospatial data provenance at dataset, feature, and attribute levels, demonstrating how PROV can capture processing steps and lineage in spatial contexts.[10] The authors illustrate how probabilistic data fusion for environmental data can be documented using PROV constructs.[10]

For environmental-exposure metadata, W3C PROV is arguably the most powerful general framework for representing **analytic provenance**. Exposure pipelines can be modeled as PROV Activities that use environmental datasets (Entities) and generate exposure tables (Entities), with Agents representing software tools or investigators.[10][1] Individual exposure assignments could be represented at the attribute level, linking each value to the workflow step that produced it.[10] PROV’s flexibility allows encoding geocoding, spatial joins, temporal aggregation, and statistical modeling as separate Activities, enabling fine-grained reproducibility when combined with appropriate tooling.[10] Moreover, PROV can be integrated with RO-Crate and DCAT, serving as the provenance backbone of research-object packages and data catalogs.[8][4][10]

However, PROV’s generic nature is both strength and weakness. It does not prescribe domain-specific vocabularies or constraints on how environmental exposures should be modeled; this must be designed by communities.[10] As the geospatial PROV paper notes, applying PROV effectively requires careful modeling of geospatial constructs and workflows, and without such modeling, PROV graphs can become unwieldy and inconsistent.[10] Adoption in environmental-exposure pipelines appears limited (unverified), with most practitioners relying on informal documentation or code-based provenance. Fowler et al. highlight that comprehensive provenance documentation is still rare in computational research, and PROV’s potential remains underexploited.[1][10] Furthermore, PROV representations can be large and complex, raising concerns about storage, query performance, and usability for meta-analysis (unverified).

The canonical PROV specifications are at https://www.w3.org/TR/prov-overview/, and the geospatial PROV application is described at https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0198971517300558.[10] W3C PROV is a **general provenance standard**, domain-neutral and focusing on entities, activities, and agents. Its primary units of description are processes and their outputs, with the purpose of supporting provenance and reproducibility. It provides strong support for instance-level provenance when used systematically, but environmental-exposure-specific profiles remain to be developed. It is firmly a **general standard**, not a pipeline sidecar.[10]

### Pipeline sidecars: DeGAUSS geocoding and geomarker outputs

DeGAUSS (Decentralized Geomarker Assessment for Multi-Site Studies) is a containerized geocoding and geomarker toolkit that operates via Docker images, allowing research sites to geocode addresses and compute geomarkers locally without sharing raw address data.[20] The DeGAUSS geocoder image processes a CSV file containing an “address” column and produces an output CSV with appended columns for matched address components, geocoding scores and precision, latitude, longitude, and categorical geocoding results.[20] Additional DeGAUSS images compute geomarkers such as proximity to major roadways and greenspace, appending new columns to the dataset while preserving original data.[20] Once geomarkers are added, addresses can be removed to create a geomarker dataset without geographic protected health information (PHI), enabling safer data sharing.[20]

From a metadata perspective, DeGAUSS embodies **pipeline sidecar** behavior. Each run generates output files with structured columns that implicitly encode geocoding and geomarker metadata: lat/lon fields, geocoding scores, buffer distances for road proximity, greenspace indexes, etc.[20] However, these columns are primarily designed for analytic use rather than as explicit metadata standards. The specifics of the geocoder version, the underlying reference datasets (e.g., road networks, land cover maps), and Docker image identifiers are documented in usage instructions and log files, not in a general metadata schema.[20] The DeGAUSS commands demonstrate this: Docker runs are invoked with image names and CSV file paths, and the resulting files contain process-related information that is interpretable but not standardized across tools.[20] This makes DeGAUSS outputs rich in instance-level exposure information but idiosyncratic to the toolkit.

The DeGAUSS documentation emphasizes operational steps—starting with an address CSV, running the geocoder image, and then applying geomarker images sequentially.[20] It does not, however, propose a reusable metadata standard for environmental exposures; it focuses on practical workflow for studies needing geocoding and geomarkers while preserving PHI privacy.[20] From Fowler et al.’s perspective, DeGAUSS provides some of the metadata needed for reproducible geocoding (e.g., scores and precision) but lacks formalized provenance modeling of the full analytic stack, including Docker environment details.[1][20] As a result, while DeGAUSS sidecars contain many of the ingredients for instance-level reproducibility of exposure assignments (geocoding results and geomarkers), they do so in a toolkit-specific, non-standardized format.[20]

The canonical DeGAUSS documentation for using the geocoder and geomarker images is at https://degauss.org/using_degauss.html.[20] DeGAUSS outputs are **pipeline-specific sidecars**, not general metadata standards, with row-level exposure and geocoding data but limited structured provenance and no cross-tool interoperability. They exemplify a common pattern in exposure science: workflows produce rich, structured outputs tailored to a project or toolkit, but without formal standardization, making them difficult to incorporate into a broader metadata registry.

### Other pipeline sidecars and project-local metadata

Beyond DeGAUSS and FHIR PIT, numerous exposure pipelines use project-local structured metadata formats such as YAML or JSON configuration files, log files, or custom sidecar tables that record processing parameters, data sources, and software versions (unverified). For example, an Amadeus/gridMET-based exposure pipeline might write a YAML file describing the gridded climate dataset used, spatial interpolation method, and temporal aggregation window, along with a log of container images and code commits (unverified). Similarly, cohort studies performing air pollution exposure modeling often maintain internal documentation of model versions, emission inventories, and calibration procedures, which may be structured within an institution but not codified in public standards (unverified). These artifacts are crucial for reproducibility within a project but are opaque to external users.

From a skeptical perspective, reliance on such **local metadata** is a major barrier to cross-project exposure meta-analysis. Fowler et al. warn that reproducibility suffers when metadata remains in ad hoc forms tied to specific computational environments.[1] Pipeline sidecars are necessary to capture the “last mile” of analytic detail but, without standardization, they cannot be reliably interpreted outside their original context. Schema engineers designing an environmental-exposure metadata registry will need to either incorporate these sidecar formats into a harmonized schema or encourage migration to general provenance standards like W3C PROV and RO-Crate, aligning local practices with global interoperability goals.[1][8][10] Absent such efforts, exposure metadata will remain siloed, and meta-analysts will face persistent challenges in understanding and reproducing exposure derivations across studies.

## Limitations, Criticisms, and Methodological Objections

### Fragmentation and underuse of provenance

Across the landscape surveyed, a recurring limitation is the **fragmentation of standards** and the underuse of provenance features even where they exist. Fowler et al. document that reproducible computational research requires metadata across the analytic stack, yet most current practices focus on data-level descriptors while neglecting tool and workflow metadata.[1] In environmental-exposure science, this manifests as reliance on geospatial and climate standards like ISO 19115, CF, and ACDD for dataset and variable semantics, while leaving exposure assignment pipelines described only in prose or code.[11][14][15] W3C PROV offers a robust provenance model, and RO-Crate provides packaging for provenance-rich research objects, but their adoption in exposure workflows is limited (unverified).[8][10] Even OMOP external_exposure and GIS vocabulary, which structurally store exposure values, do not require detailed process metadata for how exposures were computed.[18]

Critics might argue that this situation reflects **misaligned incentives** rather than technical impossibility. Metadata and provenance documentation rarely contribute directly to typical academic metrics such as publications and grants, while requiring significant effort. As Fowler et al. note, metadata is often treated as an afterthought rather than a first-class research output.[1] Environmental-exposure projects, particularly large cohort studies, face pressures to produce timely exposure-response analyses, reducing appetite for extensive metadata work (unverified). This leads to minimal metadata implementations emphasizing discoverability over reproducibility, a phenomenon observed across domains but especially problematic for exposures, where methodological differences can strongly influence health-effect estimates.[1] Without structural changes in funding and evaluation, uptake of provenance-rich standards like PROV and RO-Crate may remain limited.

### Complexity and usability of geospatial and observations standards

ISO 19115 and ISO 19156/OGC OM have been criticized for their **complexity and steep learning curve**, especially for small teams or cross-disciplinary projects (unverified).[3][11][13] Implementing full ISO 19115 metadata requires understanding numerous classes and properties, and tools like ArcGIS Pro need careful configuration to produce ISO 19139-compliant metadata.[12] ISO 19156’s conceptual richness can be intimidating, and the withdrawal of the ISO version adds uncertainty about long-term maintenance.[3] Fowler et al. suggest that heavy-weight standards may discourage thorough metadata documentation, as researchers opt for minimal fields or free-text descriptions.[1] In environmental-exposure projects, which often involve multidisciplinary teams with varying levels of geospatial expertise, this burden can be a significant barrier to standard adoption (unverified).

At the same time, **simpler standards** like CF and ACDD, while easier to implement, may be too limited for exposure provenance. CF’s cell_methods attribute and standard names improve variable semantics but do not capture full analytic pipelines, and ACDD’s history field is free-text.[14][15] Critics might argue that such standards encourage superficial metadata practices: setting titles and keywords but leaving critical methodological details in narrative descriptions or absent.[1] The geospatial PROV paper shows that it is feasible to represent detailed provenance for geospatial processes using PROV, but this requires deliberate modeling and tool support.[10] Without better integration between geospatial standards and provenance frameworks, exposure metadata will likely continue to fall short of supporting robust reproducibility.

### Clinical and exposome standards: coverage gaps and heterogeneity

Clinical and exposome-oriented standards such as OMOP, FHIR, and their environmental extensions exhibit **coverage gaps and heterogeneity**. OMOP external_exposure provides a structured table for exposure values, but exposure pipelines are not standardized, and different institutions may implement variable mappings and linkage strategies differently.[18] FHIR PIT illustrates that environmental exposures can be attached to FHIR-based EHRs, but exposure modeling remains in pipeline-specific code and configuration, not in FHIR’s core schema.[19] This heterogeneity complicates meta-analysis across health systems, as exposure definitions and derivation methods may differ significantly even when stored in seemingly similar structures.

From a skeptical standpoint, one might question whether current clinical standards can realistically support exposure science without substantial extensions and governance. FHIR’s design emphasizes clinical interoperability, and its resource model may not be ideal for complex environmental exposure processes (unverified). OMOP’s CDM was built around clinical events and measurements; environmental exposures are a later addition, and their integration may be constrained by the CDM’s relational design.[18] Fowler et al.’s emphasis on analytic stack metadata suggests that embedding environmental exposures into clinical standards must be accompanied by robust provenance frameworks, not just new tables.[1] Yet such frameworks are currently lacking, raising doubts about the long-term adequacy of existing clinical standards for exposure reproducibility.

### Pipeline sidecars: power and peril of local solutions

Pipeline sidecars like DeGAUSS outputs and project-local configuration files provide **rich instance-level detail** about exposure assignments and geocoding, often more detailed than any general standard currently deployed.[20] DeGAUSS, for instance, records geocoding scores and precision, and geomarker pipelines append buffer-based measures and greenspace metrics to each row.[20] This information is crucial for assessing exposure quality and reproducibility. However, because these formats are toolkit-specific and lack formal standardization, they are difficult to integrate across projects and tools. A meta-analyst combining DeGAUSS-derived exposures with exposures derived from different pipelines may struggle to reconcile sidecar formats and parameter semantics (unverified).

The peril of pipeline sidecars is that they **lock provenance into local practices**, making it hard to share, query, and reason about exposure pipelines at scale. Fowler et al. warn that code-only provenance, without structured metadata, hampers reproducibility.[1] Sidecars are an improvement over entirely undocumented workflows, but without harmonized schemas, they may still fall short of enabling systematic meta-analysis. Furthermore, sidecar formats are vulnerable to software obsolescence; when a pipeline is retired or its documentation lost, interpreting its sidecars becomes difficult (unverified). This argues strongly for mapping pipeline sidecar content onto general provenance standards like PROV and packaging them in research-object frameworks like RO-Crate, enabling long-term reuse beyond the lifespan of specific tools.[1][8][10]

### Conflicts of interest, funding biases, and standard-setting

Standardization efforts are often driven by specific communities and funding agencies, which can introduce **biases in scope and priorities**. ISO 19115 and ISO 19156 emerged from geospatial and survey communities, shaping their focus toward generic geographic information rather than environmental health.[3][11] CF metadata conventions were developed primarily for climate and atmospheric modeling, emphasizing gridded variables and model outputs rather than person-level exposures.[14] OMOP CDM and FHIR were driven by healthcare and clinical informatics interests, with environmental exposures added later and likely under-resourced.[18][19] DataCite, DCAT, and schema.org evolved from scholarly and web communities, optimizing for discoverability and citation over analytic reproducibility.[4][5][9] As Fowler et al. note, metadata standards often reflect the needs and incentives of their originating communities, which may not align with exposure science’s specific requirements.[1]

Funding biases further shape which standards gain traction. Large agencies like NASA, EPA, and EU bodies have invested in CF, GCMD, and geospatial metadata, while health agencies have focused more on clinical standards.[2][11][14] Exposure-specific metadata work, such as OMOP external_exposure and GIS vocabulary, is relatively young and less broadly funded.[18] RO-Crate and PROV have benefited from research-infrastructure funding but face competition from simpler practices that require less effort.[8][10][1] This ecosystem makes it difficult for exposure-specific metadata standards to emerge and gain broad adoption, as they must bridge multiple domains and compete for attention with well-established, domain-focused standards. Recognizing these structural biases is important for schema engineers, who must plan for multi-stakeholder governance and sustained investment if an environmental-exposure metadata registry is to succeed.

## Conclusion: Implications for an Environmental-Exposure Metadata Registry

The survey presented in this report demonstrates that **no single metadata standard currently satisfies all the requirements for reusable, reproducible, and meta-analysable environmental-exposure data**. Geospatial and Earth-science standards like ISO 19115, ISO 19156/OGC OM, CF, and ACDD provide essential foundations for describing environmental datasets and variables, but they either focus on dataset-level descriptors or conceptual observation models without widespread implementation in exposure pipelines.[3][11][14][15] Catalog and dataset standards like DCAT, schema.org Dataset, and DataCite excel at discoverability and citation but carry minimal provenance.[4][5][9] Clinical standards and extensions such as OMOP external_exposure and FHIR-based tools (FHIR PIT) support storing exposures alongside health data but leave derivation pipelines largely undocumented.[18][19] Provenance frameworks like W3C PROV and packaging standards like RO-Crate offer the most promising route to instance-level reproducibility, yet their adoption in environmental-exposure workflows is limited and demands significant modeling effort.[8][10][1] Pipeline sidecars, exemplified by DeGAUSS and local configuration files, often contain rich instance-level details but are not standardized, hindering cross-project integration.[20]

For schema engineers building an environmental-exposure metadata registry, several implications follow. First, the registry must be **multi-layered**, combining dataset-level and variable-level metadata from geospatial and climate standards (ISO 19115, CF, ACDD, STAC), instance-level observation modeling from ISO 19156/OGC OM or ODM2, and provenance modeling from W3C PROV and RO-Crate.[3][11][14][15][7][8][10][16] Second, it must bridge to clinical standards, representing exposures in structures compatible with OMOP and FHIR while supplementing them with detailed provenance to support reproducibility and meta-analysis.[18][19] Third, the registry should incorporate pipeline sidecars by defining harmonized schemas for common exposure operations (geocoding, spatial joins, temporal aggregation, model-based estimation), enabling existing sidecar formats to be mapped into standardized metadata without discarding their detail.[20] Fourth, the registry must rely on controlled vocabularies and ontologies (CF standard names, QUDT, EnvO, SWEET) for semantic consistency while recognizing that these vocabularies are **supporting infrastructure** rather than primary metadata standards.[14][16]

A skeptical stance suggests that success will require confronting not only technical but also social and institutional challenges. Standards proliferation has already led to fragmentation; adding yet another registry risks exacerbating this unless it is clearly aligned with and embedded in existing ecosystems such as DCAT-based catalogs, RO-Crate, and OMOP/FHIR infrastructures.[4][8][18][19] Funding and incentives must be structured so that detailed provenance documentation is valued rather than treated as overhead.[1] Furthermore, the registry should be designed to cope with heterogeneity and partial metadata: it must allow incomplete records, flag “unverified” provenance where details are missing, and provide mechanisms for iterative enrichment as more information becomes available.[1][10] Given the current state of practice, expecting perfect metadata for all exposure datasets is unrealistic; instead, the registry should prioritize critical elements that most directly affect exposure estimates and meta-analytic comparability, such as environmental dataset versions, spatial aggregation methods, temporal windows, geocoding strategies, and key model parameters.[18][20]

In conclusion, environmental-exposure metadata standardization sits at the intersection of geospatial, climate, clinical, and data-science communities, each with its own standards and incentives. The landscape is rich but fragmented, and provenance is systematically underrepresented. An effective environmental-exposure metadata registry will need to **integrate and extend existing general standards** rather than attempt to replace them, while systematically capturing pipeline-specific derivations that today live only in sidecars and local documentation.[1][3][8][10][18][19][20] It must embrace complexity where necessary—particularly in provenance modeling—while providing practical profiles and tooling that lower the barrier to adoption. Only through such an integrated, provenance-aware approach can environmental-exposure data become truly FAIR in the sense required for reproducible, reliable meta-analysis of environmental impacts on human health.
