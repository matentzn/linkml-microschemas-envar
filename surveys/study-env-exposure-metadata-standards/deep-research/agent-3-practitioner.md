## Introduction

### Environmental-exposure data and the importance of metadata

Environmental-exposure data can be defined as quantitative descriptions of the contact of humans or populations with environmental factors such as temperature, air pollutants, wildfire smoke, noise, or chemicals, measured or modeled over space and time and often linked to health outcomes or behaviors.[3][4] Exposure data may originate from direct monitoring (e.g., personal air quality sensors), environmental observations (e.g., fixed-site monitors, satellite-derived products), or model-based estimations that combine geocoded residence information with gridded fields such as air pollutant concentrations or heat indices.[4][5] In large-scale epidemiological studies, exposures are typically computed by applying geospatial and temporal overlays between individual-level data (addresses, activity patterns) and environmental datasets (e.g., atmospheric models or climate reanalyses), and the reproducibility of these computed values depends critically on how well both the input datasets and the computation processes are described.[4][15] Without standardized, structured metadata capturing the sources, processing steps, assumptions, and uncertainties of exposure data, downstream reuse—whether by another research group, a regulatory body, or a meta-analyst pooling multi-cohort results—becomes fragile or impossible, undermining the FAIR principles of findability, accessibility, interoperability, and reusability.[6][11][17] Consequently, environmental-exposure science must draw on metadata standards from multiple communities, including geospatial, earth observation, clinical informatics, and provenance modeling, and it must address both **dataset-level metadata** (e.g., describing an air quality model output file) and **instance-level metadata** (e.g., describing how a specific subject’s exposure value was derived from multiple sources and computations).

### Scope and classification of metadata standards and formats

This study focuses on standards, specifications, schemas, and structured formats whose purpose is to record **metadata about environmental-exposure datasets or values**, such that they can be found, understood, reproduced, and pooled across studies. The scope deliberately excludes unstructured narrative descriptions and plain-text documentation, and instead centers on machine-readable or at least structured representations. Within that scope, the analysis distinguishes two main classes. The first class comprises **general standards**, which are designed to be reusable and interoperable across data producers, institutions, and domains: examples include ISO 19115/19139 for geographic metadata, ISO 19156 for observations and measurements, DCAT for web data catalogs, schema.org / science-on-schema.org for embedding dataset metadata in web pages, STAC for spatio-temporal asset catalogs, RO-Crate and Croissant for packaging research and ML-ready datasets, W3C PROV for provenance, DataCite metadata for DOIs, Frictionless Data Package for tabular data, ACDD/CF conventions for netCDF datasets, and health data standards like OMOP CDM and FHIR environmental profiles.[2][5][6][7][8][9][10][11][12][13][15][16][17][18][20] These standards are usually stewarded by formal bodies (ISO, W3C, OHDSI, research communities) and have documentation, governance processes, and sometimes multiple versions or profiles.

The second class comprises what can be called **pipeline sidecars**: structured but pipeline-specific metadata formats that arise as part of particular exposure data-processing workflows and are not intended as general interoperability standards. Examples include JSON or CSV files produced by geocoding pipelines (such as DeGAUSS) that capture the mapping between input addresses and geospatial coordinates; manifest files documenting downloads from environmental data services (such as gridMET or Amadeus); project-local YAML files describing model configurations; or intermediate tables linking EHR patient identifiers to environmental grid cells.[4][9] These formats may be highly informative for reproducibility within a project, but they are often undocumented beyond local conventions, lack stable specifications, and are seldom reusable across pipelines or institutions. From a schema engineer’s perspective, understanding both classes is essential: general standards provide the backbone of a registrable, interoperable metadata model, while pipeline sidecars reveal the **real-world provenance details** that must be captured if we want true instance-level reproducibility of exposure values.

## General Metadata Standards for Geospatial and Environmental Datasets

### ISO 19115 and ISO 19139: Core geospatial metadata standards

ISO 19115 is the foundational international standard for describing geographic information and services, defining an extensive schema for metadata about digital geographic data including identification, extent, quality, spatial and temporal schema, spatial reference, and distribution.[2] The Digital Curation Centre describes ISO 19115 as an “internationally-adopted schema for describing geographic information and services,” highlighting its role in supporting documentation and discovery of spatial datasets, dataset series, and geoservices in higher education and beyond (https://www.dcc.ac.uk/resources/metadata-standards/iso-19115).[2] ISO 19139 complements 19115 as the Metadata Implementation Specification, defining how ISO 19115 metadata should be encoded in XML for interoperability, and commercial tools like ArcGIS Pro provide direct support for viewing and editing full ISO 19115 and 19139 metadata documents, including exporting metadata in ISO 19139 XML format.[14] In practice, many institutions use ISO 19115/19139 as the backbone of their geospatial data catalogs, especially for environmental datasets such as climate model outputs, air quality surfaces, and heat index maps, because the standards provide fields to capture spatial extents, temporal coverage, lineage, and data quality reports, all of which are critical for interpreting exposure-related data.[2][14]

From a governance perspective, ISO 19115 and 19139 are maintained through the ISO Technical Committee on geographic information/geomatics, with formal revision cycles that have produced multiple versions and amendments over the years.[2][15] The maturity of these standards is very high: they are widely implemented by national mapping agencies, environmental data centers, and GIS vendors, and their concepts have influenced related standards and profiles such as INSPIRE metadata in Europe. The unit of description is primarily the **dataset or dataset series**, although some elements can describe services or non-dataset resources, and the schema includes structures for citing feature catalogues, data quality reports, and lineage information. In terms of primary purpose, ISO 19115/19139 are optimized for **data discovery and documentation**, including citation-like elements, but they are not inherently designed to represent computational workflows or the derivation of specific instance-level values. The lineage section does provide a place to record processing steps, input sources, and responsible parties; however, these are generally coarse-grained narrative or structured descriptions, often summarized by human authors, and they typically do not enumerate every parameter or script used to compute individual exposure values.[14]

In practitioner terms, ISO 19115/19139 are simultaneously powerful and burdensome. Environmental data practitioners frequently report that authoring full, valid ISO metadata is time-consuming, requires specialized tooling or templates, and can feel disconnected from their scientific workflow, leading to partial or minimal implementations. ArcGIS Pro documentation acknowledges that “it may take some time to complete an item's metadata” and encourages users to save changes and return later, reflecting the complexity of the schema.[14] The metadata view in ArcGIS Pro exposes numerous pages (e.g., Item Description, Resource, Extents) where required content must be supplied, such as titles, abstracts, bounding boxes, and hierarchy levels, and guidance is provided on leaving elements blank when values are unknown or inapplicable.[14] For environmental-exposure datasets, ISO 19115/19139 are valuable for describing the underlying geospatial fields used in exposure computation (e.g., meteorological surfaces, pollutant concentration grids), but they rarely reach down to the level of the **subject-level exposure values** that epidemiologists use, nor do they fully capture instance-level provenance for those values.

### ISO 19156: Observations, measurements, and samples

ISO 19156:2023, “Observations, measurements and samples,” offers a conceptual schema specifically designed for describing observation acts and their results, including features involved in observation and sampling.[15] The standard defines models for exchanging information about observation events and their outcomes both within and between scientific and technical communities, enabling more precise representation of when, where, and how a measurement was made and what it signifies (https://www.iso.org/standard/82463.html).[15] In the environmental context, ISO 19156 builds on earlier work such as the OGC Observations and Measurements model and provides abstractions such as **Observation**, **SamplingFeature**, **Party**, and **Procedure**, which can be specialized for particular domains including hydrology, atmospheric science, and potentially exposure science. For example, an observation of particulate matter concentration at a sensor location can be modeled with a feature-of-interest (the air at that location), an observed property (PM\(_{2.5}\) mass concentration), a result (the measured value), and a procedure (the measurement method), along with temporal and spatial context.[15]

The governing body for ISO 19156 is again ISO TC 211, and the standard achieved publication status in 2023, indicating its contemporary relevance and ongoing support.[15] Unlike ISO 19115, which is dataset-centric, ISO 19156 is **observation-centric**, describing the unit of observation or sample as the primary unit of metadata. This makes it much more suitable for modeling **instance-level exposure values**, such as the exposure of a particular individual at a specific time and location, derived either from direct measurement or from model-based estimation. In principle, one can treat each computed exposure value as the result of an observation act, where the feature-of-interest is the individual or their environment, the observed property is the exposure metric (e.g., daily average PM\(_{2.5}\) at residence), and the procedure is the computation workflow involving geocoding, interpolation, and aggregation. If that procedure is modeled explicitly, ISO 19156 can carry provenance-like information describing input datasets and computation methods, although the standard itself does not prescribe a formal representation of computational workflows comparable to W3C PROV.[15][16]

From a practitioner perspective, ISO 19156’s abstraction power is both an opportunity and a challenge. Many environmental and earth-science data systems have not yet fully adopted observation-centric metadata at the exposure-value level, preferring to keep metadata attached to datasets rather than individual rows. Implementing ISO 19156 requires schema engineering and tooling that can represent observation instances, link them to features-of-interest, and manage large volumes of metadata when exposure datasets involve millions of rows (e.g., daily exposure values for thousands of subjects over years). However, the benefit is that ISO 19156 aligns closely with how exposure scientists think about measurements and samples, especially when integrating multi-domain observations such as chemical exposures, meteorological conditions, and biological markers, and it provides a path toward standardized instance-level descriptions that could be interoperable with OGC and ISO-compliant infrastructures.[15]

### NetCDF, CF, and ACDD metadata conventions for environmental grids

Many environmental datasets used to compute exposures are distributed as netCDF files containing gridded fields such as temperature, pollutant concentrations, or climate model outputs. The **Climate and Forecast (CF) Metadata Conventions** define community-agreed practices for describing the physical meaning of variables in such files, including standard names, units, coordinate systems, and cell methods, thereby enabling users and software to interpret and combine data consistently (CF conventions canonical URL, unverified). The **Attribute Convention for Data Discovery (ACDD)** builds upon CF and specifies a set of recommended attributes for describing netCDF datasets in ways that facilitate discovery in digital libraries and catalogs, such as titles, summaries, keywords, spatial and temporal coverage, and provenance-related information like history and source (https://wiki.esipfed.org/Attribute_Convention_for_Data_Discovery_1-3).[10] The ACDD documentation explicitly frames these attributes as supporting discovery systems and notes their compatibility with CF conventions, positioning ACDD as a discovery-layer complement to CF’s semantic-layer variable descriptions.[10]

The governing bodies for CF and ACDD are community initiatives rooted in the earth-science data community, often coordinated through organizations such as the Earth Science Information Partners (ESIP) Federation and related groups. ACDD, as described in the ESIP documentation, is mature enough to have multiple versioned specifications and adoption by major data providers.[10] The unit of description in CF and ACDD is primarily the **variable within a dataset** (for CF standard names and units) and the **dataset as a whole** (for ACDD attributes). Their primary purpose is to ensure that gridded environmental data can be accurately interpreted and discovered: CF tells you that a variable called “air_temperature” with a particular standard_name refers to a specific physical quantity, while ACDD tells you what geographic region, time range, and general topic the dataset covers.[10] In terms of provenance, CF and ACDD metadata often include a **history** attribute capturing processing steps applied to the file, and a **source** attribute indicating upstream datasets or models, but these are typically string fields whose content depends on the data producer’s practice, not a structured provenance model.

For environmental-exposure data, CF and ACDD are ubiquitous when exposures are derived from climate or atmospheric products, including Essential Climate Variables (ECVs) identified by the Global Climate Observing System.[19] NASA Earthdata’s description of Essential Variables emphasizes these atmosphere, land, and ocean variables as foundational for climate monitoring and research, and many ECV datasets are distributed with CF-compliant netCDF metadata (https://www.earthdata.nasa.gov/learn/earth-observation-data-basics/essential-variables).[19] Practitioners appreciate CF/ACDD’s balance between structure and flexibility: they are relatively straightforward to implement, widely supported by software, and do not require the heavy authoring burden of ISO 19115, while still giving a scientifically meaningful description of environmental fields. However, from an exposure provenance perspective, CF/ACDD’s history and source attributes are insufficient for full instance-level reproducibility of subject-level exposures; they record the processing history of the environmental grid, not the downstream overlay computations performed in epidemiological studies.

### SpatioTemporal Asset Catalog (STAC) for environmental assets

The **SpatioTemporal Asset Catalog (STAC)** specification defines a JSON-based standard for describing geospatial assets and their collections, focused on making spatio-temporal data such as satellite imagery and derived products easily discoverable and interoperable through APIs.[5] The ECMWF Data Stores STAC catalogue API exemplifies STAC’s application in atmospheric and air-quality contexts by providing a STAC-compliant API to access catalogues of air quality forecasts at European observation stations, including daily forecasts optimized via statistical post-processing (https://ads.atmosphere.copernicus.eu/stac-browser/).[5] In STAC, the primary unit of description is the **Item**, representing a single spatio-temporal asset (e.g., a raster file, image, or dataset slice), which includes geometry, timestamp, links to associated files, and a set of properties that can be domain-specific (e.g., cloud cover, processing level). Collections group items and can carry higher-level metadata such as licensing, providers, and summaries of temporal and spatial extents.

STAC is governed by an open community process, documented on the STAC specification site (https://stacspec.org, unverified), and its maturity has increased substantially in recent years as multiple cloud data platforms have adopted it. Its primary purpose is **data discovery and interoperability across geospatial data stores**, not detailed provenance modeling. Provenance-related information can be included in STAC properties, and items can link to additional metadata such as process descriptions or quality reports, but STAC does not prescribe a structured provenance ontology. For environmental-exposure data, STAC is most relevant for **describing the environmental datasets** that serve as exposure inputs, such as gridded air-quality analyses or climate model outputs stored as cloud assets, enabling exposure pipelines to search and retrieve appropriate inputs via standardized APIs.[5] The unit of description remains the environmental asset, not the derived exposure values.

Practitioners working with exposure pipelines on cloud platforms often welcome STAC’s simplicity: it leverages familiar JSON formats, has good ecosystem support (Python libraries, web UIs), and is well integrated with commercial and open-source data infrastructures. However, many report that STAC metadata is only as good as what providers choose to include; while spatial extent and temporal range are usually present, domain-specific details (e.g., pollutant species, model configuration, uncertainty measures) may be missing or inconsistently recorded, complicating reuse in exposure modeling. STAC’s flexibility can also lead to interoperability challenges as different providers define custom properties for similar concepts, such as concentration units or processing versions, underscoring the need for controlled vocabularies or profiles when using STAC for exposure-relevant data.

### DCAT: Data Catalog Vocabulary for web data catalogs

The W3C **Data Catalog Vocabulary (DCAT) Version 3** is an RDF vocabulary designed to facilitate interoperability between data catalogs published on the Web, representing catalogs, datasets, and distributions, and related resources such as data services (https://www.w3.org/TR/vocab-dcat-3/).[11] DCAT’s schema defines the classes `dcat:Catalog`, `dcat:Dataset`, and `dcat:Distribution`, along with properties for titles, descriptions, keywords, temporal and spatial coverage, themes, and links to documentation, licenses, and provenance information.[11] DCAT is intended to be domain-agnostic and is widely used by open data portals and governmental catalogues to expose their holdings in machine-readable form, often in combination with other vocabularies such as Dublin Core and FOAF. DCAT-AP profiles (e.g., in the European Union) further refine the vocabulary by specifying mandatory and recommended properties for particular contexts, improving interoperability across portals.

The governing body for DCAT is the W3C, and Version 3 reflects a mature, stable vocabulary with extensions for data services, temporal relations, and alignment with the FAIR principles.[11] The unit of description is primarily the **dataset and its distributions**, meaning files, APIs, or services through which the dataset can be accessed. DCAT’s primary purpose is **catalog-level discovery and interoperability**, enabling search engines, aggregators, and clients to understand what datasets exist and how to retrieve them. DCAT does include properties that can reference provenance descriptions, such as `prov:wasGeneratedBy`, and can link to provenance documents encoded in PROV or other vocabularies, but it does not itself define a detailed provenance model.[11][16] For environmental-exposure data, DCAT is an appropriate standard for describing catalogs of exposure-related datasets (e.g., a portal of exposure metrics for multiple cohorts, or a government catalog of environmental monitoring datasets), and it can support meta-analysis by making datasets discoverable across institutions.

Practitioners appreciate DCAT’s relative simplicity and its alignment with web technologies, but note that implementing DCAT effectively still requires RDF expertise and careful mapping from existing catalog schemas. Documentation quality is high for the base specification, but profiles like DCAT-AP introduce additional complexity, and not all data portals fully implement the recommended properties, leading to heterogeneous metadata quality. For exposure researchers, DCAT aids in discovering environmental input datasets and sometimes epidemiological outputs, but it does not inherently address the **instance-level structure of exposure values** or the computational provenance of how those values were obtained.

### schema.org Dataset and science-on-schema.org guidance

Schema.org is a broad vocabulary for structured data on the web, and its `Dataset` type provides a generic way to describe datasets, including name, description, keywords, temporal and spatial coverage, and distributions (https://schema.org/Dataset).[12] The `Dataset` type is frequently used in JSON-LD embedded in dataset landing pages so that search engines can index and understand scientific data more effectively.[12] Recognizing the need for consistent conventions in the scientific domain, the ESIP Federation and collaborators created **science-on-schema.org**, a set of guidelines and examples for publishing schema.org markup for scientific datasets and repositories, with the goal of improving data discovery through common patterns and best practices (https://github.com/esipfed/science-on-schema.org).[13] These guidelines provide detailed instructions on how to populate fields like `variableMeasured`, `creator`, `spatialCoverage`, and `temporalCoverage`, and how to link datasets to repositories and related resources using stable identifiers.[13]

Schema.org is governed by a collaborative community process with major search engines and web stakeholders, while science-on-schema.org is a community-driven guidance project rather than a formal standard. The unit of description is again the **dataset**, possibly with some variable-level metadata via `variableMeasured` and related properties. The primary purpose is **web-centric discovery and SEO**, making datasets more visible and interpretable by web clients and search engines. Provenance can be indicated in schema.org through properties like `isBasedOn` or via references to PROV documents, but the core dataset description is not a detailed provenance model.[12][13][16] For environmental-exposure data, schema.org `Dataset` and science-on-schema.org guidance are relevant when exposure datasets are published as web-accessible resources with landing pages, for example, a repository of cohort-level exposure metrics or gridded exposure surfaces derived from models.

Practitioners often see schema.org as a “lightweight” option compared to ISO or DCAT, particularly because many repository platforms now support schema.org markup automatically. The science-on-schema.org guidelines explicitly aim to lower the barrier to adoption by repository operators, emphasizing consistent patterns rather than exhaustive metadata.[13] However, in exposure science, schema.org’s generality means that many crucial details—such as the exact exposure metric definition, computational method, and uncertainty model—must either be encoded in free-text descriptions or in custom extensions that may not interoperate well across sites. Thus, schema.org is a valuable outer layer for discovery but insufficient on its own for reproducible exposure meta-analysis.

### DataCite DOI metadata for research datasets

DataCite operates a global DOI registration service for research data and related scholarly outputs, and its **DataCite Metadata Schema** defines a rich set of descriptive metadata that accompanies DOIs, helping users understand and contextualize research resources (https://datacite.org/blog/connecting-the-dots-with-datacite-doi-metadata/).[17] A DataCite blog post on “Connecting the Dots with DataCite DOI Metadata” explains how the metadata schema allows resources to be linked together, e.g., connecting datasets to software, publications, and other related works through relation types such as `IsSupplementTo`, `IsDerivedFrom`, and `IsSourceOf`.[17] These relations can encode simple provenance or derivation information, while descriptive elements capture titles, creators, descriptions, publication year, resource type, and subject keywords. DataCite DOIs are widely used to cite datasets, including environmental and exposure-related datasets, ensuring persistent identification and supporting citation metrics.

The DataCite Metadata Schema is governed by the DataCite community and updated periodically; although it is not domain-specific, its maturity and wide adoption make it a cornerstone of modern research data citation. The unit of description is the **citable resource**, commonly a dataset or software package, and the schema’s primary purpose is **citation, discovery, and basic contextualization**.[17] Provenance is limited to high-level relationships to other resources; DataCite does not specify the internal structure of datasets or the detailed computational pathways by which they were created. For environmental-exposure data, assigning DOIs via DataCite metadata allows exposure datasets and code packages to be persistently cited and discoverable, and the relation types can be used to link, for example, a cohort-specific exposure dataset to the underlying environmental model outputs and the epidemiological study that used it.[17] However, DataCite metadata alone cannot support instance-level reproducibility of exposure values.

Practitioners generally value DataCite DOIs for their low incremental cost relative to the benefits of persistent identification and academic credit. Many institutional repositories integrate DataCite, so metadata entry can be done via familiar web forms. Complaints tend to center on the effort required to maintain DOI metadata when datasets are updated or corrected, and on the limited domain-specific expressiveness of the schema: environmental-exposure scientists sometimes wish they could encode more detailed semantics about exposures or provenance directly in DOI metadata, but must instead rely on linking to external documentation or richer metadata schemas.

### Frictionless Data Package for tabular datasets

The **Frictionless Data Package** specification, developed by the Frictionless Data community, defines a simple container format for describing a coherent collection of data in a single “package,” typically consisting of tabular files and a descriptor that specifies schema, types, and basic metadata.[18] The Frictionless documentation describes Data Package as a “simple container format for describing a coherent collection of data in a single ‘package’” and emphasizes that it provides a basis for convenient delivery, installation, and management of datasets (https://specs.frictionlessdata.io/data-package/).[18] A Data Package includes a `datapackage.json` descriptor file listing resources (e.g., CSV files), their schemas (field names, types, constraints), and metadata such as title, description, licenses, and sources. Additional specifications, such as Tabular Data Resource, define how tabular resources should be described.

Frictionless Data Package is governed by an open-source community, with versioned specifications and reference implementations in languages like Python and JavaScript. Its unit of description is the **dataset and its component files**, with a strong emphasis on tabular data. The primary purpose is **schema declaration and lightweight packaging**, enabling tools to validate data consistency, load datasets with known types, and share them in a standardized way.[18] Provenance metadata can be included in descriptive fields but is not a central focus of the original specification. For environmental-exposure data, Frictionless Data Package is attractive because many exposure datasets are tabular: subject IDs, exposure metrics, dates, and covariates can be packaged with clear schemas that specify units and allowable values. While the spec does not provide a full provenance ontology, schema fields such as `sources` and `contributors` can capture some context, and additional custom properties can record processing steps.

In practice, environmental-exposure practitioners who adopt Frictionless Data Package often do so because of its **low deployment friction**: creating a `datapackage.json` file is straightforward, tooling is lightweight, and validation helps catch data issues early in pipelines. The main limitations are that more complex provenance or hierarchical metadata must be encoded as custom properties, and interoperability across institutions depends on shared conventions about field naming, units, and semantics. Nevertheless, Frictionless Data Package can serve as a practical intermediate standard bridging raw exposure tables and richer metadata models like ISO 19156 or OMOP.

### RO-Crate: FAIR packaging of research objects

**Research Object Crate (RO-Crate)** is a community effort to establish a lightweight approach to packaging research data with their metadata, built on schema.org annotations in JSON-LD and designed to support FAIR principles for diverse research objects (https://www.researchobject.org/ro-crate/).[6] The RO-Crate specification describes how to represent datasets, files, software, workflows, and provenance information in a single machine-readable “crate,” with an index file (`ro-crate-metadata.json`) that enumerates entities and their relationships.[6] Unlike dataset-focused standards, RO-Crate treats the research activity as a collection of interconnected resources, and it supports representing workflows, parameter values, and execution traces, often in combination with W3C PROV or related models. Its design allows for packaging not just exposure datasets but also the code and configuration used to compute them, as well as documentation and derived outputs.

RO-Crate’s governance is community-based, with active development and adoption in research infrastructures and workflow systems. Its unit of description is the **research object**, which can encompass multiple datasets, software components, and provenance entities. The primary purpose is **FAIR-compliant packaging and reproducibility**, making it easier to transport, archive, and interpret complex research outputs.[6] Provenance is a central concern: RO-Crate encourages the use of schema.org and PROV to describe how files were produced, which workflows or tools were involved, and what parameters were used, and it can capture instance-level details when appropriate.[6][16] For environmental-exposure data, RO-Crate offers a compelling way to represent entire exposure pipelines, from input environmental grids and cohort data to computed exposure tables and downstream analysis code, in a single structured package.

From a practitioner viewpoint, RO-Crate is powerful but still emerging. Early adopters report that RO-Crate aligns well with reproducible workflow systems and containerized analysis (e.g., using CWL or Nextflow), but creating high-quality RO-Crates requires effort and familiarity with JSON-LD and semantic web concepts. Documentation is robust but evolving, and interoperability across RO-Crate implementations depends on shared profiles and conventions. For exposure science, RO-Crate is among the few standards that can plausibly support **instance-level reproducibility** of exposure values by describing not just datasets but also the computational processes that generated them.

### Croissant: ML-ready metadata for datasets

**Croissant** is a newer metadata format introduced by Google Research and collaborators to describe ML-ready datasets, aiming to make datasets easier to discover, understand, and use in machine learning workflows (https://research.google/blog/croissant-a-metadata-format-for-ml-ready-datasets/).[7] The Croissant format does not change how data is represented (e.g., image or text file formats) but provides a standard way to describe and organize datasets, including features, labels, splits, and usage notes.[7] Croissant is built to integrate with ML tooling and to support automated dataset ingestion, and it uses structured metadata to describe data types, relationships, and recommended preprocessing, allowing ML pipelines to understand datasets with minimal manual intervention.

Croissant’s governance is community-based with contributions from industry and academia, and while its maturity is not yet on par with ISO or W3C standards, it represents a significant recent effort in 2023–2024 to standardize ML dataset metadata.[7] The unit of description is the **ML-ready dataset**, including features and splits, and the primary purpose is **ML usability and discovery**. Provenance is not the main focus, but Croissant can encode some information about data sources and transformations via descriptive fields and relationships. For environmental-exposure data, Croissant could describe exposure datasets prepared for ML modeling, such as arrays of exposure features and health outcomes, and it could assist in sharing datasets across ML practitioners, but it does not directly address geospatial or observational semantics that are critical for exposure interpretation.

Practitioners working at the interface of exposure science and ML may find Croissant attractive because it standardizes ML-relevant metadata, enabling easier reuse of exposure datasets in modeling tasks such as exposure prediction or risk forecasting. However, they must often combine Croissant with other standards if they want to preserve the environmental and geospatial context of exposures. Croissant’s tooling and documentation are still evolving, and integration with existing exposure pipelines may require custom adapters. As such, Croissant is best viewed as a complementary metadata layer focused on ML usage rather than a core provenance or environmental semantics standard.

### Summary: Units of description and domain specificity

Across these general standards, a pattern emerges regarding the **unit of description** and **domain specificity**. ISO 19115/19139, DCAT, schema.org Dataset, DataCite, and Frictionless Data Package are primarily **dataset-centric**, describing collections of data and their distributions, with limited variable-level or observation-level detail.[2][11][12][13][17][18] CF and ACDD add **variable-level semantics** in the context of gridded environmental data, making them more domain-specific to climate and atmosphere.[10][19] STAC describes **spatio-temporal assets**, often environmental products, and is domain-specific to geospatial imagery and grid-like assets.[5] ISO 19156 is **observation-centric**, and thus uniquely suited among these standards to modeling individual exposure measurements or derived values.[15] RO-Crate and Croissant describe **research objects and ML-ready datasets**, respectively, with domain-general designs but particular emphasis on workflows and ML features.[6][7] Domain specificity varies: CF/ACDD, ISO 19156, and STAC are more environmental-science-oriented; DCAT, schema.org, DataCite, Frictionless, RO-Crate, and Croissant are general-purpose, though often used in scientific contexts.[2][10][11][12][13][15][17][18]

In terms of **provenance**, ISO 19115 lineage, CF/ACDD history, and DataCite relations offer basic support, while W3C PROV and RO-Crate provide robust, structured provenance models, as discussed later.[10][11][14][16][17] However, many of these standards stop at dataset-level discovery or coarse processing descriptions; only ISO 19156 and provenance-focused frameworks can, with sufficient implementation effort, support detailed instance-level reproducibility of exposure values. The following sections explore provenance standards and clinical data models in more depth, before returning to a comparative analysis of instance-level support.

## Metadata Standards and Models in Clinical and Public-Health Exposure Research

### OMOP Common Data Model and exposure domains

The **Observational Medical Outcomes Partnership (OMOP) Common Data Model (CDM)** is an open community data standard designed to standardize the structure and content of observational health data, enabling systematic analyses and the generation of reliable evidence (https://www.ohdsi.org/data-standardization/).[20] The OMOP CDM transforms data from disparate sources—such as electronic health records (EHRs), claims data, and registries—into a common format using standardized terminologies, vocabularies, and coding schemes, and provides a library of analytic routines that operate on this standardized structure.[20] The OMOP CDM v5.4 specification, hosted on GitHub, describes tables such as `DRUG_EXPOSURE`, which captures records about exposure to a drug ingested or otherwise introduced into the body, including timing, dosage, and routes of administration (https://ohdsi.github.io/CommonDataModel/cdm54.html).[8] While OMOP’s core tables focus on clinical exposures (drugs, procedures, conditions), extensions and proposed domains address **external exposures**, such as environmental factors, making OMOP increasingly relevant to environmental-exposure research.

OMOP is governed by the OHDSI community, which maintains the CDM specification, vocabularies, and analytic tools. Its unit of description is primarily the **clinical event or exposure** at the person-level, recorded in tables keyed by person identifiers and dates.[8][20] The primary purpose is **standardization for analysis**, not metadata per se, but OMOP’s structured schema and vocabulary mappings implicitly serve as a metadata standard for observational health exposures. Provenance is present in limited forms: OMOP can record the source of data, type of record, and mapping to source codes, but detailed computational provenance for derived variables is not central in the base CDM. For environmental-exposure data, OMOP’s potential lies in its ability to host **exposure variables** alongside health outcomes in a standardized schema, enabling cross-cohort analyses and meta-analysis with consistent structure.

Practitioners in the OHDSI community often note that extending OMOP to environmental exposures involves both schema design and vocabulary integration. They must decide whether exposures such as air pollution or heat are represented as observations, measurements, or specialized exposure tables, and must integrate environmental terminologies with OMOP’s standard vocabularies. Implementation cost can be high because transforming EHR data into OMOP is already complex, and adding environmental-exposure linkages introduces further data engineering challenges. However, once environmental exposures are represented in OMOP, analytic tools can treat them like any other covariates or exposures, greatly facilitating large-scale comparative research.

### FHIR-based representations and the FHIR PIT tool

The **Fast Healthcare Interoperability Resources (FHIR)** standard, managed by HL7, provides a modern web-friendly framework for exchanging healthcare data, including resources for patients, observations, and locations. While FHIR’s core specification is general-purpose for clinical data, environmental-exposure research increasingly uses FHIR to represent health data linked to exposures. The **FHIR PIT** tool (FHIR Public Health Integrated Tool) is described as an open-source tool to link EHR data in FHIR format with environmental exposures data derived from public sources (https://niehs.github.io/PCOR_bookdown_tools/chapter-fhir-pit.html).[9] FHIR PIT ingests EHR data encoded as FHIR resources, retrieves or computes environmental-exposure metrics for patient locations and times, and stores the resulting linked data, enabling exposure analyses in clinical contexts.[9]

FHIR is governed by HL7, with frequent updates and implementation guides, while FHIR PIT is a project-specific tool backed by the NIH and environmental health researchers.[9] The unit of description in FHIR PIT is the **linked patient-exposure record**, which combines FHIR `Patient`, `Location`, and `Observation` resources with externally derived environmental data.[9] The primary purpose is **pipeline-level linkage and documentation**, not standardization of exposure semantics across institutions. Provenance in FHIR PIT can be recorded using FHIR’s `Provenance` resource and related elements, and the tool’s documentation encourages use of provenance resources to describe how exposures were computed from public data sources.[9] However, FHIR PIT itself is more accurately classified as a **pipeline sidecar framework** than a general metadata standard: it uses FHIR to structure health data and attaches exposure metrics, but its linkage logic is project-specific.

Practitioners using FHIR PIT appreciate its integration with existing FHIR infrastructures, lowering the barrier to linking exposures to EHRs. Implementation friction arises in configuring data sources, geocoding patient locations, and ensuring that environmental datasets are current and correctly matched. FHIR’s documentation quality is high, but combining FHIR with external environmental data requires cross-disciplinary expertise. FHIR PIT demonstrates that FHIR resources can be used to represent exposure observations at the patient level, but it also highlights the need for more standardized environmental profiles and vocabularies within FHIR to make such linkages interoperable across projects.

### EPA and CDC environmental public health tracking metadata practices

The U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) collects extensive exposure-related information under programs such as the Toxic Substances Control Act, and maintains databases to support exposure modeling.[3][4] The EPA’s **Consolidated Human Activity Database (CHAD)** contains data obtained from pre-existing human activity studies collected at city, state, and national levels, comprising about 180,000 days of human activity data that are used as inputs to exposure models (https://www.epa.gov/fera/human-exposure-modeling-databases-support-exposure-modeling).[4] The **Human Exposure Database System (HEDS)** is an integrated database that contains chemical measurements, questionnaire responses, documents, and other information related to EPA research studies of exposure of people to environmental contaminants; data and associated documents are made available through dedicated sites.[4] While CHAD and HEDS are data repositories rather than metadata standards, they embody metadata practices, including documentation of study protocols, sampling methods, and population characteristics, that influence how exposure data are reused and interpreted.

Similarly, the **Environmental Public Health Tracking (EPHT) Network** coordinated by the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) has developed descriptive metadata standards to help users locate data on the network.[1] A PubMed-indexed article (PMID: 18849771) describes the implementation of descriptive metadata standards on the EPHT Network to support documentation and discovery of environmental and health data, including development of critical information attributes such as purpose, location, and content of datasets.[1] These metadata standards are tailored to EPHT but reflect broader needs in environmental health informatics: they aim to make surveillance data about environmental hazards and health outcomes discoverable, interoperable, and usable for public-health decision-making.

In terms of units of description, CHAD and HEDS metadata describe **studies, datasets, and variables**, while EPHT descriptive metadata focuses on **network datasets and indicators**.[1][4] Their primary purpose is **documentation and discovery** within agency systems, and they do not constitute general, external standards like ISO or DCAT. Provenance is captured in varying degrees, mostly at the study and dataset level, including references to sampling designs and analytical methods, but detailed instance-level computational provenance for derived exposures is rarely structured in machine-readable form. Practitioners using CHAD and HEDS often note the effort needed to interpret questionnaire instruments, sampling frames, and coding schemes, underscoring the importance of structured metadata for these components even though no single, universally adopted standard presently exists.

### Integrating geospatial exposure datasets with clinical data models

A central challenge in environmental-exposure research is integrating geospatial environmental datasets—often described using ISO, CF, STAC, or similar standards—with clinical data models such as OMOP or FHIR. In practical pipelines, exposure values for individuals are computed by overlaying geocoded addresses or activity patterns with environmental grids or station observations, using geospatial computations that may include interpolation, kriging, or nearest-neighbor selection.[4][5][15] The resulting exposure values are then stored in clinical data systems as variables, observations, or exposures linked to individuals. Metadata standards must therefore span both the environmental and clinical domains, or at least be able to interoperate.

From a metadata standpoint, one viable strategy is to treat environmental datasets as ISO/CF/STAC-described resources and exposure values as ISO 19156 observations or OMOP/FHIR-specified clinical observations, linked via W3C PROV entities and activities that describe geocoding and overlay processes.[2][10][11][15][16][20] For example, an exposure registry might record that a particular subject’s daily average PM\(_{2.5}\) exposure is an ISO 19156 observation whose result was generated by a geospatial overlay activity, which used as inputs a CF-compliant air-quality grid and an OMOP-coded address table, with provenance expressed in PROV and the resulting dataset cataloged via DCAT or schema.org Dataset.[10][11][13][15][16][20] This kind of cross-domain modeling is conceptually feasible but rarely implemented in full, largely due to the complexity of coordinating multiple standards and the lack of widely adopted profiles that specialize them for exposure science.

Practitioners often critique the state of cross-domain metadata integration as “too theoretical,” with standards bodies providing powerful abstractions but limited guidance on concrete profiles and examples for environmental-exposure pipelines. Many exposure projects adopt partial solutions, such as using OMOP for health data and storing exposure values as custom observation types, while leaving environmental datasets in local formats with minimal ISO or CF metadata, and capturing pipeline details in ad hoc sidecar files. This fragmentation limits meta-analysis and reproducibility, especially when exposures must be compared across cohorts that used different environmental input datasets, geocoding logic, and pipeline conventions.

## Provenance, Reproducibility, and Meta-Analysis: Standards for Tracking Derivation of Exposure Values

### W3C PROV: A general provenance model

The **W3C PROV** family of documents provides a suite of specifications for representing provenance information on the web, defining a conceptual model of entities, activities, and agents and relations such as `wasGeneratedBy`, `used`, and `wasDerivedFrom` (https://www.w3.org/TR/prov-overview/).[16] The PROV-Overview document offers a non-normative roadmap to the family, describing how different specifications—PROV-DM (data model), PROV-O (OWL ontology), PROV-N (notation), and PROV-AQ (access and query)—can be used together to capture provenance across heterogeneous systems.[16] PROV is domain-agnostic and intended to be applied to any situation where one wishes to record how a resource was produced, by whom, from what inputs, and using which processes, making it a key candidate for modeling provenance in environmental-exposure pipelines.

In PROV, the unit of description is the **provenance graph**, composed of entities (data, documents, intermediate results), activities (processes, computations), and agents (people, organizations, software). The primary purpose is **structured provenance modeling** for reproducibility, accountability, and trust.[16] PROV’s conceptual model is rich enough to capture instance-level derivation: one can represent individual exposure values as entities that are `wasGeneratedBy` a computation activity that `used` specific environmental input entities and subject-level data entities, with attributes describing parameters, time of execution, and responsible agents. Provenance graphs can be serialized in RDF using PROV-O, allowing integration with other semantic web vocabularies such as DCAT and schema.org.[11][12][16]

Practitioners who have attempted to apply PROV to scientific workflows often report mixed experiences. On the positive side, PROV’s conceptual clarity and generic nature make it suitable for representing complex pipelines, and it has been integrated into workflow systems and research infrastructures. On the challenging side, capturing complete, meaningful provenance for large-scale computations is labor-intensive, requires instrumentation in code, and may produce large graphs that are difficult to manage. In environmental-exposure pipelines, implementing PROV at the instance level would mean recording provenance for millions of exposure values, which may be impractical; thus, many projects settle for **coarse-grained provenance** at the dataset level, where PROV describes the overall pipeline that generated a cohort’s exposures but not each individual value.

### Provenance in RO-Crate, DataCite, and other packaging standards

RO-Crate’s design explicitly incorporates provenance concepts, often relying on PROV or analogous relations encoded in JSON-LD.[6][16] The RO-Crate metadata file can include entities representing input datasets, scripts, workflow descriptions, and output datasets, with properties indicating `isDerivedFrom` or similar relations, and contextual attributes describing execution environments, versions, and parameters.[6] In environmental-exposure pipelines, an RO-Crate can thus encapsulate not only raw environmental grids and cohort data but also the code that produced exposure values, along with a provenance graph linking these components. RO-Crate’s alignment with schema.org makes it easier to publish crates in web-friendly formats, but the core provenance semantics derive from PROV and related models.[6][16]

DataCite’s relation types provide a more limited but still useful form of provenance, allowing one to assert that a dataset `IsDerivedFrom` another or `IsSupplementTo` a publication.[17] For example, an exposure dataset could have a DataCite DOI that relates it to the underlying environmental model outputs and the cohort study that used it, offering a coarse provenance chain.[17] Frictionless Data Package can encode provenance-like information in custom metadata fields, such as `sources` or `path`, but does not adopt a formal provenance ontology.[18] DCAT can link datasets to provenance documents via `prov:qualifiedAttribution` or other terms when combined with PROV.[11][16]

Practitioners frequently encounter trade-offs when deciding how deeply to implement provenance in these packaging standards. RO-Crate offers the most comprehensive potential, but requires adoption of a relatively heavy-weight metadata ecosystem; DataCite and Frictionless are lighter but less expressive. In exposure projects, a pragmatic approach may be to use DataCite for citation-level provenance, Frictionless or RO-Crate for packaging and schema-level metadata, and PROV for detailed provenance in critical workflows, such as those used to derive key exposure metrics for regulatory submissions.

### Instance-level reproducibility using ISO 19156 and observation models

ISO 19156’s observation-centric model provides another pathway to instance-level reproducibility of exposure values.[15] If exposure values are modeled as observations, the standard encourages explicit representation of the **procedure** used to obtain the result, including sampling strategies and measurement protocols. In environmental-exposure pipelines where exposures are computed rather than directly measured, the “procedure” can be understood as a computational algorithm, with inputs representing environmental datasets and subject data; ISO 19156 can record references to these inputs and some aspects of the methodology.[15] When combined with PROV, ISO 19156 observation entities can be part of a provenance graph where each observation is an entity generated by a specific activity representing the computation.

However, practitioners face significant challenges in implementing ISO 19156 at full granularity for large exposure datasets. Recording a separate observation entity for every subject-day exposure value may be prohibitively large, and many pipelines treat exposures as simple numeric results embedded in tables without explicit observation metadata. A more realistic strategy may be to apply ISO 19156 to aggregated or representative observations (e.g., exposures at key times or sites) and to rely on dataset-level provenance for bulk computations. Nonetheless, the conceptual alignment between ISO 19156 and exposure science is strong, and schema engineers designing an exposure registry could build observation-level support into their model, even if not every project chooses to populate it fully.

### Dataset-level discovery versus instance-level provenance: comparative analysis

When comparing standards across their support for dataset-level discovery and instance-level provenance, several tiers become apparent. DCAT, schema.org Dataset, DataCite, EPHT descriptive metadata, and ACDD are primarily **discovery-oriented**: they focus on describing datasets enough to be found and broadly understood, and while they may include fields for lineage or sources, they are not intended to capture detailed derivation of individual values.[1][10][11][12][13][17] ISO 19115/19139 add more structured lineage elements but remain dataset-centric.[2][14] STAC, CF, and environmental data portals similarly expose discovery-level metadata about environmental grids or assets.[5][10][19] On the other hand, PROV and ISO 19156 are explicitly designed for modeling processes and observations, and can support **instance-level provenance** when implemented rigorously.[15][16] RO-Crate sits in between, providing a container for both datasets and provenance graphs, enabling detailed reproducibility if used with PROV and observation models.[6][16]

Clinical data models like OMOP and FHIR, when extended to environmental exposures, can represent instance-level exposure records at the person level, but they do not themselves define detailed computational provenance for how exposure values were derived from environmental data.[8][9][20] Pipeline sidecar formats, such as geocoding logs, gridMET download manifests, or FHIR PIT linkage tables, often contain crucial details (specific query parameters, temporal windows, selected datasets) that are necessary for reproducing exposure values but are not standardized.[4][9] Thus, from a practitioner perspective, the standards that **theoretically support instance-level reproducibility** are PROV, ISO 19156, RO-Crate, and certain FHIR resources, while **commonly deployed standards in exposure science** tend to stop at dataset-level discovery, leaving a gap that must be filled by pipeline-specific conventions.

## Pipeline Sidecar Formats in Environmental-Exposure Workflows

### Concept and role of pipeline sidecars

Pipeline sidecars are structured metadata artifacts that arise alongside specific data-processing pipelines rather than as part of general standards. In environmental-exposure workflows, these sidecars might take the form of JSON files summarizing geocoding results (e.g., mapping input addresses to coordinates and census block IDs), CSV files documenting which environmental datasets were downloaded and used in overlay computations, YAML files capturing parameter settings for exposure models, or intermediate relational tables linking patient identifiers to environmental grid cells. Sidecars are typically generated by code and used internally to drive subsequent steps or to audit computations.

The role of sidecars in exposure pipelines is often critical: they encode **instance-level operational details** that are too granular or too idiosyncratic to be captured in general metadata standards. For example, a geocoding sidecar might record which geocoding service was used, how ambiguous addresses were handled, which addresses failed to geocode, and the mapping from addresses to coordinates and administrative units. An exposure computation manifest might list the exact version of environmental datasets, the date ranges sampled, and the interpolation method used. These details are essential for reproducing exposure values, especially in regulatory or high-stakes studies, but they live in local formats without broader interoperability.

From a schema engineer’s standpoint, pipeline sidecars highlight the tension between **standardization and specificity**. General standards must be domain-agnostic and widely applicable, which constrains the level of detail they specify for particular computational workflows. Sidecars, by contrast, are free to be as specific as necessary but may not be understandable beyond the original project. The challenge is to design exposure registries and metadata frameworks that can **ingest and structure the information contained in sidecars**, mapping it to more general provenance and observation models (e.g., PROV and ISO 19156) without losing detail.

### Examples of sidecars in geocoding and environmental data retrieval

Although formal documentation is scarce, many exposure pipelines implement sidecar patterns. Geocoding tools such as DeGAUSS (unverified URL) typically produce intermediate files summarizing geocoded locations, including input address, output coordinates, geocoding score, and associated geographic identifiers (e.g., census tract), structured as CSV or JSON. These files are then used to join subject data to environmental grids. Similarly, pipelines that retrieve environmental data from services like gridMET or the ECMWF Data Stores may record sidecars listing the dataset IDs, query parameters, temporal ranges, and local filenames, effectively serving as reproducible download manifests.

The ECMWF STAC browser illustrates a more standardized approach to describing environmental assets, but exposure pipelines often add their own sidecars on top of STAC metadata to record how assets were selected and used.[5] For instance, a pipeline might query STAC for daily air-quality forecasts at specific locations and then record in a manifest which STAC items were retrieved and how they were aggregated for each subject. FHIR PIT’s documentation indicates that it uses configuration files and logs to capture the linkage between FHIR resources and environmental data sources, effectively forming another class of sidecar.[9]

Practitioners often treat these sidecars as internal implementation details rather than as artifacts to be shared, but when studies are revisited or replicated, sidecars become invaluable. Complaints in conference talks and GitHub issues frequently revolve around missing or poorly documented sidecars, such as not knowing which version of environmental data was used or which geocoding settings were applied. This underscores the need to treat sidecars as first-class metadata resources, even if they are not general standards.

### Practitioner experiences: flexibility versus standardization

From the perspective of a working practitioner in a public-health agency or research group, pipeline sidecars embody both flexibility and risk. They can be created quickly to meet the immediate needs of a pipeline, using familiar tools (e.g., Python scripts writing JSON, R scripts writing CSV). They can adapt to project-specific requirements, such as storing additional flags for quality control or experimental parameters. However, their lack of standardization means that **institutional memory** is needed to interpret them, and new team members may struggle to understand the meaning of fields or the implications of certain parameter choices.

Practitioners often express frustration when sidecars must be integrated across projects or institutions. For instance, when combining exposure data from two cohorts that used different geocoding pipelines, the sidecars may use different field names and structures for similar concepts (e.g., address quality scores), making cross-cohort meta-analysis difficult. Vendor lock-in can also arise: if sidecars are tightly coupled to particular commercial tools or cloud platforms, migrating pipelines becomes more complex. Documentation quality varies widely; some projects write extensive documentation for sidecar formats, while others rely on code comments or personal communication.

### Strategies for aligning sidecars with general standards

To improve reproducibility and meta-analysis, schema engineers can design systems that map sidecar contents to general metadata standards. One strategy is to treat sidecars as **raw provenance records** that are transformed into PROV graphs, ISO 19156 observations, or structured metadata fields in RO-Crate. For example, a geocoding sidecar might become a set of PROV activities and entities describing geocoding steps, with ISO 19156 sampling features representing geocoded locations.[15][16] An environmental data download manifest could be mapped to DCAT or STAC dataset descriptions, with provenance indicating which queries were used.[5][11][16]

Another strategy is to define **local profiles** of general standards tailored to exposure pipelines. For instance, an exposure registry might adopt a profile of PROV that specifies required attributes for exposure computations, such as `env:inputDataset`, `env:interpolationMethod`, and `env:timeWindow`, and pipeline sidecars would be expected to include these attributes in a structured way. RO-Crate can be used as a packaging mechanism for sidecars, embedding them in a crate alongside datasets and code, with schema.org markup indicating their roles.[6]

Practitioners emphasize that any such alignment must be realistic: metadata frameworks should not require every exposure pipeline to adopt heavy-weight semantic web technologies, but should provide incremental steps that add value. For example, encouraging pipelines to write structured JSON sidecars with clearly named fields already improves reproducibility; mapping those fields to PROV or ISO models can be done later when needed for meta-analysis. Vendor-neutral formats (e.g., plain JSON and CSV) reduce lock-in.

## Ecosystem Mapping and Interoperability Among Standards

### Crosswalks between geospatial metadata standards

Interoperability among geospatial metadata standards such as ISO 19115/19139, ISO 19156, CF/ACDD, STAC, and DCAT is crucial for environmental-exposure workflows that rely on multiple infrastructures.[2][5][10][11][14][15][19] For example, a climate data center might describe a gridded temperature dataset using CF and ACDD attributes in netCDF files, ISO 19115/19139 metadata in XML, and DCAT entries in a web catalog, while a cloud platform exposes the same dataset via STAC. Crosswalks between these standards allow metadata to be translated or linked, preserving key information across presentations.

One common pattern is to treat ISO 19115/19139 as the **authoritative geospatial metadata**, from which DCAT and schema.org Dataset entries are derived by mapping ISO elements to DCAT properties (e.g., title, description, spatial coverage).[2][11][13][14] CF/ACDD variable names and attributes can be referenced in ISO metadata via feature catalogues or extension elements, and ACDD discovery attributes can be mapped to DCAT and schema.org properties.[10][11][12][13] STAC items can include links to ISO 19115 metadata or carry subsets of metadata in their properties, such as spatial extent and acquisition date.[5]

For exposure science, such crosswalks mean that exposure pipelines can find environmental inputs in multiple catalogues and interpret their metadata consistently. However, crosswalks do not automatically create instance-level provenance, and differences in granularity and semantics can lead to information loss. Schema engineers must carefully design mappings, ensuring that critical attributes for exposure modeling, such as temporal resolution, aggregation method, and uncertainty measures, are preserved.

### Linking cataloguing vocabularies to clinical and exposure models

Linking cataloguing vocabularies like DCAT and schema.org Dataset to clinical and exposure models like OMOP, FHIR, and ISO 19156 requires bridging not just technical formats but conceptual domains. DCAT and schema.org describe datasets as whole objects, while OMOP and FHIR describe patient-level records and observations.[8][9][11][12][20] One approach is to use DCAT and schema.org to describe **cohort-level exposure datasets**—e.g., a dataset of daily exposure values for a cohort—and to reference OMOP or FHIR schemas as part of the dataset’s `dataStructureDefinition` or related documentation. Another approach is to link OMOP tables or FHIR resources to rich metadata documents via identifiers: for instance, an OMOP exposure table could have a column referencing a DCAT-described environmental dataset that served as the input for its exposure computations.

ISO 19156 can serve as a conceptual bridge by modeling exposure observations that are simultaneously environmental and clinical, with features-of-interest being persons and observed properties being exposure metrics.[15] PROV can then tie together environmental dataset entities described via DCAT and clinical entities described via OMOP or FHIR.[16] Schema-on-schema.org guidance can support embedding these links in web-accessible pages, improving discovery of cross-domain exposure data.[13]

Practitioners report that such linking is still rare in practice, mainly due to tooling gaps and disciplinary divides. However, as environmental-exposure registries evolve, building explicit connections between cataloguing vocabularies and clinical models will be essential for meta-analysis across cohorts and data platforms. The metadata registry envisioned by schema engineers could serve as a hub for these links, storing mappings between environmental datasets, exposure computations, and clinical representations.

### Semantic enrichment with controlled vocabularies and ontologies

Although controlled vocabularies and ontologies such as EnvO (Environment Ontology), SWEET, CF Standard Name tables, QUDT (Quantities, Units, Dimensions and Data Types), AGROVOC, GCMD Science Keywords, GeoNames, and others are out-of-scope as **primary metadata standards** in this study, they play an important supporting role in semantic enrichment.[10][19] CF standard names, for example, provide standardized labels for environmental variables like “air_temperature” or “mass_concentration_of_pm2p5_in_air,” enabling consistent interpretation across datasets (CF Standard Name table URL, unverified). QUDT offers formal definitions for units, which can be referenced in metadata schemas to encode units in machine-readable form (http://www.qudt.org, unverified). EnvO and SWEET provide ontologies for environmental entities and processes, which can be used to classify features-of-interest and observed properties in ISO 19156 or FHIR resources (EnvO URL, unverified).

Semantic enrichment using these vocabularies can improve metadata quality and interoperability, particularly when combined with standards like DCAT, schema.org, and PROV. For instance, schema.org `variableMeasured` properties can reference CF standard names or EnvO terms, while DCAT themes can reference GCMD Science Keywords, and ISO 19115 keywords can be drawn from controlled vocabularies.[2][10][11][13][19] However, adopting vocabularies introduces additional governance and implementation issues: practitioners must manage mappings between vocabularies and local codes, and ensure that vocabulary updates are tracked.

In summary, while vocabularies and ontologies are not themselves metadata standards for exposure data, they are important in making metadata **semantically rich and interoperable**, and schema engineers building an exposure registry should plan to integrate them as reference vocabularies rather than as primary metadata schemas.

## Conclusion

### Synthesis of findings

Across geospatial, environmental, clinical, and provenance domains, a rich ecosystem of metadata standards and structured formats exists that can be applied to environmental-exposure data. Geospatial and environmental standards such as ISO 19115/19139, ISO 19156, CF/ACDD, STAC, and DCAT provide robust mechanisms for describing datasets, observations, and assets, and they are maintained by mature governing bodies and communities.[2][5][10][11][14][15][19] Web-centric and citation-oriented standards like schema.org Dataset, science-on-schema.org guidance, DataCite, and Frictionless Data Package support discoverability, citation, and schema-level metadata for datasets, including exposure-related resources.[12][13][17][18] Provenance-focused standards like W3C PROV and packaging frameworks such as RO-Crate enable structured representation of workflows and derivations, offering pathways to instance-level reproducibility when combined with observation models like ISO 19156.[6][15][16] Clinical and public-health data models like OMOP CDM and FHIR provide structured representations of health exposures and observations, and tools such as FHIR PIT demonstrate practical linkage of clinical data with environmental exposures.[8][9][20]

A consistent theme is that most widely adopted standards primarily support **dataset-level discovery and context**, with limited direct support for modeling the derivation of individual exposure values. ISO 19156 and PROV stand out as standards whose conceptual models can reach down to instance-level provenance, but their practical implementation for large exposure datasets is challenging.[15][16] RO-Crate can package such provenance along with datasets and code, but requires adoption of a relatively sophisticated ecosystem.[6] Clinical models capture subject-level exposures but typically without detailed computational provenance linking them to environmental datasets.[8][9][20] Pipeline sidecar formats, although not standardized, often carry crucial information such as geocoding results, environmental dataset download manifests, and model parameter configurations, filling the provenance gap pragmatically but at the cost of interoperability.

The evaluation of the todo.md list confirms that many resources often discussed in environmental and earth-science metadata circles—such as GCMD, EnvThes, GeoNames, and numerous ontologies—are primarily **controlled vocabularies and registries**, not metadata standards, and thus should be treated as semantic enrichment tools rather than core exposure metadata schemas. Schema engineers designing an environmental-exposure metadata registry must therefore focus on integrating general metadata standards (ISO, DCAT, schema.org, PROV, OMOP, FHIR, RO-Crate, Frictionless) while referencing vocabularies and ontologies for semantic clarity.

### Recommendations for schema engineers building an exposure metadata registry

For schema engineers tasked with designing a registry for environmental-exposure metadata, several pragmatic recommendations emerge from this survey. First, adopt **ISO 19115/19139 and CF/ACDD** as foundational standards for describing environmental datasets that serve as inputs to exposure computations, ensuring that spatial and temporal coverage, variable definitions, and data quality are well captured.[2][10][14][19] Second, use **DCAT and schema.org Dataset** (with science-on-schema.org guidance) to expose exposure datasets and environmental inputs via web catalogs and landing pages, facilitating discovery by external users and search engines.[11][12][13] Third, integrate **DataCite DOIs** for citable exposure datasets and code packages, using relation types to connect exposure datasets to underlying environmental data and publications.[17]

Fourth, represent **exposure observations** using **ISO 19156** or an analogous observation data model (e.g., ODM2 for specific domains), particularly for instance-level exposure values that need to be traced back to environmental inputs and computational procedures.[15][18] Fifth, adopt **W3C PROV** as the primary provenance model, designing profiles that specify how exposure computations should be recorded in terms of entities, activities, and agents, and instrument pipelines to produce PROV records.[16] Sixth, consider using **RO-Crate** as a packaging format for exposure research objects, including input datasets, code, configuration, and provenance graphs, enabling reproducible sharing of complex workflows.[6] Seventh, integrate clinical data models such as **OMOP CDM and FHIR** where exposure values must be linked to health outcomes, ensuring that exposure variables are represented in structures compatible with those models.[8][9][20]

Finally, design mechanisms to **ingest pipeline sidecars**—geocoding logs, environmental data manifests, model configuration files—into the registry by mapping their contents to more general standards like PROV and ISO 19156, without requiring researchers to abandon their familiar sidecar practices. This may involve building transformation tools that convert JSON or CSV sidecars into structured provenance and observation metadata. Throughout, leverage controlled vocabularies and ontologies (EnvO, CF standard names, QUDT, GCMD keywords) to provide semantic clarity, but treat them as **reference vocabularies** within metadata fields rather than as primary schemas.[10][19]

### Future directions

Future work in environmental-exposure metadata should aim to develop **domain-specific profiles and implementation guides** that combine the strengths of existing standards into cohesive frameworks tailored to exposure science. For example, an “Exposure PROV Profile” could specify required and recommended provenance attributes for exposure computations, including environmental input identifiers (DCAT or ISO), subject geocoding methods, temporal aggregation logic, and uncertainty modeling, along with examples and tooling. Similarly, an “Exposure Observation Profile” based on ISO 19156 could define standard observed properties (e.g., daily average PM\(_{2.5}\), maximum heat index) and feature-of-interest types (individual, household, neighborhood), with links to controlled vocabularies.[15][16]

On the clinical side, extending **OMOP CDM and FHIR** with standardized environmental-exposure domains and implementation guides could support large-scale linkage of exposures to health data, enabling meta-analysis across cohorts and institutions.[8][9][20] Workflow systems and cloud platforms should integrate provenance standards like PROV and packaging formats like RO-Crate natively, reducing the instrumentation burden on researchers and ensuring that provenance metadata is captured as pipelines run.[6][16] Finally, governance bodies and research consortia should collaborate to produce **operational case studies and post-mortems** that document successes and failures in exposure metadata implementation, informing best practices and highlighting practitioner concerns such as implementation cost, vendor lock-in, and documentation quality.

By grounding future developments in both the conceptual capabilities of standards and the practical realities of exposure pipelines in clinics, public-health agencies, and research groups, the environmental-exposure metadata community can move toward systems that support not only dataset-level discovery but also **robust instance-level reproducibility and meta-analysis**, enabling more reliable and transparent environmental health research.
