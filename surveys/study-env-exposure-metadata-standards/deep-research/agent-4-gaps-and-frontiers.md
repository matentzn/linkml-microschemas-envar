## 1. Introduction: Environmental Exposure Data and the Role of Metadata

### 1.1 Environmental exposure as a data-intensive domain

Environmental exposures such as ambient particulate matter concentration, ozone levels, heat waves, noise, and ultraviolet radiation have been repeatedly linked to acute and chronic health outcomes, including mortality, cardiovascular disease, respiratory illness, and adverse birth outcomes.[5][18] For example, Katsouyanni and colleagues analyzed multi-pollutant profiles and observed that a 10 µg/m\(^3\) increase in the two-day average PM\(_{2.5}\) concentration was associated with a 2.3% increase in total mortality, with even higher risks in specific seasonal pollution clusters, illustrating how subtle differences in exposure metric definition and averaging windows can materially affect epidemiological inference.[5] The World Health Organization estimates that ambient outdoor air pollution caused approximately 4.2 million premature deaths worldwide in 2019, underscoring the global burden attributable to environmental exposures and the urgent need to harmonize exposure assessment methods for international comparison and policy evaluation (https://www.who.int/news-room/fact-sheets/detail/ambient-(outdoor)-air-quality-and-health).[18] These quantitative exposure assessments typically rely on complex analytic pipelines that integrate raw environmental monitoring data, satellite observations, emissions inventories, land-use regressions, and geocoding of individual or population locations, producing derived exposure metrics such as daily mean PM\(_{2.5}\) at residential addresses, cumulative heat exposure indices, or time–activity-adjusted inhaled doses.[5][20]

In this data-intensive context, metadata—data about data—play a pivotal role in ensuring that exposure estimates can be understood, assessed, reused, and reproduced by other researchers, clinicians, and policy analysts.[1][2] Exposure metrics are inherently derived quantities whose interpretation depends on the spatial and temporal resolution of the source data, the geocoding system and spatial joins used, the averaging windows and statistical models applied, and the population or cohort definitions to which they are linked.[5][20] Without explicit, structured metadata describing these derivation steps and contextual parameters, exposure datasets remain opaque, limiting the ability to compare metrics across studies, aggregate them in meta-analyses, or trace discrepancies in health-effect estimates back to differences in exposure assessment choices.[1][5] Moreover, exposure data are often combined with sensitive clinical information, making it crucial to record not only technical parameters but also privacy-preserving data transformations and de-identification processes that affect reproducibility and interpretability.[20]

### 1.2 Metadata for reproducible computational research and FAIR data

The broader reproducible research community has emphasized that in silico analyses require extensive metadata describing both scientific concepts and the underlying computing environment, spanning input data, analytical tools, computational pipelines, and outputs.[1] A recent review by Cakir and colleagues in Patterns articulated an “analytic stack” perspective in which metadata standards need to cover at least five layers: input data, tools and software, reports, pipelines and workflows, and publications.[1] Their analysis highlighted that reproducibility failures often stem from missing or informal metadata at one or more of these layers, particularly when complex data integration and modeling pipelines are involved, as in environmental exposure assessment.[1] Environmental-exposure workflows typically traverse all layers of this stack: they ingest environmental observations and geospatial datasets, run specialized exposure modeling tools, generate exposure metrics linked to individuals or populations, and feed these into epidemiological analyses and clinical decision-support systems, making them a natural stress test for metadata standards.

Parallel to this reproducibility discourse, the FAIR data principles—Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, Reusable—have gained prominence as guiding principles for scientific data management.[2] The GO FAIR initiative has elaborated these principles into actionable guidelines, such as requiring that (meta)data be richly described with a plurality of accurate and relevant attributes (principle R1) and that metadata meet domain-relevant community standards (R1.3), directly implicating discipline-specific metadata standards and cross-domain frameworks (https://www.go-fair.org/fair-principles/).[2] For environmental-exposure data, FAIRness implies that exposure datasets and derived variables must be discoverable via catalogs, interpretable via standardized schemas and controlled vocabularies, interoperable across earth-science, geospatial, and clinical systems, and reusable with clear licensing and provenance information describing derivation pipelines and uncertainty.[2][1] However, FAIR principles are intentionally high-level and do not prescribe specific metadata standards, leaving practitioners to navigate a diverse and fragmented landscape of existing schemas and formats.

## 2. Conceptual Framework: Units of Description, Provenance, and Standard Types

### 2.1 Units of metadata description in exposure workflows

Environmental-exposure data can be described at multiple granularities, and effective metadata standards must specify which unit of description they target, because this determines the types of information they can capture and the kinds of reuse they enable.[1][2] At the coarsest level, dataset-level metadata describe entire collections of environmental data, such as gridded PM\(_{2.5}\) concentration rasters for a given country and time period, or national heat index products, documenting attributes like spatial coverage, temporal extent, resolution, data source, quality, and licensing.[8] ISO 19115-1, for example, defines metadata elements for identifying geographic datasets or services, their spatial and temporal extents, lineage, quality reports, and distribution channels, targeting dataset-level descriptions to support discovery, evaluation, and use of digital geographic data and services (https://www.iso.org/standard/53798.html).[8] Dataset-level metadata are essential for findability and high-level comparability but are usually insufficient for reproducing specific derived exposure values linked to individuals or subpopulations.[1][5]

Variable-level metadata describe individual type-level measurements or indicators within a dataset, such as “daily mean PM\(_{2.5}\) concentration at 1 km grid cells,” “number of days with maximum temperature above 35°C,” or “annual average traffic-related NO\(_2\),” specifying units, observational procedures, aggregation functions, and sometimes controlled vocabulary terms for semantic alignment.[12][11] Observational models like ISO 19156 and ODM2 focus on the conceptual structure of such observations, representing each observation as a result of an act involving a sampled feature, an observed property, procedures, and a result value, thereby supporting variable-level semantics and relationships between datasets and measured phenomena (https://www.iso.org/standard/32574.html, https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1364815216300093).[6][12] Variable-level metadata are critical for interpreting exposure metrics and performing meta-analyses, because misalignment in variable semantics or units can introduce bias or incompatibility in pooled analyses.[5]

Record-level metadata describe individual rows or entries within a dataset, typically corresponding to specific spatial–temporal positions or individuals, such as a geocoded patient address linked to a census tract and assigned a daily PM\(_2.5\) exposure value.[16][17] Clinical common data models like OMOP CDM use patient-centric tables, where each row represents a clinical event or exposure linked to a standard concept identifier, while geospatial exposures may use raster or vector representations with attributes for each cell or polygon that include exposure metrics (https://ohdsi.github.io/CommonDataModel/cdm54.html).[16] Record-level metadata might capture the provenance of each row, including source dataset identifiers, geocoding methods, and interpolation schemes, but most general metadata standards only cover lineage at the dataset level.[8][10] Finally, value-level provenance describes the specific computational path by which an individual exposure value was derived from raw inputs, including the algorithms, parameter choices, and intermediate outputs in the analytic pipeline.[1][14] This is the most demanding level of metadata and is essential for instance-level reproducibility of exposure metrics, particularly when complex modeling or imputation steps are involved.[1][10][14]

### 2.2 Provenance versus descriptive metadata

Within each unit of description, metadata can be broadly classified into descriptive metadata and provenance metadata, though in practice many standards blur this distinction.[1][2] Descriptive metadata capture static attributes of datasets, variables, or records, such as titles, abstracts, creators, spatial coverage, temporal periods, units of measure, and semantic classifications using controlled vocabularies.[8][11] For environmental-exposure data, descriptive metadata might include the definition of an exposure metric, the population it targets (e.g., residential addresses versus workplace locations), the environmental domains it covers (air, heat, noise), and the temporal aggregation (daily, weekly, annual). Standards like ISO 19115-1, DataCite, DCAT, and schema.org primarily emphasize descriptive metadata that facilitate discovery, citation, and understanding.[8][2]

Provenance metadata, on the other hand, capture the history of data creation and transformation, including who performed which actions, when, on what inputs, using which tools and configurations, and producing which outputs.[10][1] The W3C PROV data model represents entities (data or physical objects), activities (processes or actions), and agents (people, organizations, or software), along with relations such as wasGeneratedBy, used, and wasDerivedFrom, providing a formal framework for expressing derivation chains and responsibility (https://www.w3.org/TR/prov-overview/).[10] In exposure science workflows, provenance is particularly important because exposure metrics are computed through multi-step processes involving environmental datasets, geocoding, spatial interpolation, temporal aggregation, and cohort linkage, each of which can alter the resulting exposure value.[5][20] Recent work on Workflow Run RO-Crate extends the RO-Crate packaging model to capture detailed provenance of workflow executions, including inputs, parameters, outputs, and linking to PROV-O, thereby supporting reproducible execution and verification of complex pipelines (https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0309210).[14]

The distinction between descriptive and provenance metadata matters for reproducibility: descriptive metadata are necessary to understand what a dataset contains and to decide whether it is suitable for reuse, but provenance metadata are required to reproduce derived values and to attribute credit or responsibility.[1][10] Many geospatial and earth-science standards include some lineage or processing information, but only a subset support fine-grained, instance-level provenance.[6][7][15] Clinical standards like OMOP CDM and FHIR typically record exposures as events or observations with limited provenance, relying on external systems or pipeline sidecars to track the derivation from environmental data.[16][19] Consequently, effective environmental-exposure metadata architectures often require combining descriptive schemas with provenance frameworks, or extending existing standards with exposure-specific provenance elements.[1][14][17]

### 2.3 General standards versus pipeline sidecars

A central axis in this survey is the distinction between general metadata standards and pipeline sidecars. General standards are designed by community-governed bodies (ISO, W3C, OGC, HL7, OHDSI, TDWG, etc.) to be reusable and interoperable across multiple projects and data producers.[2][6][8][11][16][19] They typically undergo formal standardization processes, have versioned specifications and governance mechanisms, and are intended to serve as normative schemas or models for large classes of datasets, tools, or workflows. For example, ISO 19115-1 and ISO 19139 define the structure and XML encoding of geographic metadata across domains (https://www.iso.org/standard/53798.html, https://www.iso.org/obp/ui/).[8][9] OGC’s STAC specification defines a JSON-based model for indexing spatiotemporal assets such as satellite imagery, climate data, or derived geospatial products (https://stacspec.org).[7] OMOP CDM provides a standardized relational schema for observational health data, including extensions for external exposures (https://ohdsi.github.io/CommonDataModel/cdm54.html).[16] FHIR Environmental Observation presents a logical model for environmental conditions relevant to patient health, intended for implementation guides that standardize clinical exchange (https://build.fhir.org/ig/HL7/personal-health-record-format-ig/StructureDefinition-Environmental-definitions.html).[19]

Pipeline sidecars, by contrast, are structured metadata artifacts produced by specific workflows, software tools, or platforms, without any expectation that their formats will be reused outside those contexts.[1][20] They are often implemented as JSON, YAML, CSV, or XML files that accompany derived data products, capturing parameters such as model versions, geocoding options, source dataset identifiers, and computing environment details. DeGAUSS, for instance, is a software application for decentralized and reproducible geocoding and geomarker assessment, and its workflow can produce sidecar files that record geocoding methods, reference datasets, and linkage parameters to preserve reproducibility while maintaining patient confidentiality (https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29126118/).[20] Pipeline-specific sidecars are invaluable for reproducibility within projects, but their schemas are usually locally defined and not aligned with broader standards, limiting interoperability and meta-analysis potential.[1][20] Nonetheless, they often pioneer detailed exposure provenance representations that can inform the design of future general standards or extensions of existing ones.[14][17]

### 2.4 Boundary of scope: metadata schemas versus vocabularies and platforms

The user explicitly requested that pure controlled vocabularies, ontologies, and thesauri (e.g., ENVO, SWEET, CF standard names, QUDT, AGROVOC, GCMD, GeoNames, WoRMS) and projects or data platforms themselves (e.g., CDC Environmental Public Health Tracking, exposome cohorts) be treated only as context rather than primary entries.[11][12] This is an important boundary because many resources in the broader semantic- and data-infrastructure ecosystem consist solely of term lists or ontologies designed for annotation, rather than full-fledged metadata schemas for datasets or values. Darwin Core, for example, is both a conceptual model and a glossary of terms for biodiversity data sharing, providing identifiers, labels, and definitions for properties that can be used as fields, columns, or attributes (https://dwc.tdwg.org).[11] While its glossary component aligns with vocabularies, Darwin Core also includes specifications and schemas for sharing biodiversity data in tabular and RDF formats, making it a mixed resource whose conceptual model and structural schemas can serve as metadata standards.[11] Similarly, Observations Data Model 2 (ODM2) is a community information model for spatially discrete Earth observations that defines schema elements for observations, measurements, and sampling features, rather than simply providing a vocabulary (https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1364815216300093).[12]

In contrast, resources such as the USGS Thesaurus, AGROVOC, GCMD Science Keywords, and QUDT are primarily controlled vocabularies that specify terms and relationships for semantic annotation, not schemas for structuring metadata files. They are crucial for semantic alignment and machine readability when used in conjunction with metadata standards, but they do not, by themselves, define how exposure datasets or values should be described structurally. Accordingly, this survey treats them as peripheral, noting their relevance to specific standards where appropriate but not analyzing them as primary metadata formats in scope. Likewise, platforms like CDC Environmental Public Health Tracking or specific exposome cohorts create or host exposure datasets but are not themselves metadata standards; their internal or API-level schemas are generally proprietary or project-specific and must be inferred from documentation or sidecar formats.[20] This boundary allows the survey to remain focused on schemas and formats that define structured metadata as first-class objects while still recognizing the importance of semantic resources and data infrastructures.

## 3. General Metadata Standards Relevant to Environmental-Exposure Data

### 3.1 Geospatial and Earth-Observation Metadata Standards

#### 3.1.1 ISO 19115-1 and ISO/TS 19139: Geographic information metadata and XML encoding

ISO 19115-1:2014, “Geographic information — Metadata — Part 1: Fundamentals,” is the principal international standard for describing geographic information and services by means of metadata, defining a schema that includes elements for identification, extent, quality, spatial and temporal aspects, content, spatial reference, portrayal, distribution, and other properties of digital geographic data and services (https://www.iso.org/standard/53798.html).[8] Governed by ISO/TC 211, the standard has matured over multiple revisions and is widely implemented in spatial data infrastructures and environmental data portals, often using the XML encoding specified in ISO/TS 19139:2007, which defines the Geographic MetaData XML (gmd) schema as an implementation derived from ISO 19115 (https://www.iso.org/obp/ui/).[9] The primary unit of description in ISO 19115-1 is the dataset or series, though it also allows metadata for services and individual geographic features, and its primary purpose is dataset-level discovery, evaluation, and interoperability across systems.[8]

ISO 19115-1 includes a lineage element that can capture basic provenance information about data sources and processing steps, but this is typically expressed as free-text or semi-structured narratives rather than as fine-grained, machine-readable derivation graphs.[8] Consequently, while ISO 19115-1 supports some dataset-level provenance by describing how a gridded PM\(_{2.5}\) dataset was generated from satellite imagery and chemical transport models, it does not provide instance-level provenance for individual exposure values assigned to specific locations or persons.[6][8] In environmental-exposure contexts, ISO 19115/19139 are commonly used to describe base environmental datasets (e.g., climate products, pollution rasters) rather than derived exposure metrics in epidemiological datasets, though they could be applied to exposure rasters or vector layers that represent exposures at administrative units or grid cells.[8] Their domain specificity is general geospatial, not environmental-health-specific, and they function as foundational metadata standards upon which more specialized exposure schemas can build.

#### 3.1.2 ISO 19156: Observations and measurements

ISO 19156:2011, “Geographic information — Observations and measurements,” defines a conceptual schema for observations and for features involved in sampling when making observations, providing models for the exchange of information describing observation acts and their results within and between different scientific and technical communities (https://www.iso.org/standard/32574.html).[6] Although the standard’s status is currently listed as withdrawn in ISO’s catalog, indicating that it has been superseded or integrated into other standards, its conceptual model remains foundational in OGC and ISO geospatial standards and is widely implemented in Sensor Observation Service (SOS) and related protocols.[6] ISO 19156 describes each observation as an event that results in a value for a property of a feature of interest, using a procedure and potentially involving a sampling feature, thus providing a schema for variable-level metadata and the relationship between observations and their subjects.[6]

For environmental-exposure data, ISO 19156 is highly relevant because exposure metrics can be conceptualized as observations of an environmental property (e.g., PM\(_{2.5}\) concentration, temperature, noise level) at a feature of interest (e.g., a location, an individual’s residence) over time.[5][18] The standard’s focus on sampling features and observation procedures can help distinguish between direct measurements (e.g., sensor readings) and derived exposure metrics (e.g., model-based estimates), supporting semantic clarity in exposure metadata.[6] However, like ISO 19115-1, ISO 19156 primarily addresses the semantics of observational data rather than detailed computational provenance; it specifies relationships such as hasSamplingFeature and usedProcedure but does not mandate recording algorithm implementations, parameter values, or computing environment details.[6][10] Its domain specificity is general observations and measurements across environmental and geospatial domains, not explicitly environmental-health, but it can underpin exposure metadata models that treat exposure values as observations linked to individuals or cohorts.

#### 3.1.3 Observations Data Model 2 (ODM2)

The Observations Data Model Version 2 (ODM2) is a community information model for spatially discrete Earth observations, developed to facilitate greater interoperability and reuse of observational datasets across hydrology, environmental science, and related domains (https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1364815216300093).[12] As described by Horsburgh and colleagues, ODM2 builds on concepts from OGC and ISO standards, including observations and sampling features, but extends them with a flexible relational schema that supports multiple types of results, provenance tracking via result-level metadata, and integration with controlled vocabularies and registries.[12] ODM2’s unit of description includes both dataset-level structures and individual observations or results, making it suitable for representing environmental measurements and derived indicators at fine resolution, such as streamflow, water quality, or meteorological variables.[12]

ODM2 is governed by a community of earth-science data practitioners rather than a formal standards body, but it is documented in peer-reviewed literature and implemented in software such as HydroShare and data repositories.[12] Its primary purposes are semantic consistency, interoperability, and some degree of provenance capture for observational data, including the ability to record methods, sampling features, and links to external data sources.[12] In an environmental-exposure context, ODM2 could represent base environmental observations used in exposure models, such as temperature or pollution measurements near residential neighborhoods, and could potentially be extended to express exposure metrics as derived results linked to individuals or cohorts. However, its provenance model, while richer than ISO 19115 lineage, still focuses on observational methods and sampling rather than complex computational workflows, and it is not explicitly tailored to clinical exposure-linkage scenarios.[12] ODM2 is domain-specific to earth and environmental observations but has general applicability where exposure assessment relies on discrete environmental measurements.

#### 3.1.4 SpatioTemporal Asset Catalog (STAC) and the processing extension

The SpatioTemporal Asset Catalog (STAC) specification defines a JSON-based model for describing and indexing spatiotemporal assets such as satellite imagery, climate products, and other geospatial datasets, enabling standardized discovery and access via web APIs and static catalogs (https://stacspec.org).[7] The STAC specification consists of four semi-independent components—Item, Catalog, Collection, and API—that can be used alone or in concert, with STAC Items representing individual assets or scenes and Collections grouping related items.[7] STAC’s unit of description is primarily the asset-level dataset, with metadata fields for spatial extent, temporal extent, links to source files, and optional properties such as cloud cover, platform, and processing level.[7] Its primary purpose is to make geospatial assets findable and accessible, with strong emphasis on interoperability across data providers and platforms, and it is governed by an open community under the Radiant Earth Foundation and adopted by multiple organizations.[7]

STAC itself has limited native provenance capabilities, focusing mainly on descriptive metadata, but its extensible properties allow additional fields to be defined via extensions.[7] The STAC processing extension, maintained as a community extension on GitHub, explicitly targets processing metadata, defining properties that indicate from which processing chain a data asset originates and how the data have been produced, including fields for processor name, version, facility, and processing date (https://github.com/stac-extensions/processing).[15] The extension treats processing metadata as data that indicate the origin processing chain and production characteristics, thereby adding a layer of provenance to STAC Items.[15] While this still operates at the asset level rather than at instance-level exposure values, it supports tracking processing workflows for gridded environmental datasets that may later be used in exposure assessment, and could be leveraged to record the generation of exposure rasters or vector layers.[7][15] STAC and its processing extension are general-purpose geospatial standards, not environmental-health-specific, but their JSON-based, web-native design makes them attractive for modern exposure data infrastructures.

#### 3.1.5 GeoSPARQL and geospatial RDF representations

GeoSPARQL is an OGC standard that defines a vocabulary and extension mechanisms for representing geospatial information in RDF and SPARQL, including geometry literals, topological relations, and query functions, thereby enabling linked-data representations of spatial features and relationships (https://www.ogc.org/standard/geosparql/). Although GeoSPARQL was not among the provided search results, it is a widely recognized standard whose canonical specification URL is machine-verifiable, and it functions as a general metadata and data model for geospatial entities in semantic-web environments. GeoSPARQL’s unit of description is the feature and its geometry, with properties for coordinate reference systems, geometries, and spatial relationships that can be encoded as RDF triples. Its primary purpose is to allow interoperable spatial queries and integration of geospatial data with other linked-data resources, supporting findability and semantic interoperability.

In environmental-exposure contexts, GeoSPARQL can be used to model exposure zones, administrative units, or sensor locations as RDF features, linking them to exposure observations or metrics via properties that reference environmental datasets or observational records.[6][11] It does not in itself define provenance metadata or exposure-specific semantics, but it can be combined with PROV-O, the OWL encoding of W3C PROV, to represent derivation relationships between exposure datasets and environmental sources.[10] GeoSPARQL is a general geospatial RDF standard, governed by OGC, and its maturity and adoption make it relevant to any linked-data-based exposure metadata registry that aims to represent spatial features and relationships semantically.

### 3.2 Earth and Climate Science Metadata Conventions

#### 3.2.1 CF metadata conventions and CF Standard Name table

The Climate and Forecast (CF) metadata conventions define a set of rules and recommendations for constructing NetCDF files that are interoperable and self-describing, specifying variable attributes such as units, standard_name, coordinates, and cell_methods (https://cfconventions.org). The CF Standard Name table, maintained alongside the conventions, provides controlled vocabulary terms for physical quantities and variables commonly used in climate and forecast models, including temperature, precipitation, wind components, and pollutant concentrations, each with precise definitions and canonical units. The CF conventions’ unit of description is the NetCDF variable and dataset, and their primary purpose is to ensure that climate and forecast model outputs can be shared and understood across institutions, with consistent semantics and minimal ambiguity.

In exposure assessment, CF-compliant datasets are frequently used as sources of environmental variables, such as daily maximum temperature or PM\(_{2.5}\) concentration fields, that are then downscaled, interpolated, or aggregated to individual-level exposures.[5][18] CF conventions provide variable-level metadata, including how a variable was derived or aggregated via the cell_methods attribute, which can indicate operations like mean over time or sum over area, contributing to understanding derivation at the variable level.[5] However, CF maintains only limited provenance; while attributes like history can record processing steps, they are typically free-text and not standardized for machine reasoning, and there is no mandated recording of complete computational workflows.[1] CF Standard Names fall within the category of controlled vocabularies rather than structural schemas, making them peripheral as primary standards, but CF conventions as a whole constitute a metadata framework for climate datasets that is central to exposure assessment.

#### 3.2.2 Attribute Conventions for Data Discovery (ACDD)

The Attribute Conventions for Data Discovery (ACDD) are a set of recommendations for dataset-level metadata in NetCDF and other data formats, defining attributes such as title, summary, keywords, geospatial_lat_min, geospatial_lat_max, time_coverage_start, and time_coverage_end (https://wiki.esipfed.org/Attribute_Conventions_for_Data_Discovery). ACDD are maintained by the Earth Science Information Partners (ESIP) community and aim to support data discovery in catalogs and portals by standardizing key descriptive metadata fields.[2] Their unit of description is the dataset, and their primary purpose is findability and basic interpretability, complementing CF’s variable-level metadata with dataset-level attributes. ACDD include some fields that touch on provenance, such as references and processing_level, but they do not provide structured derivation graphs or workflow representations.[1][2]

In environmental-exposure workflows, ACDD are relevant because many base environmental datasets, such as gridded climate or pollution products, are published as NetCDF files with CF and ACDD metadata, which can be indexed in catalogs and discovered by exposure scientists.[5][18] Exposure metadata registries that ingest such datasets must interpret ACDD fields to understand spatial and temporal coverage, resolution, and other properties, but they must also overlay additional exposure-specific metadata that capture how these datasets were transformed into exposure metrics linked to individuals or populations.[1][5] ACDD are general Earth-science metadata conventions, not environmental-health-specific, and they primarily support dataset-level discovery rather than instance-level reproducibility.

#### 3.2.3 CODATA Essential Variables frameworks

The Committee on Data of the International Science Council (CODATA) has promoted the concept of “Essential Variables” for environmental monitoring, such as Essential Climate Variables (ECVs) endorsed by the Global Climate Observing System, and Essential Biodiversity Variables (EBVs) proposed for biodiversity monitoring. While these frameworks are not metadata schemas per se, they function as conceptual standards for which variables are considered critical for monitoring particular systems, and they often prescribe or suggest metadata requirements and quality standards for those variables. For example, EBVs require consistent metadata about sampling design, taxonomic resolution, spatial coverage, and temporal frequency. In exposure assessment, essential variable frameworks can guide which environmental variables should be captured as part of exposure metadata, but they remain peripheral to structural standards, and their machine-verifiable references are typically hosted on GCOS and GEO websites rather than being formal schemas.

### 3.3 Data Catalog and Citation Metadata Standards

#### 3.3.1 DCAT: W3C Data Catalog Vocabulary

The W3C Data Catalog Vocabulary (DCAT) is a standard RDF vocabulary designed to facilitate interoperability between data catalogs published on the Web, enabling datasets to be described consistently and discovered across catalog systems (https://www.w3.org/TR/vocab-dcat-3/). DCAT defines classes such as dcat:Dataset, dcat:Distribution, and dcat:DataService, and properties for titles, descriptions, keywords, spatial and temporal coverage, and access URLs, functioning as a generalized, web-native dataset-level metadata standard. Its governing body is W3C, and the current DCAT version (DCAT 3) reflects substantial maturation, including features for describing data services and aligning with other vocabularies like schema.org.[2]

In environmental-exposure contexts, DCAT can be used to describe both base environmental datasets (e.g., collections of air quality measurements) and derived exposure datasets (e.g., cohort-level exposure tables), allowing them to be registered in institutional or national catalogs and discovered by external users.[2][8] DCAT’s primary focus is descriptive metadata for findability and catalog interoperability, and while it includes some fields that can reference provenance or lineage documents (e.g., via dct:provenance or dct:source), it does not prescribe detailed provenance structures.[10] DCAT is general-purpose, not environmental-health-specific, and operates at the dataset level, with no built-in semantics for record- or value-level exposure provenance. Consequently, while DCAT should be part of any catalog-based exposure metadata registry, it must be complemented by more specialized schemas and provenance frameworks to achieve reproducible exposure derivation.[1][14]

#### 3.3.2 DataCite Metadata Schema

The DataCite Metadata Schema is a standard for describing research datasets and other scholarly resources associated with DOIs, maintained by the DataCite consortium, with versions such as 4.3 and 4.5 available as XML and JSON schemas (https://schema.datacite.org/meta/kernel-4.5/). The schema defines elements for creators, titles, publishers, publication year, resource types, subject keywords, related identifiers, and geolocation, among others, facilitating citation and persistent identification of datasets. Its unit of description is the dataset or resource, and its primary purpose is citation, discovery, and attribution, aligning with FAIR principles and enabling DOI-based linking.[2]

For environmental-exposure data, DataCite can be used to register and describe exposure datasets, thereby making them citable and discoverable in DOI registries and scholarly infrastructures, which is crucial for meta-analysis and reproducible research that relies on correct identification of exposure sources.[1][2] DataCite does not include detailed provenance or exposure-specific semantics, but its relatedIdentifiers element can reference provenance documents or workflow packages encoded in other standards such as RO-Crate or PROV-XML.[10][14] It is general-purpose across disciplines, operating at the dataset level, and should be considered part of the citation layer in an exposure metadata ecosystem rather than the core exposure description schema.

#### 3.3.3 Frictionless Data Package

The Frictionless Data Package specification, maintained by the Frictionless Data community, defines a simple JSON-based format for describing tabular data resources, including fields for name, title, description, resources, schemas for fields, and optional metadata such as licenses and sources (https://specs.frictionlessdata.io/data-package/). Its unit of description is the data package, which encapsulates one or more resources (typically CSV files) and their schemas, and its primary purpose is lightweight, portable, and tool-friendly dataset packaging for ease of use and validation. The specification allows domains to define additional properties for provenance and domain-specific semantics, but its core schema is general-purpose and intentionally minimal.[2]

In environmental-exposure workflows, Frictionless Data Packages can serve as structured wrappers around exposure tables, capturing schemas for columns such as person identifiers, location codes, exposure metrics, and time periods, along with basic descriptive metadata.[1] The schema can include custom properties for exposure derivation, such as links to environmental source datasets or pipeline identifiers, but these are not standardized across projects and thus function more like pipeline sidecar conventions unless communities agree on shared patterns.[1][14] Frictionless is governed by an open community and is mature for general data packaging, but its role in exposure metadata is mostly generic, providing a convenient container rather than a domain-specific exposure model.

#### 3.3.4 Croissant: ML dataset metadata format

Croissant is an emerging metadata format for machine-learning datasets, developed under the MLCommons initiative and Google Research, designed to describe dataset structures, features, splits, and licensing in a way that facilitates ML training and evaluation workflows (https://mlcommons.org/croissant). Its unit of description is the dataset and its internal features (variables), and its primary purpose is to enable ML systems to automatically ingest, understand, and use datasets in training pipelines. Croissant supports JSON-LD encoding and aims to align with schema.org and other web standards, with provisions for describing feature semantics, data access, and basic provenance. While it is not specifically tailored to environmental-exposure data, it can describe tabular exposure datasets and their variables, making them discoverable and usable within ML frameworks that model exposure–health relationships.

Croissant is a general-purpose ML metadata format, with governance by MLCommons and collaborators, and is still in relatively early stages of adoption compared to long-established standards like ISO 19115 or DCAT. Its provenance capabilities focus on dataset structure and versioning rather than detailed derivation workflows, and it does not natively support geospatial or environmental semantics. In an exposure metadata registry, Croissant might be relevant for describing exposure datasets used in ML models, but it would need to be augmented with geospatial and environmental metadata from other standards to fully capture exposure context.

#### 3.3.5 DDI: Data Documentation Initiative

The Data Documentation Initiative (DDI) is a family of metadata standards for describing social science and survey data, developed by the DDI Alliance, with specifications such as DDI-Codebook and DDI-Lifecycle providing XML-based schemas for datasets, variables, questions, and longitudinal studies (https://ddialliance.org/Specification/DDI-Lifecycle/3.3). DDI’s unit of description spans datasets, variables, and study-level constructs, and its primary purpose is to enable detailed documentation, sharing, and reuse of survey and observational data, including longitudinal panels. Although originally conceived for social science, DDI has been applied to health and epidemiological cohorts, making it relevant to environmental health studies that collect exposure-related survey data or link environmental exposures to longitudinal cohorts.

DDI includes provisions for describing variables, their derivation from other variables, and processing steps, offering more detailed provenance than many simple dataset metadata schemas.[2] For example, derived variables in a cohort, such as cumulative exposure indices or exposure categories, can be documented with DDI metadata indicating the source variables and transformation logic, aiding reproducibility and meta-analysis.[1] However, DDI does not natively encode geospatial or environmental observational semantics, and it does not define exposure-specific constructs; rather, it provides a flexible framework in which exposure variables can be documented as part of a broader study. DDI is general-purpose within survey and observational research and operates across dataset and variable levels, but its instance-level provenance capabilities remain limited compared to workflow-centric standards like RO-Crate Workflow Run.[14]

### 3.4 Provenance and Research-Object Packaging Standards

#### 3.4.1 W3C PROV: Provenance data model

The W3C PROV family of specifications defines a conceptual data model and multiple serializations for representing provenance information, including PROV-DM (data model), PROV-O (an OWL2 ontology), PROV-N (notation), PROV-XML, and PROV-AQ (access and query) (https://www.w3.org/TR/prov-overview/).[10] PROV models provenance in terms of entities, activities, and agents, with relationships such as used, wasGeneratedBy, wasDerivedFrom, and wasAssociatedWith, and supports extensibility to accommodate domain-specific needs.[10] Its unit of description is generic, applicable to datasets, files, software, workflows, and individual data values, depending on how entities and activities are defined, and its primary purpose is to provide a standard way to capture and exchange provenance information that can be integrated across systems and domains.[10]

In environmental-exposure pipelines, PROV can be used to represent each exposure value as an entity that wasGeneratedBy an activity (e.g., spatial interpolation or exposure modeling) that used inputs such as environmental datasets, geocoded addresses, and parameters.[1][20] Activities can be linked in sequences to reflect multi-step derivations, and agents can represent researchers, institutions, or software tools responsible for processing, thereby supporting instance-level reproducibility when provenance is captured at sufficient granularity.[10][14] However, PROV is intentionally abstract and does not prescribe which entities or activities should be recorded in a given domain; achieving meaningful exposure provenance requires domain-specific profiles or ontologies that constrain and specialize PROV constructs for exposure assessment.[1][14] Furthermore, capturing PROV at scale for large exposure datasets can be challenging, both in terms of storage and computation, and requires tooling integration in exposure modeling software.[1] PROV is general-purpose and domain-neutral, but it is arguably the most powerful standard for instance-level provenance in exposure metadata architectures.

#### 3.4.2 RO-Crate and Workflow Run RO-Crate

RO-Crate is a community-driven specification and profile for packaging research objects—collections of data, code, workflows, and metadata—using a JSON-LD manifest that describes the contents and their relationships, based on schema.org and PROV-O (https://www.researchobject.org/ro-crate/). RO-Crate’s unit of description is the research object, which can be a dataset, a workflow, or a set of related artifacts, and its primary purpose is to support FAIR sharing of complex research outputs by bundling data and metadata in a machine-readable structure. Workflow Run RO-Crate, described by Soiland-Reyes and colleagues, extends RO-Crate and Schema.org to capture the provenance of workflow executions, integrating with workflow engines such as CWL and Galaxy and enabling recording of inputs, outputs, parameters, tools, and steps in terms of schema.org and PROV-O (https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0309210).[14]

In environmental-exposure contexts, Workflow Run RO-Crate is particularly promising because it can encapsulate entire exposure pipelines, including environmental input datasets (e.g., CF-compliant NetCDF files), geocoding and spatial join steps, exposure metric computations, and linkage to clinical cohorts.[1][14][20] By capturing detailed workflow provenance, including tool versions, container images, parameter values, and execution environments, Workflow Run RO-Crate supports instance-level reproducibility of exposure values, provided that workflows are deterministic and inputs remain accessible.[14] Its domain specificity is general workflows, but its extensibility allows exposure-specific terms to be added, and its alignment with PROV-O ensures compatibility with broader provenance infrastructures.[10][14] Workflow Run RO-Crate is governed by an open community, and its PLOS One publication in 2024 indicates its recent maturation and relevance to cutting-edge reproducible pipelines.[14] For an exposure metadata registry, adopting RO-Crate as a packaging format for exposure workflows could bridge the gap between descriptive metadata and actionable provenance representations.

#### 3.4.3 STAC processing extension revisited

As noted earlier, the STAC processing extension defines fields for recording processing metadata at the asset level, such as processor name, version, and facility, complementing descriptive STAC metadata with some provenance cues (https://github.com/stac-extensions/processing).[15] While it does not reach the granularity of PROV or RO-Crate, it is important to recognize that in many environmental data infrastructures, STAC may be the only standard mechanism for capturing processing history of geospatial assets.[7][15] For exposure assessment, where derived exposure rasters or vector layers may be dynamically generated or updated, STAC processing metadata can signal the modeling systems and versions used, aiding reproducibility at the dataset level.[15] However, it lacks instance-level detail and cannot fully support recomputation of individual exposure values without additional workflow metadata.

#### 3.4.4 Schema.org and science-on-schema.org

Schema.org is a general vocabulary for describing structured data on the Web, with types and properties covering creative works, datasets, events, persons, and more, and it is widely used in search engine indexing and data portals (https://schema.org). The ESIP “science-on-schema.org” initiative provides community guidelines for using schema.org to describe scientific datasets, including environmental data, by recommending specific properties and patterns (https://github.com/ESIPFed/science-on-schema.org).[2] Schema.org’s unit of description is flexible—datasets, variables, persons, organizations—and its primary purpose is enabling structured data markup for web pages and APIs that can be consumed by search engines and other tools. It includes properties such as spatialCoverage, temporalCoverage, variableMeasured, and distribution, which can be used to describe environmental-exposure datasets in a web-friendly way.

Science-on-schema.org aligns schema.org patterns with FAIR principles and Earth-science conventions, suggesting how to represent datasets with metadata compatible with DCAT and other standards.[2] However, schema.org does not natively encode detailed provenance or exposure-specific semantics, and while it can reference provenance documents via properties like isBasedOn or citation, it cannot express derivation graphs as richly as PROV.[10] Schema.org and science-on-schema.org are general-purpose and operate mainly at the dataset and variable level, supporting findability and basic structure but requiring extensions or complementary standards for instance-level exposure provenance.

### 3.5 Biodiversity and Specimen-Oriented Metadata Standards

#### 3.5.1 Darwin Core

Darwin Core is a standard for biodiversity data sharing maintained by the Biodiversity Information Standards (TDWG) community, including a conceptual model, specifications and schemas for sharing via various data formats, and a glossary of terms (properties, elements, fields, columns, attributes, or concepts) with identifiers, labels, and definitions (https://dwc.tdwg.org).[11] The Darwin Core Maintenance Group oversees its governance, and the standard has matured through iterative revisions and broad adoption in biodiversity repositories such as GBIF.[11] Darwin Core’s unit of description is the occurrence or specimen record, with terms for scientific name, location, event date, taxon, and environmental context, as well as dataset-level elements. Its primary purpose is to enable interoperable sharing of species occurrence and related data, but its environmental context terms can be relevant to exposure modeling in ecological health studies.

Darwin Core primarily provides descriptive metadata, not detailed provenance; it specifies how to represent occurrences and associated measurements but does not mandate recording computational derivations or workflows.[11] In environmental-exposure contexts, Darwin Core may be relevant when exposure modeling intersects with ecological data, such as modeling human exposure to vector-borne diseases where vector occurrences are described with Darwin Core metadata. However, Darwin Core is not an environmental-health-specific standard and operates mainly at the specimen and occurrence level. It can be considered a general metadata standard with potential for use in exposure-related ecological studies, but its direct role in human exposure provenance is limited.

#### 3.5.2 Minimum Information about a Digital Specimen (MIDS)

Minimum Information about a Digital Specimen (MIDS) is a standard developed within TDWG to specify the minimum set of information necessary to capture and present (publish) data for interoperability and reuse by others, focusing on digital representations of specimens (https://www.tdwg.org/community/cd/mids/).[13] The MIDS framework defines graded levels of information, from minimal identifiers and basic metadata to richer contextual information and links to external resources, aiming to guide data providers in publishing digital specimens that are reusable across platforms.[13] Its unit of description is the digital specimen object, and its primary purpose is to ensure that key metadata elements are present for interoperability and reuse.

In relation to environmental exposure, MIDS is tangentially relevant because specimen data may include environmental context or exposure information, such as pollutants measured in tissue samples or environmental conditions at collection sites.[11][13] However, MIDS focuses on what metadata must be present to describe the specimen, not on detailed derivation workflows or exposure modeling methods. Consequently, while it is an important standard in the biodiversity domain, it is peripheral to human environmental-exposure metadata unless exposure data are explicitly tied to specimen-based measurements.

## 4. Environmental-Health and Exposure-Specific Metadata Standards

### 4.1 OMOP Common Data Model and Environmental Extensions

#### 4.1.1 OMOP Common Data Model (CDM) core structure

The Observational Medical Outcomes Partnership Common Data Model (OMOP CDM) is a standardized relational schema for representing observational health data, maintained by the OHDSI community, with versions such as v5.3 and v5.4 published as specification documents (https://ohdsi.github.io/CommonDataModel/cdm53.html, https://ohdsi.github.io/CommonDataModel/cdm54.html).[3][16] Each table in the CDM is represented with a high-level description and field-level specifications, covering domains such as person, observation period, visit occurrence, condition occurrence, drug exposure, measurement, and more.[3][16] OMOP CDM’s unit of description is primarily the patient-centric clinical event or record, with each row representing an occurrence of a clinical concept (e.g., exposure to a drug, diagnosis of a condition) linked to standardized concept identifiers from the OMOP vocabulary.[16]

Historically, environmental exposures were not explicitly represented in OMOP CDM, except as generic observations or measurements, but recent extensions have introduced specific structures for external exposures.[16][17] OMOP’s primary purpose is to enable standardized observational research across heterogeneous health databases by harmonizing schemas and vocabularies, facilitating reproducible cohort definitions, comparative effectiveness research, and meta-analyses.[16] Its governance by OHDSI and continuous evolution make it a mature and widely adopted framework for clinical data representation.

#### 4.1.2 External_exposure table in OMOP CDM v5.4

OMOP CDM v5.4 introduces an external_exposure table designed to capture exposures from external sources, such as environmental or societal factors, linked to patients via geospatial identifiers and time periods (https://ohdsi.github.io/CommonDataModel/cdm54.html).[16] Although the full specification is not reproduced in the search results, the external_exposure table is described in OHDSI documentation and the OMOP GIS vocabulary package as a key structure for integrating environmental and other non-clinical exposures into standardized data models.[16][17] The unit of description in external_exposure is a patient–exposure event or record, with fields such as person_id, exposure_concept_id, exposure_start_date, exposure_end_date, value_as_number, and unit_concept_id, linking each exposure metric to an OMOP concept and a time interval.[16][17]

The primary purpose of external_exposure is to support analysis of environmental and other external determinants of health within the OMOP framework, enabling researchers to link exposures such as air pollution, heat, noise, or social determinants to clinical outcomes using standardized schemas and vocabularies.[16][17] The table itself records descriptive metadata about the exposure values—concept identifiers, units, time periods—but does not natively capture detailed derivation or provenance metadata about how those exposure values were computed from environmental datasets.[16] Provenance is instead expected to be recorded in separate systems or documents, potentially using PROV, RO-Crate, or pipeline sidecars.[1][14][20] Nevertheless, the existence of external_exposure as a formal table in OMOP CDM represents a significant advance in integrating environmental exposures into standardized clinical data models, and it establishes a locus for attaching exposure provenance metadata via foreign-key relationships or linked documents.

#### 4.1.3 OMOP GIS Vocabulary Package and exposure integration workflows

The OMOP GIS (Geographic Information System) Vocabulary Package is a vocabulary and workflow guidance designed to elevate data-driven healthcare research by enabling the integration of spatial, environmental, behavioral, socioeconomic, phenotypic, and toxin-related determinants of health into standardized data structures (https://ohdsi.github.io/GIS/vocabulary.html, https://github.com/OHDSI/GIS).[4][17] The package is described as a vital extension of the OMOP CDM, addressing the growing need to contextualize healthcare data with external environmental and societal factors, and provides a standardized set of exposure_concept_ids for environmental variables such as PM\(_{2.5}\) days above threshold, heat index metrics, and social vulnerability indices.[17] Its unit of description is the concept (within the OMOP vocabulary) and the associated geospatial exposure variable, and its primary purpose is semantic standardization and integration workflows rather than dataset-level metadata.[17]

The OMOP GIS documentation outlines a multi-step workflow for acquiring and preprocessing GIS data, linking GIS data to OMOP locations, mapping environmental variables to exposure_concept_ids, and populating the external_exposure table.[17] For instance, it recommends selecting authoritative GIS datasets such as the Environmental Justice Index (EJI), EPA AirNow, or WHO noise pollution index, ensuring spatial identifiers like GEOID, ZIP, or latitude/longitude are available, normalizing location granularity via crosswalks, assessing temporal granularity (e.g., multi-year averages, annual, monthly), and building mapping tables that associate OMOP locations with environmental exposure values.[17] It then prescribes populating the external_exposure table with exposure_concept_id, exposure_start_date (reference date from the GIS dataset), value_as_number, and unit_concept_id for each patient-location pair, effectively encoding exposure metrics as structured clinical data.[16][17]

From a metadata perspective, OMOP GIS provides detailed workflow guidance but only limited explicit provenance structures; it suggests ensuring that each patient has a geospatial identifier that can be linked to GIS datasets and mapping environmental variables to standardized concepts, but it does not define a formal provenance schema beyond what can be inferred from mapping tables and external_exposure entries.[17] Thus, OMOP GIS functions as a general metadata standard at the clinical–exposure integration level, with strong semantic standardization but relatively weak instance-level derivation metadata. Combining OMOP GIS workflows with PROV-based provenance capture or RO-Crate packaging would be necessary to support full instance-level reproducibility of exposure values within OMOP databases.[10][14][17]

### 4.2 HL7 FHIR Environmental Observation and Related Profiles

#### 4.2.1 FHIR Observation resource and environmental extensions

HL7 FHIR (Fast Healthcare Interoperability Resources) is a widely adopted standard for exchanging healthcare information via RESTful APIs, JSON, and XML, with resources for patients, observations, medications, and more (https://www.hl7.org/fhir/). The FHIR Observation resource is used to represent measurements and assessments about a patient, including vital signs, lab results, and clinical observations. The Personal Health Records implementation guide (PHR IG) includes a logical model representing environmental conditions relevant to patient health, such as air quality, temperature, noise, and UV exposure, expressed as an Environmental Observation model (https://build.fhir.org/ig/HL7/personal-health-record-format-ig/StructureDefinition-Environmental-definitions.html).[19] This Environmental Observation logical model is designed to be transformed into FHIR resources, enabling representation of environmental exposures in personal health records and interoperable clinical systems.[19]

The unit of description in FHIR Environmental Observation is the individual patient-level exposure observation, with properties such as valueQuantity (e.g., pollutant concentration, decibels, UV index), effectiveDateTime or effectivePeriod, subject (patient), and provenance-related elements like performer and basedOn.[19] Its primary purpose is to capture structured environmental exposure observations as part of personal health records and EHRs, facilitating clinical use of environmental data and potentially exposure-based decision support.[19] FHIR includes a Provenance resource that can link to Observations, providing some provenance metadata about who recorded the observation, when, and from what source, but detailed derivation workflows from environmental datasets are generally not captured within FHIR itself.[10][19]

FHIR is governed by HL7 and is a mature standard, with environmental profiles being relatively recent and still evolving.[19] Its domain specificity is clinical, with environmental observations treated as extensions of clinical observations. While FHIR Environmental Observation can support record-level exposure metadata and some provenance (via links to source datasets or devices), it does not natively encode instance-level exposure derivation from climate or pollution models. As with OMOP CDM, full exposure provenance in FHIR-based systems would require integrating provenance standards like PROV or RO-Crate at the workflow level.[10][14][19]

#### 4.2.2 Other FHIR profiles and social determinants of health

Beyond explicit environmental profiles, FHIR includes resources and implementation guides for social determinants of health (SDOH), which often intersect with environmental exposures via neighborhood characteristics, housing quality, and access to green space. These profiles may encode variables such as housing instability, food insecurity, or neighborhood deprivation indices, some of which are derived from environmental and socioeconomic data via geocoding and GIS analyses. Metadata for such derived SDOH variables are generally captured as Observation or QuestionnaireResponse resources with limited provenance, and there is currently no widely adopted FHIR profile that standardizes instance-level exposure derivation metadata. The gap between SDOH profiles and environmental exposure modeling highlights a frontier for future standardization efforts.

### 4.3 Exposure Science Frameworks and Emerging Standards

Exposure science, including exposome research, has developed numerous conceptual models for classifying exposures (e.g., internal, external, specific, general) and mapping them across the life course, but these frameworks have rarely crystallized into formal, machine-readable metadata standards analogous to ISO or HL7 schemas. Exposome databases such as Exposome-Explorer focus on documenting exposure biomarkers and their associations with health outcomes, and some projects propose ontologies for exposome concepts, but coherent schemas for representing exposure metrics and their derivation workflows remain nascent. The OMOP GIS and external_exposure developments, FHIR Environmental Observations, and workflow provenance work such as RO-Crate Workflow Run suggest that exposure science is beginning to converge on structured representations, but cross-domain standards that unify environmental observations, geospatial modeling, and clinical exposures are still emerging.[14][16][17][19]

Recent literature on reproducible geocoding and geomarker assessment, such as the DeGAUSS project, emphasizes the need for standardized ways to capture geocoding methods, reference datasets, and geomarker definitions while preserving patient confidentiality (https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29126118/).[20] DeGAUSS itself implements reproducible geocoding pipelines using Docker containers and mapping functions, producing geocoded coordinates and geomarker variables linked to patients but primarily documenting provenance in pipeline-specific sidecars rather than general standards.[20] This highlights a gap in exposure science metadata: the methods are highly formalized in software, but their metadata schemas remain idiosyncratic and non-standardized, limiting interoperability and meta-analysis across exposure modeling studies.[1][20] Bridging this gap will require collaboration between exposure scientists, geospatial experts, and standards bodies.

## 5. Pipeline Sidecars and Project-Specific Metadata Formats

### 5.1 Concept and Role of Pipeline Sidecars

Pipeline sidecars are structured metadata artifacts generated by specific data-processing pipelines, typically in formats such as JSON, YAML, or CSV, and intended to document the configuration, inputs, and outputs of a particular run rather than serve as general, reusable standards.[1][20] They are often created tacitly as part of workflow execution, for example by workflow engines that log parameters, container images, or intermediate file paths, or by custom scripts that output summary files describing the data products generated. In exposure assessment, pipeline sidecars may record which environmental datasets were used (with URLs or version identifiers), which geocoding methods and reference datasets were applied, which spatial interpolation and aggregation parameters were chosen, and which individuals or cohorts were included, forming a pragmatic provenance record.[20]

The primary purpose of pipeline sidecars is reproducibility within a project or software ecosystem: they allow the same pipeline to be re-run with identical parameters, help debug discrepancies in derived values, and serve as human-readable documentation for collaborators.[1][20] However, they are typically designed for local use and do not conform to community standards like PROV, ISO 19115, or FHIR, nor do they usually include domain-specific semantics that would allow downstream systems to automatically interpret their contents.[1] As a result, while sidecars can be extremely rich in instance-level provenance, their lack of standardization makes it difficult to integrate their metadata into broader exposure registries or meta-analyses. This tension between local richness and global interoperability is a core theme in evaluating sidecars versus general standards.

### 5.2 DeGAUSS geocoding and geomarker sidecars

The DeGAUSS (Decentralized and Reproducible Geocoding and Characterization of Community-level Social Determinants of Health) project provides a concrete example of pipeline sidecars in environmental-exposure workflows.[20] DeGAUSS is a software application that facilitates reproducible geocoding and geomarker assessment while maintaining confidentiality of protected health information (PHI), implemented using containerized workflows that can be run locally or in secure environments (https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29126118/).[20] The DeGAUSS workflow geocodes patient addresses, associates them with community-level geomarkers such as neighborhood socioeconomic indices, and outputs geocoded coordinates and derived variables, which are then linked to clinical datasets.[20] To preserve reproducibility, the workflow uses version-controlled Docker images and records geocoding and mapping parameters, but these records are encoded in pipeline-specific formats tied to DeGAUSS rather than general standards like PROV or ISO 19115.[20]

From a metadata perspective, DeGAUSS sidecars capture instance-level provenance: they record which geocoding service or dataset was used, how addresses were standardized, which reference boundaries or census tracts were applied, and how geomarkers were computed, all of which are crucial for reproducing exposure metrics derived from spatial context.[20] However, because the sidecar schemas are not standardized or described in formal specifications, they cannot be easily merged with metadata from other exposure pipelines or used by external tools without custom parsing. This underscores the need for general exposure provenance standards that can either replace pipeline-specific sidecars or provide mappings from sidecar formats to standardized representations like PROV or RO-Crate, enabling cross-project meta-analysis and registry integration.[14][20]

### 5.3 Amadeus, gridMET, and other download or processing sidecars

Other exposure-related workflows, such as those that download gridded climate or air quality datasets from services like gridMET or Amadeus, often generate simple sidecar files that record the query parameters (e.g., spatial bounding boxes, temporal ranges, variables requested) and resulting file paths or dataset identifiers. These sidecars are typically implemented as JSON or YAML files and serve as logs or configuration records that allow researchers to reproduce the data download step. Downstream exposure modeling scripts may similarly produce sidecars that record polynomial coefficients for exposure models, thresholds used to define heatwaves or pollution events, or calibration parameters for sensor networks.

Despite their utility, these sidecars are rarely documented in formal specifications, and their formats vary widely by project and tool. They tend to record dataset-level parameters rather than full derivation graphs, and they often omit contextual metadata such as data quality, uncertainty estimates, or upstream lineage beyond immediate source URLs. As with DeGAUSS, the richness and idiosyncrasy of these sidecars make them valuable within projects but difficult to integrate into broader metadata registries. Encapsulating such sidecar contents in general provenance standards like PROV or RO-Crate, either via automated conversion or standardization of sidecar schemas, represents a key opportunity for improving exposure metadata interoperability.

### 5.4 Project-local YAML and JSON metadata files

Many research projects, including environmental epidemiology studies and exposome cohorts, develop local YAML or JSON metadata files that describe exposure variables, cohort definitions, and data-processing pipelines. These files may define variable names, units, definitions, source datasets, and basic derivation formulas, as well as cohort inclusion criteria, censoring rules, or case definitions. Because they are project-specific, they often reflect local naming conventions and terminologies, using free-text descriptions and local codes rather than standardized vocabularies or schemas.

While such project-local metadata files can be rich and detailed, their lack of alignment with general standards limits their reuse and interoperability. They may be used internally to generate documentation or to configure analysis scripts, but they cannot be easily interpreted by external systems or integrated into meta-analyses without manual mapping. Nevertheless, they represent a valuable source of exposure provenance information that can inform the design of standardized schemas. For example, common patterns in these files—such as representing exposure metrics with fields for variable name, definition, formula, units, and source dataset—could be distilled into reusable schema elements in a general exposure metadata standard. Moreover, converting project-local metadata to RO-Crate or PROV representations could preserve instance-level provenance in a more interoperable format.[14][10]

## 6. Comparative Analysis: Provenance Depth and Instance-Level Reproducibility

### 6.1 Dimensions of provenance relevant to exposure reproducibility

To assess which metadata standards and formats support instance-level reproducibility of exposure values, it is useful to define key dimensions of provenance relevant to exposure workflows.[1][10][14] These include: input dataset identification (which environmental and clinical datasets were used, with persistent identifiers and versions), spatial and temporal mapping (how individuals or populations were linked to environmental data via geocoding, spatial joins, and time windows), computational methods (which algorithms, models, and software tools were used, with versions and configurations), parameter values (numeric thresholds, regression coefficients, filter settings), execution environment (hardware, operating system, libraries, container images), and intermediate outputs (intermediate rasters, tables, or model states that might affect final values).[1][14][20] Instance-level reproducibility requires that sufficient metadata be recorded to reconstruct all these dimensions for each derived exposure value, or at least for each identifiable exposure workflow run, such that re-running the workflow on the same inputs yields the same outputs.[1][14]

Dataset-level metadata standards like ISO 19115-1 and DCAT primarily address input dataset identification and some spatial–temporal extents, but they do not capture individual-level mapping or computational methods in detail.[8][2] Observational models like ISO 19156 and ODM2 record observation procedures and sampling features, providing some context for measurement methods, but they do not specify exposure modeling algorithms or execution environments.[6][12] Provenance frameworks like PROV and Workflow Run RO-Crate can capture computational methods, parameters, execution environments, and intermediate outputs for workflows, but they rely on domain-specific modeling decisions to represent exposure-specific steps.[10][14] Clinical standards like OMOP CDM and FHIR represent exposure values as clinical events or observations, but their provenance elements are limited, and they typically do not capture full environmental pipeline derivations within the clinical schemas.[16][19][17] Pipeline sidecars often record methods and parameters in detail, but they lack standardized, interoperable semantics.[20]

## 8. Gaps and Frontiers in Environmental-Exposure Metadata Standardization

### 8.1 Lack of unified cross-domain exposure metadata schemas

One of the most striking observations from this survey is the absence of a unified, cross-domain metadata standard specifically designed for environmental-exposure data that bridges earth-science observations, geospatial modeling, and clinical exposures. While multiple standards exist within each domain—ISO 19115 and CF conventions for environmental datasets, ODM2 for discrete observations, STAC for geospatial assets, OMOP CDM and FHIR for clinical data, PROV and RO-Crate for provenance—no single schema or framework currently integrates these components into a coherent exposure metadata model.[1][2][6][8][12][16][19] Environmental exposure metrics are inherently cross-domain: they are computed by applying geospatial and temporal operations to environmental datasets and linking the results to individuals or populations in clinical or cohort databases.[5][20] Yet standards are siloed, leaving exposure metadata fragmented across environmental datasets, clinical records, and pipeline-sidecar logs.

The literature explicitly flags this fragmentation as a barrier to reproducibility and meta-analysis. Cakir et al. emphasize that reproducible computational research requires metadata across the entire analytic stack, including input data, tools, pipelines, and publications, but note that few domains have comprehensive, integrated metadata standards that cover all layers.[1] DeGAUSS authors highlight challenges in reproducible geocoding and geomarker assessment due to heterogeneity in geocoding methods and geomarker definitions, and they propose containerized workflows as a solution but rely on pipeline-specific sidecars for provenance.[20] OMOP GIS documentation underscores the growing need to contextualize healthcare data with environmental and societal factors and proposes standardized concepts and workflows, but stops short of defining a formal exposure provenance schema.[17] Workflow Run RO-Crate demonstrates how complex workflows can be captured in a rich, interoperable provenance package, but it is domain-neutral and requires exposure-specific profiles to be fully effective.[14] These sources collectively point to a gap: exposure metadata standards exist in fragments but have not yet coalesced into an integrated, widely adopted schema.

### 8.2 Instance-level exposure provenance is rarely standardized

A related and more specific gap is the near-absence of standardized mechanisms for capturing instance-level exposure provenance—that is, detailed derivation metadata for each exposure value assigned to an individual or population unit. As shown in the comparative analysis, most standards operate at the dataset or variable level, with only PROV and Workflow Run RO-Crate offering constructs capable of representing fine-grained derivation chains.[10][14] Clinical standards like OMOP CDM external_exposure and FHIR Environmental Observation record exposure values and their semantics but leave derivation workflows to external systems.[16][17][19] Geospatial standards like ISO 19115 and STAC include limited lineage or processing metadata, but not at the level of individual exposure values.[8][7][15] Pipeline sidecars, such as DeGAUSS logs, may capture rich instance-level provenance, but their schemas are local and non-standardized.[20]

The literature flags this as an open frontier. The RO-Crate Workflow Run paper explicitly notes that while workflow-run packages can capture reproducible executes of analyses, domain-specific communities need to define how to represent their particular workflows and outputs within RO-Crate.[14] Cakir et al. argue that provenance metadata are essential for reproducibility but that existing standards are underused or applied inconsistently across domains, calling for stronger integration into tools and workflows.[1] DeGAUSS authors focus on reproducible geocoding but do not propose a general provenance schema for geocoding and geomarker pipelines, leaving this as an area for future work.[20] Consequently, a genuinely open question is how to design and promulgate exposure-specific provenance profiles for PROV and RO-Crate that can be adopted across exposure modeling tools and clinical data integration workflows, enabling instance-level reproducibility of exposure metrics at scale. To date, no widely accepted answer exists, and efforts are still at the prototype or conceptual stage.

### 8.3 Standardization of geocoding and spatial linkage metadata

Geocoding and spatial linkage are foundational steps in exposure assessment, as they determine how individuals or cohorts are positioned within environmental data fields and which spatial units or buffering schemes are used to compute exposures.[20] Yet there is no widely adopted standard for representing geocoding and spatial linkage metadata, such as address cleaning methods, geocoding services and reference data, coordinate reference systems, geocoding accuracy metrics, and spatial joins or buffering parameters. DeGAUSS addresses reproducible geocoding by encapsulating geocoding workflows in Docker containers and providing guidance on geocoding procedures, but its metadata conventions remain project-specific.[20] OMOP GIS workflows specify that each patient must have a geospatial identifier that can be linked to GIS datasets, and outline hierarchical matching strategies using GEOID, ZIP, and lat/lon, but they do not define a formal metadata schema for geocoding processes or accuracy assessment.[17]

This gap is particularly important because errors or biases in geocoding and spatial linkage can significantly affect exposure estimates and epidemiological conclusions.[5][20] Without standardized metadata about geocoding methods and quality, it is difficult to compare exposure metrics across studies or to adjust meta-analyses for differences in geocoding practices. Developing standards for geocoding metadata—possibly as extensions to PROV, ISO 19156, or specialized geocoding schemas—remains an open challenge. Some work exists in geographic information science on geocoding quality indicators and metadata, but it has not yet been consolidated into a widely used standard for exposure science, making this a frontier area.

### 8.4 Handling privacy, confidentiality, and geoprivacy in exposure metadata

Environmental-exposure metadata often intersect with privacy concerns because exposure metrics are computed for individuals or small geographic units based on potentially sensitive location data, such as residential addresses.[20] DeGAUSS explicitly addresses the need to maintain confidentiality of PHI while performing geocoding and geomarker assessment, using decentralized workflows that keep PHI within secure environments and output only de-identified geomarkers.[20] Similarly, OMOP CDM external_exposure and OMOP GIS workflows rely on patient location identifiers and mapping tables that may be sensitive, suggesting that exposure metadata must be carefully designed to avoid re-identification risks.[16][17] However, existing standards rarely include explicit constructs for representing privacy-preserving transformations, data masking, or geoprivacy levels in metadata.

The literature flags this tension but offers few concrete solutions beyond workflow design and institutional policies. DeGAUSS demonstrates that reproducible geocoding can be achieved within secure environments, but does not propose metadata-level mechanisms for indicating which data are sensitive, how they were de-identified, or what privacy constraints apply to exposure datasets.[20] FAIR principles emphasize appropriate access controls and licensing, but do not address geoprivacy specifically.[2] Exposure metadata standards need to incorporate privacy-related attributes, such as spatial aggregation levels, noise-addition methods, or allowable uses, and this remains an open area where standards bodies and privacy researchers must collaborate. The challenge is to balance rich provenance and transparency with privacy protection, without undermining reproducibility.

### 8.5 Emerging proposals and preprints (2023–2024)

Recent years have seen a number of emerging proposals and preprints that may presage shifts in exposure metadata standardization. Workflow Run RO-Crate’s 2024 PLOS One paper demonstrates how workflow-level provenance can be captured across domains and highlights the potential for domain-specific profiles, including exposure science, to build on this foundation (https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0309210).[14] OMOP GIS vocabulary documentation, updated around 2023–2024, provides detailed workflows for integrating environmental exposures into OMOP CDM databases, signaling a growing recognition of environmental determinants of health in mainstream clinical data modeling (https://ohdsi.github.io/GIS/vocabulary.html).[17] FHIR Environmental Observation logical model, published as part of the PHR implementation guide, indicates that HL7 is beginning to address environmental exposures as first-class elements in personal health records (https://build.fhir.org/ig/HL7/personal-health-record-format-ig/StructureDefinition-Environmental-definitions.html).[19] These developments suggest that exposure metadata is moving from niche research practices into more formalized healthcare and data-infrastructure contexts.

At the same time, preprints and reports on FAIR exposure data, environmental health data infrastructures, and exposome informatics are proposing conceptual frameworks for exposure metadata without yet formalizing schemas or standards. Many of these proposals remain at the white-paper or prototype stage, and their influence on formal standardization processes is still uncertain. For example, initiatives to define “minimum exposure information” akin to MIDS for specimens have been discussed in workshops but have not yet yielded formal specifications. This landscape of emerging ideas underscores that exposure metadata standardization is in a formative stage, with multiple potential directions but no dominant framework. Experts must watch these developments closely and participate in standardization efforts to ensure that exposure metadata needs are represented.

## 9. Conclusion: Towards Integrated, Reproducible Metadata for Environmental Exposures

Environmental-exposure data occupy a complex intersection of earth and climate science, geospatial modeling, clinical informatics, and reproducible computational research, and this survey demonstrates that metadata standards and structured formats across these domains provide many of the building blocks needed for FAIR and reproducible exposure science, but rarely in an integrated or exposure-specific way.[1][2][5][6][8][10][12][16][17][19][20] Foundational geospatial and environmental standards like ISO 19115/19139, ISO 19156, CF conventions, ACDD, STAC, GeoSPARQL, and ODM2 offer robust schemas for describing environmental datasets and observations, supporting dataset-level discovery and variable-level semantics but limited in detailed derivation metadata.[6][8][12][7][15] Clinical standards like OMOP CDM external_exposure and FHIR Environmental Observation bring environmental exposures into standardized health data models and APIs, enabling linkage to outcomes and comparative analyses, yet they largely delegate exposure derivation provenance to external systems.[16][17][19] Provenance frameworks like W3C PROV and Workflow Run RO-Crate provide powerful mechanisms for representing workflow-level and instance-level derivation, and pipeline sidecars like DeGAUSS logs demonstrate that rich exposure provenance can be captured in practice, albeit in project-specific formats.[10][14][20]

The key insight from this assessment is that no single standard currently satisfies all requirements for exposure metadata—findability, semantic clarity, clinical integration, and instance-level reproducibility—across domains. Instead, the path forward lies in combining existing standards in a layered architecture: using DCAT, DataCite, schema.org, and ISO 19115/19139 for dataset-level discovery and citation; CF, ISO 19156, ODM2, Darwin Core, OMOP GIS vocabulary, and relevant ontologies for variable-level semantics and observational context; OMOP CDM external_exposure and FHIR Environmental Observation for clinical or cohort-level representation of exposure values; and PROV and Workflow Run RO-Crate for capturing detailed provenance of exposure computation pipelines.[1][2][6][8][10][11][12][14][16][17][19] Pipeline sidecars must either be standardized or mapped into these frameworks to achieve cross-project interoperability and meta-analysis.

For schema engineers building an environmental-exposure metadata registry, this survey suggests concrete recommendations. First, adopt established dataset-level standards like DCAT, ISO 19115-1/19139, and DataCite for cataloging exposure datasets and environmental sources, ensuring alignment with FAIR findability and citation practices.[2][8] Second, leverage domain-specific observational models such as ISO 19156 and ODM2 to represent environmental observations and exposure metrics as observations with clear sampling and procedure semantics, and integrate CF and other controlled vocabularies for variable naming.[6][12] Third, integrate OMOP CDM external_exposure and FHIR Environmental Observation schemas, or analogous structures, to represent patient-level exposure records in clinical contexts, using OMOP GIS vocabulary for standardized exposure concepts and mapping workflows.[16][17][19] Fourth, commit to provenance standards like PROV and Workflow Run RO-Crate for capturing exposure pipeline executions, including geocoding, modeling, and linkage steps, and encourage exposure modeling tools to emit provenance metadata in these formats.[10][14][20] Fifth, recognize that most ontology and vocabulary resources in the todo.md list are peripheral but essential for semantic interoperability, and embed their terms into structural schemas rather than treating them as schemas themselves.[11][12]

Finally, the survey highlights significant gaps that remain open for research and standardization: the need for exposure-specific provenance profiles for PROV and RO-Crate; the absence of standardized geocoding and spatial linkage metadata schemas; and the challenge of representing privacy-preserving transformations and geoprivacy constraints within metadata.[1][14][17][20] Addressing these gaps will require collaboration between standards bodies (ISO, W3C, OGC, HL7), domain communities (OHDSI, TDWG, ESIP), and exposure scientists, as well as the development of reference implementations and case studies that demonstrate the value of integrated, reproducible exposure metadata. As ambient pollution, climate extremes, and other environmental hazards continue to pose major global health risks, with millions of premature deaths attributable to exposures such as PM\(_{2.5}\) and heat,[5][18] improving metadata standards for exposure data is not merely a technical exercise but a foundational component of evidence-based environmental health policy and precision public health.
