## 1. Background and Problem Framing

### 1.1 Environmental-exposure data and their scientific uses

Environmental-exposure data describe the intensity, duration, and spatiotemporal pattern of contact between humans or populations and environmental agents such as ambient air pollutants, temperature extremes, noise, sunlight, and chemical contaminants, and they are increasingly central to environmental epidemiology and exposome research.[20](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1257643/) These data may arise from direct personal monitoring, such as wearable sensors measuring temperature and fine particulate matter, or from indirect exposure assessment, such as assigning modeled air-quality fields to geocoded residential addresses using spatial interpolation or land-use regression models.[20](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1257643/) In public health surveillance systems like the U.S. Centers for Disease Control and Prevention’s Environmental Public Health Tracking (EPHT) Network, exposure data are typically generated by combining environmental measurements from monitoring networks with demographic and health outcome data to support population-level analyses.[1](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18849771/) Exposure metrics are also increasingly derived from remote sensing products, climate reanalyses, and high-resolution weather models, which supply gridded environmental fields that must be intersected with human activity and residence patterns.[2](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13022413/)

In environmental epidemiology, exposure data are linked to clinical and health outcome datasets to quantify associations between environmental risk factors and disease incidence, hospital admissions, or mortality, often using complex time-series, case-crossover, or spatial regression designs.[20](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1257643/) These linkages require detailed information on exposure variables, including units, spatial and temporal resolution, exposure definitions (e.g., daily mean versus maximum), and the algorithms used for exposure assignment, all of which must be adequately documented in metadata to enable reproducibility and meta-analysis. Exposure science as a discipline has emphasized the need to track both external exposure (concentrations in air, water, soil, and food) and internal dose (biomarkers, such as pollutant concentrations in blood or urine), further complicating metadata requirements because both environmental measurements and human biological measurements must be aligned in time, space, and analytic protocols.[20](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1257643/)

Environmental-exposure data are inherently heterogeneous across domains: air-quality datasets follow conventions from atmospheric sciences, climate-exposure datasets adopt geospatial and climate-model metadata practices, and clinical datasets adopt healthcare standards that may only loosely describe environmental factors.[2](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13022413/)[7](https://www.iso.org/standard/53798.html) This heterogeneity becomes a barrier for data reuse and multi-study meta-analysis, because exposure variables may be labeled differently, units may vary, and the derivation of exposure measures may not be sufficiently transparent to allow harmonization across studies. For example, efforts to build harmonized global air-quality monitoring metadata have revealed substantial variation in how station types, siting characteristics, and measurement protocols are documented, complicating attempts to pool data across national and regional networks.[2](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13022413/) Similar challenges emerge when aggregating heat-exposure metrics based on different definitions of heat waves, or when pooling wildfire-smoke exposure estimates derived from different satellite products and modeling chains, underscoring the critical role of metadata standards in exposure science.

### 1.2 The role of metadata for reuse, reproducibility, and meta-analysis

Metadata, understood as structured information describing datasets, variables, and derived values, play several distinct roles in the lifecycle of environmental-exposure data: they support discovery and access, enable correct interpretation, underwrite reproducibility of exposure derivation, and facilitate pooling and meta-analysis across studies.[4](https://schema.org/Dataset)[11](https://schema.datacite.org) At the discovery level, catalog metadata standards such as DCAT, DataCite, and schema.org Dataset allow exposure datasets to be indexed by data catalogs and general-purpose search engines, making them findable as required by FAIR principles and improving visibility for potential reusers.[3](https://semiceu.github.io/DCAT-AP-reuse-guidelines/)[5](https://github.com/esipfed/science-on-schema.org) At the interpretive level, variable-level metadata standards and conventions such as the Climate and Forecast (CF) standard names and Attribute Convention for Data Discovery (ACDD) contribute semantics about the physical quantities, units, and spatial-temporal coverage represented in exposure datasets, helping analysts understand what exposure measures mean and how they can be compared.[10](https://wiki.esipfed.org/Attribute_Convention_for_Data_Discovery_1-3)

Reproducibility of exposure derivation, however, demands deeper metadata, including provenance information about data sources, algorithms, parameters, and software environments used to compute exposure values from raw environmental and geocoded input data.[9](http://www.opengeospatial.org/standards/om) Provenance models such as W3C PROV-O and workflow packaging standards like RO-Crate provide structures to record these derivation chains, allowing future analysts to reconstruct exposure assessments, verify them, or adapt them to new populations.[17](https://www.researchobject.org/ro-crate/specification/1.1/index.html) In environmental epidemiology, instance-level provenance is particularly important, because exposure measures are assigned to individual observations of health outcomes (such as patient admissions or cohort participants), and subtle changes in exposure assignment algorithms—like different buffer sizes around addresses or different lag windows—can materially affect effect estimates and meta-analytic conclusions.[20](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1257643/)

Meta-analysis of exposure–health associations across studies requires metadata that support harmonization of exposure variables, including mappings between different pollutant names, units, aggregation schemes, and exposure windows, as well as clear documentation of station siting and monitoring protocols that affect comparability.[2](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13022413/) Without standardized metadata, meta-analysts often resort to manual coding of study characteristics, which is labor-intensive and error-prone and may fail to capture critical details about exposure derivation. Emerging efforts such as harmonized air-quality station metadata and standardized environmental public health tracking metadata indicate the feasibility of constructing structured metadata schemas that span datasets from multiple producers, but they also reveal the complexity of reconciling different legacy practices and national standards.[1](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18849771/)[2](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13022413/)

### 1.3 Research questions and scope of this review

Against this backdrop, the present review addresses five central questions that are particularly relevant to schema engineers designing an environmental-exposure metadata registry. First, it asks what metadata standards and structured formats exist for environmental-exposure data across earth and climate science, geospatial and OGC communities, clinical and health informatics, provenance and FAIR-packaging ecosystems, and exposure science itself.[7](https://www.iso.org/standard/53798.html)[9](http://www.opengeospatial.org/standards/om)[12](https://www.ohdsi.org/data-standardization/) Second, for each identified standard or format, it characterizes the governing body and maturity, the unit it describes (dataset, variable type, geocoded row, produced exposure value, or file), its primary purpose (discovery, semantic definition, computation, reproducibility and pooling, or citation), whether it supports derivation and provenance metadata, its domain specificity (environmental-health specific versus general-purpose), and its canonical specification URL.[3](https://semiceu.github.io/DCAT-AP-reuse-guidelines/)[11](https://schema.datacite.org)[17](https://www.researchobject.org/ro-crate/specification/1.1/index.html)

Third, the review distinguishes general standards intended to be reusable and interoperable across data producers and domains from pipeline sidecars, defined here as structured but pipeline-specific documentation formats that serve to record metadata within particular analytic workflows but are not designed as broadly adopted standards.[10](https://wiki.esipfed.org/Attribute_Convention_for_Data_Discovery_1-3)[17](https://www.researchobject.org/ro-crate/specification/1.1/index.html) Fourth, it evaluates which standards carry sufficient provenance and derivation detail to support instance-level reproducibility of produced exposure values (that is, the ability to recompute exposure assignments for specific individuals or records), and which stop at dataset-level discovery and interpretation, thereby limiting reproducibility to high-level characteristics rather than specific values.[9](http://www.opengeospatial.org/standards/om)[15](https://build.fhir.org/ig/HL7/fhir-us-phpl/artifacts.html) Finally, it systematically assesses a curated list of candidate vocabularies, ontology registries, and domain infrastructures—such as GCMD, AGROVOC, QUDT, GeoNames, and Darwin Core—to determine whether they fall within the scope of exposure metadata standards, whether they are peripheral supporting resources, or whether they should be considered out of scope as pure controlled vocabularies, providing a one-line justification for each classification.[18](https://www.qudt.org/2.1/catalog/qudt-catalog.html)[19](http://www.ukoln.ac.uk/metadata/rslp/schema/)

The scope of the review deliberately excludes pure controlled vocabularies, ontologies, and thesauri—such as the Environment Ontology (ENVO), QUDT units ontologies, GCMD Science Keywords, GeoNames, and WoRMS—from being treated as primary metadata standards, although these resources are frequently referenced as value sets or semantic anchors within metadata schemas.[18](https://www.qudt.org/2.1/catalog/qudt-catalog.html) Similarly, specific data platforms and projects, such as the CDC EPHT Network or particular exposome cohorts, are considered as users or exemplars of metadata standards rather than standards themselves.[1](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18849771/) This distinction allows the review to focus on standards whose explicit purpose is to structure metadata for datasets, variables, and derived values, while still acknowledging the critical role of vocabularies and repositories in enabling semantic interoperability and discovery.

## 2. Conceptual Taxonomy for Evaluating Exposure Metadata Standards

### 2.1 Defining metadata standards, structured formats, and pipeline sidecars

To reason systematically about the landscape of metadata standards, it is useful to define several key concepts that will be used throughout this review. A metadata standard is understood here as a schema, specification, or data model formally documented and maintained by a governing body, such as a standards organization, consortium, or community working group, whose explicit purpose is to define the structure and semantics of metadata describing datasets, variables, files, observations, or derived values.[7](https://www.iso.org/standard/53798.html)[12](https://www.ohdsi.org/data-standardization/) Examples include ISO 19115 for geospatial metadata, the DataCite Metadata Schema for dataset citation metadata, and the DCAT vocabulary for data catalogs, all of which are accompanied by published specifications, versioning, and implementation guidance.[3](https://semiceu.github.io/DCAT-AP-reuse-guidelines/)[11](https://schema.datacite.org)

A structured-metadata format refers more broadly to any machine-readable representation of metadata, such as JSON, XML, RDF, or relational schemas, that impose structure on metadata fields even if they are not formally standardized across communities.[8](https://www.iso.org/obp/ui/) Some structured formats begin as project-specific solutions and later evolve into de facto standards when widely adopted, while others remain local to particular pipelines or repositories. Pipeline sidecars, in this framing, are structured metadata files or tables that accompany analytic workflows—such as geocoding pipelines or climate data extraction workflows—and document details such as input file paths, parameter values, algorithm versions, and processing dates, but that are neither governed by formal standards bodies nor intended for broad reuse outside the originating pipeline.[10](https://wiki.esipfed.org/Attribute_Convention_for_Data_Discovery_1-3) Examples include YAML or JSON files encoding configuration and processing history for exposure assignment scripts, or sidecar CSV files recording geocoding precision and data sources for each address in a cohort, which are vital for reproducibility within a project but typically lack standardized semantics beyond that context.

This review treats formally documented schemas such as DCAT, ISO 19115, OGC Observations and Measurements, OMOP CDM, FHIR, W3C PROV-O, RO-Crate, and ACDD as metadata standards because they are governed and versioned, while treating pipeline sidecars as structured formats whose semantics may be informally defined within project documentation or code repositories.[7](https://www.iso.org/standard/53798.html)[9](http://www.opengeospatial.org/standards/om)[12](https://www.ohdsi.org/data-standardization/)[15](https://build.fhir.org/ig/HL7/fhir-us-phpl/artifacts.html)[17](https://www.researchobject.org/ro-crate/specification/1.1/index.html) This distinction matters for schema engineers: general standards can be reused and extended to build interoperable registries, whereas pipeline sidecars must be abstracted and mapped into more general schemas if their content is to be made interoperable and discoverable beyond the pipeline where they originated.

### 2.2 Units of description: dataset, variable, geocoded row, value, and file

Environmental-exposure metadata can describe different granular units, and the adequacy of a standard for reproducibility depends heavily on which units it addresses. At the coarsest level, dataset-level metadata describe entire datasets, such as a gridded temperature reanalysis product, a collection of air-monitor measurements, or a cohort exposure dataset, including information on title, creators, spatial and temporal coverage, access conditions, and high-level descriptions of content.[7](https://www.iso.org/standard/53798.html)[11](https://schema.datacite.org) Standards such as DataCite, DCAT, ISO 19115, RSLP Collection Description, and schema.org Dataset primarily operate at this level, facilitating discovery and citation but generally not describing individual variables or observations in detail.[3](https://semiceu.github.io/DCAT-AP-reuse-guidelines/)[4](https://schema.org/Dataset)[19](http://www.ukoln.ac.uk/metadata/rslp/schema/)

Variable-level metadata describe specific measured or modeled quantities within datasets, such as daily mean \(\mathrm{PM_{2.5}}\) concentration, maximum daily temperature, ozone mixing ratio, or a composite heat-exposure index, and they often include units, standardized names, and semantic links to physical concepts.[10](https://wiki.esipfed.org/Attribute_Convention_for_Data_Discovery_1-3) Conventions such as the CF Standard Name Table and ACDD provide variable-level semantics for netCDF datasets, while ontologies like the Scientific Variables Ontology (SVO) and ENVO supply controlled terms to label environmental variables, though these vocabularies themselves are out of scope as metadata standards by the criteria of this review.[10](https://wiki.esipfed.org/Attribute_Convention_for_Data_Discovery_1-3)[18](https://www.qudt.org/2.1/catalog/qudt-catalog.html) Variable-level metadata are essential for harmonizing exposure variables across studies, yet they rarely capture how individual exposure values were computed.

Geocoded-row metadata describe records representing individual locations or individuals with associated geospatial coordinates, such as cohort participants with geocoded residential addresses, census tracts, or monitoring stations, and they may include information on geocoding precision, source datasets, and linkage keys.[2](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13022413/) Exposure assignments often attach environmental values to these geocoded rows, making metadata about location quality and linkage methods critical for reproducibility. Value-level or instance-level metadata describe individual exposure values assigned to specific records at specific times, such as an estimate of daily \(\mathrm{PM_{2.5}}\) exposure for a particular patient on a particular date, including potentially the algorithm, data sources, and parameters used in the computation.[9](http://www.opengeospatial.org/standards/om)[15](https://build.fhir.org/ig/HL7/fhir-us-phpl/artifacts.html) Standards such as OGC Observations and Measurements, FHIR Observation with Provenance, and certain clinical data models can represent individual observations with links to procedures and sources, thereby approaching the level required for instance-level reproducibility of exposure values.

File-level metadata, finally, describe individual digital objects such as data files and images, including formats, sizes, checksums, and URLs, and they are often represented through distribution entities in DCAT or through RO-Crate file descriptors.[3](https://semiceu.github.io/DCAT-AP-reuse-guidelines/)[17](https://www.researchobject.org/ro-crate/specification/1.1/index.html) For exposure data, file-level metadata can capture the exact netCDF or GeoTIFF files used in computations, which is essential provenance information if recomputation requires reusing the same environmental fields. An effective exposure metadata registry will likely need to combine dataset-, variable-, geocoded-row-, value-, and file-level metadata, but not all standards address each level, and this review will highlight those that offer more fine-grained descriptions.

### 2.3 Domain specificity: environmental-health versus general-purpose standards

Metadata standards vary in domain specificity, ranging from general-purpose schemas designed to describe any kind of dataset or digital object to highly domain-specific models tailored to environmental monitoring or clinical exposures. General-purpose standards such as DCAT, DataCite, schema.org Dataset, Dublin Core, and W3C PROV-O are not inherently tied to environmental data but can be applied to exposure datasets by using domain-specific controlled vocabularies in metadata fields such as subject, variable names, and units.[3](https://semiceu.github.io/DCAT-AP-reuse-guidelines/)[4](https://schema.org/Dataset)[11](https://schema.datacite.org) Domain-specific standards in the geospatial and environmental-monitoring domain, such as ISO 19115, ISO/TS 19139, ISO 19156/OGC Observations and Measurements, ACDD, and STAC, are tailored to describing spatial and temporal properties of environmental datasets and observations, making them natural candidates for exposure metadata.[7](https://www.iso.org/standard/53798.html)[8](https://www.iso.org/obp/ui/)[9](http://www.opengeospatial.org/standards/om)[6](https://stacspec.org) Clinical and health informatics standards such as OMOP CDM and HL7 FHIR are domain-specific in the healthcare realm but include constructs for environmental exposures, making them relevant for linking exposure metadata to health outcomes.[12](https://www.ohdsi.org/data-standardization/)[15](https://build.fhir.org/ig/HL7/fhir-us-phpl/artifacts.html)

Exposure-science–specific metadata frameworks are less mature but include environmental public health tracking metadata profiles and specialized checklists or minimal information standards proposed for exposure assessment studies, which may encode study-level and variable-level characteristics relevant to exposure derivation.[1](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18849771/)[20](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1257643/) These domain-specific schemas often build on general standards by defining profiles or extensions, such as DCAT-AP profiles tailored to environmental data, or OMOP extensions for external exposures and geospatial linkage, illustrating a recurring pattern in metadata ecosystems where general standards provide base structures and domain communities supply specialized semantics.[3](https://semiceu.github.io/DCAT-AP-reuse-guidelines/)[14](https://www.ohdsi.org/web/wiki/doku.php?id=projects%3Aworkgroups%3Agis)

### 2.4 Provenance depth and instance-level reproducibility

A central theme of this review is the depth of provenance captured by different standards and formats, particularly as it relates to instance-level reproducibility. Provenance depth can be conceptualized along a continuum from minimal dataset-level provenance, which records only the origin of datasets and perhaps a general description of methods, to full instance-level provenance, which records, for each derived exposure value, the specific source data, algorithms, parameter settings, and software versions used in its computation.[9](http://www.opengeospatial.org/standards/om)[17](https://www.researchobject.org/ro-crate/specification/1.1/index.html) Standards such as ISO 19115 and DataCite capture dataset-level provenance through fields like lineage and related identifiers, but they do not natively describe individual exposure assignments.[7](https://www.iso.org/standard/53798.html)[11](https://schema.datacite.org) W3C PROV-O, by contrast, defines a generic model for describing entities, activities, and agents involved in data production, and when applied at sufficient granularity, it can capture instance-level provenance by linking specific exposure values (entities) to computation activities and input datasets.[17](https://www.researchobject.org/ro-crate/specification/1.1/index.html)

Domain-specific observation models such as OGC Observations and Measurements and HL7 FHIR’s Observation and Provenance resources offer intermediate to deep provenance capabilities by representing observations as results of procedures applied to features-of-interest, with links to sampling features, sensors, and measurement protocols.[9](http://www.opengeospatial.org/standards/om)[15](https://build.fhir.org/ig/HL7/fhir-us-phpl/artifacts.html) In exposure science, where many exposure values are model-derived rather than directly measured, provenance must also describe modeling chains, which can be recorded using workflow metadata standards such as RO-Crate that embed PROV-O graphs and software environment descriptors alongside data files.[17](https://www.researchobject.org/ro-crate/specification/1.1/index.html) Pipeline sidecars often contain detailed configuration and process logs that, if structured and mapped into PROV-O or similar models, could provide instance-level reproducibility, but in their raw form they are usually ad hoc and lack stable semantics.[10](https://wiki.esipfed.org/Attribute_Convention_for_Data_Discovery_1-3)

In the following sections, this conceptual taxonomy is used to analyze specific standards and formats, asking for each: what units of description it supports, whether it is general or domain-specific, how deeply it captures provenance, and whether it can support reproducibility at the level of individual exposure values or only at higher levels of aggregation.

## 3. General-Purpose Dataset and Catalog Metadata Standards

### 3.1 DCAT and DCAT-AP: data catalog vocabularies

The Data Catalog Vocabulary (DCAT) is a W3C recommendation that defines an RDF vocabulary for describing datasets and data catalogs on the web, with core classes such as dcat:Dataset, dcat:Distribution, and dcat:Catalog, and properties for titles, descriptions, keywords, spatial and temporal coverage, and access URLs.[3](https://semiceu.github.io/DCAT-AP-reuse-guidelines/)[https://www.w3.org/TR/vocab-dcat-3/](https://www.w3.org/TR/vocab-dcat-3/) DCAT’s primary purpose is to support dataset discovery and interoperability between catalogs by providing a common model for representing metadata about datasets and their distributions, making it a general-purpose standard widely adopted in open data portals and research data repositories.[3](https://semiceu.github.io/DCAT-AP-reuse-guidelines/) For environmental-exposure data, DCAT can be used to describe exposure datasets hosted in portals such as national open data platforms, capturing dataset-level metadata including spatial and temporal extent, but it does not natively represent variable-level or instance-level exposure information.

DCAT-AP, the DCAT Application Profile for data portals in Europe, is a profile maintained under the EU’s SEMIC umbrella that tailors DCAT to European open data portals by constraining and extending certain properties, adding elements such as themes based on EU Vocabularies and specific usage notes.[3](https://semiceu.github.io/DCAT-AP-reuse-guidelines/)[https://joinup.ec.europa.eu/collection/semantic-interoperability-community-semic/document/dcat-ap-v21](https://joinup.ec.europa.eu/collection/semantic-interoperability-community-semic/document/dcat-ap-v21) The DCAT-AP reuse guidelines explicitly discuss how to create new DCAT-AP profiles for particular domains, emphasizing the importance of describing usage context in the introductory sections and, where necessary, defining new properties in supporting vocabularies linked to admissible vocabularies to make intended semantics explicit.[3](https://semiceu.github.io/DCAT-AP-reuse-guidelines/) For an environmental-exposure metadata registry, one could envision a DCAT-AP profile specifically for exposure datasets, restricting and extending DCAT-AP classes to capture exposure-relevant attributes such as exposure type (air pollution, heat, noise), population groups, and exposure assessment methods, following the profile design guidance in the DCAT-AP reuse guidelines.[3](https://semiceu.github.io/DCAT-AP-reuse-guidelines/)

In terms of units of description, DCAT and DCAT-AP operate primarily at the dataset and distribution level, with some ability to describe collections through dcat:Catalog and to relate datasets to services or other entities, but they do not define structures for individual variables or observations.[3](https://semiceu.github.io/DCAT-AP-reuse-guidelines/) Their primary purpose is discovery and citation: DCAT metadata are often harvested by catalogs and search engines, and DCAT-AP is mandated in some European infrastructures for data portal metadata.[3](https://semiceu.github.io/DCAT-AP-reuse-guidelines/) Provenance support in DCAT is modest, with properties such as dct:source, dct:isVersionOf, and dct:provenanceStatement inherited from Dublin Core Terms, which can be used to record lineage and source datasets but generally at a coarse level.[https://www.w3.org/TR/vocab-dcat-3/](https://www.w3.org/TR/vocab-dcat-3/) As such, DCAT and DCAT-AP are general standards that are highly suitable for dataset-level discovery of exposure datasets but insufficient by themselves to support instance-level reproducibility of exposure assignment.

### 3.2 DataCite Metadata Schema: citation-oriented dataset metadata

The DataCite Metadata Schema is maintained by DataCite, an international non-profit organization that issues DOIs for research data and other scholarly outputs, and it defines a set of core metadata properties required and recommended for accurate and consistent identification and citation of digital resources.[11](https://schema.datacite.org)[https://doi.org/10.5438/0014](https://doi.org/10.5438/0014) The schema includes fields such as identifier (typically a DOI), creators, titles, publisher, publication year, resource type, subjects, contributors, dates, language, alternate identifiers, related identifiers, size, format, version, rights, descriptions, geo-location, funding references, and related resources, and it is serialized in XML and JSON formats.[11](https://schema.datacite.org) For environmental-exposure datasets, DataCite metadata enable precise citation of curated datasets, including exposure data products derived from monitoring networks or modeling chains, and they can incorporate spatial and temporal bounding boxes, keywords, and links to related publications or other datasets.

DataCite’s governing body is DataCite itself, with schema versions periodically revised in consultation with the community, and the schema is widely adopted by data repositories across disciplines including environmental sciences and health.[11](https://schema.datacite.org) The unit of description in the DataCite schema is primarily the dataset or digital object such as a collection of exposure data or a single netCDF file, and its primary purpose is citation and discovery rather than detailed semantic description of variables or observations.[11](https://schema.datacite.org) Provenance is supported to a limited degree through relatedIdentifier elements that can indicate relationships such as “IsDerivedFrom” or “IsSupplementTo”, thereby allowing repositories to record that an exposure dataset was derived from particular environmental or health datasets, but the schema does not entail formal provenance models like PROV-O.[https://doi.org/10.5438/0014](https://doi.org/10.5438/0014)

In the context of environmental-exposure metadata, DataCite metadata are important for identifying and citing exposure datasets in publications and meta-analyses, and they can provide high-level links to source datasets and related articles, but they do not themselves encode the detailed exposure derivation steps needed for instance-level reproducibility.[11](https://schema.datacite.org) Consequently, DataCite is classified here as a general-purpose standard focused on dataset-level citation and discovery, complementing but not replacing more detailed domain-specific or provenance-focused schemas.

### 3.3 Dublin Core and RSLP Collection Description: foundational descriptive schemas

The Dublin Core Metadata Element Set (DCMES) and its refinement Dublin Core Terms are general-purpose metadata vocabularies originally developed by the Dublin Core Metadata Initiative to describe a wide range of resources including documents, images, datasets, and services, with core elements such as title, creator, subject, description, publisher, contributor, date, type, format, identifier, source, language, relation, coverage, and rights.[https://www.dublincore.org/specifications/dublin-core/dces/](https://www.dublincore.org/specifications/dublin-core/dces/) Dublin Core has been widely adopted as a foundational descriptive schema in library and repository contexts and underpins later standards such as DCAT and DataCite, which reuse Dublin Core properties.[3](https://semiceu.github.io/DCAT-AP-reuse-guidelines/)[11](https://schema.datacite.org) For environmental-exposure datasets, Dublin Core elements can capture basic descriptive information but lack domain-specific semantics and provenance detail.

The RSLP Collection Description Schema, proposed in the late 1990s within the UK Research Support Libraries Programme (RSLP), defines a structured set of metadata attributes for describing collections, including fields for collection identifier, title, description, subject, collector, location, access conditions, and relationships to other collections.[19](http://www.ukoln.ac.uk/metadata/rslp/schema/) The schema was designed for library and archive collections but conceptually extends to dataset collections, and it has influenced subsequent work on collection-level metadata in various domains.[19](http://www.ukoln.ac.uk/metadata/rslp/schema/) In an exposure metadata registry, RSLP-like collection description could be used to describe collections of exposure datasets or multi-cohort exposure data resources, capturing high-level information about what the collections contain and how they relate to other resources.

Both Dublin Core and RSLP are governed by their respective initiatives and are mature but relatively simple schemas, operating at resource or collection levels rather than variable or instance levels.[19](http://www.ukoln.ac.uk/metadata/rslp/schema/)[https://www.dublincore.org/specifications/dublin-core/dces/](https://www.dublincore.org/specifications/dublin-core/dces/) Their primary purpose is discovery and general description, and they include minimal provenance in the form of source and relation fields but do not support detailed derivation narratives. As such, they are foundational general-purpose standards that underpin more specialized schemas but do not themselves suffice for exposure-specific reproducibility needs.

### 3.4 Schema.org Dataset and science-on-schema.org: web-native dataset markup

Schema.org is a collaborative initiative led by major search engine providers that defines a vocabulary of types and properties for structured data on web pages, enabling search engines to better understand and index content; its Dataset type is specifically intended to describe datasets.[4](https://schema.org/Dataset)[https://schema.org/] The Dataset type includes properties such as name, description, creator, publisher, spatialCoverage, temporalCoverage, variableMeasured, distribution, isBasedOn, citation, and measurementTechnique, among others, and schema.org is typically embedded in HTML using JSON-LD or microdata formats.[4](https://schema.org/Dataset) For environmental-exposure datasets, schema.org Dataset markup can make dataset landing pages more visible to general web search, as search engines like Google have documented support for Dataset markup to enable dataset search features.[4](https://schema.org/Dataset)

The science-on-schema.org initiative, coordinated by the Earth Science Information Partners (ESIP), provides guidance for repository operators and data providers on how to use schema.org markup for scientific datasets, including recommendations for mapping common metadata elements to schema.org properties and examples for datasets and data repositories.[5](https://github.com/esipfed/science-on-schema.org) The guidance includes best practices for describing datasets and distributions (files), linking to organizations, specifying spatial and temporal coverage, and using the variableMeasured property to describe scientific variables, often in concert with domain ontologies and controlled vocabularies.[5](https://github.com/esipfed/science-on-schema.org) For environmental-exposure datasets, science-on-schema.org provides a practical path to expose metadata for discovery via web search and can complement more formal standards like DCAT and ISO 19115.

Schema.org is governed through a community process stewarded by schema.org editors, and the Dataset type is stable but may be extended over time; science-on-schema.org is a community-driven guidance repository without formal standards-body governance but grounded in practices of scientific repositories.[4](https://schema.org/Dataset)[5](https://github.com/esipfed/science-on-schema.org) The unit of description in schema.org Dataset is the dataset, with distribution-level description for individual files, and variableMeasured can provide variable-level semantics, though without strict constraints.[4](https://schema.org/Dataset) Provenance properties such as isBasedOn and measurementTechnique can record relationships to source datasets and methods but do not enforce detailed provenance models.[4](https://schema.org/Dataset) Overall, schema.org Dataset and science-on-schema.org are general standards for enhancing web discoverability of datasets, including environmental-exposure data, with moderate support for semantic description but limited instance-level provenance.

### 3.5 DDI Alliance standards: social and health science metadata

The Data Documentation Initiative (DDI) Alliance is an international membership organization that develops technical standards for describing research data in the social, demographic, economic, and health sciences, including survey data, longitudinal studies, and aggregate data.[16](https://ddialliance.org) DDI standards such as DDI-Codebook and DDI-Lifecycle define XML schemas and conceptual models for documenting datasets, variables, questions, instruments, and study designs, with rich metadata for sampling, data collection, processing, and data management.[16](https://ddialliance.org)[https://doi.org/10.5281/zenodo.3665575](https://doi.org/10.5281/zenodo.3665575) For environmental-exposure studies that involve questionnaires, cohort designs, and linked environmental measures, DDI can provide comprehensive study-level and variable-level metadata, including information on how exposure variables were collected or derived and how they relate to other study variables.

DDI’s governing body is the DDI Alliance, and the standards have undergone multiple versions with broad adoption in social science data archives and some health data repositories.[16](https://ddialliance.org) Units of description in DDI encompass datasets, variables, questions, and study components, making it more granular than purely dataset-level schemas like DCAT.[16](https://ddialliance.org) DDI includes constructs for process metadata, such as data processing steps and transformation instructions, which can support reproducibility at the level of variable derivation, though it does not natively implement formal provenance models like PROV-O.[https://doi.org/10.5281/zenodo.3665575](https://doi.org/10.5281/zenodo.3665575) DDI is domain-specific to social and health sciences but can be combined with environmental data models to describe exposure assessment within broader studies.

In the context of environmental-exposure metadata, DDI is particularly relevant for documenting cohort studies and epidemiological analyses that link exposures to health outcomes, providing detailed metadata about human subjects, sampling frames, study timelines, and derived variables.[16](https://ddialliance.org) However, DDI does not specialize in environmental observational data and may need to be integrated with geospatial and environmental-monitoring standards such as ISO 19115 and OGC O&M to fully capture the environmental side of exposure assessments.

### 3.6 Attribute Conventions for Data Discovery (ACDD) and CF metadata in netCDF

The Attribute Convention for Data Discovery (ACDD) is a community-developed convention, formalized through ESIP documentation, that specifies recommended global attributes for netCDF datasets to facilitate discovery and interoperability in digital libraries and data portals.[10](https://wiki.esipfed.org/Attribute_Convention_for_Data_Discovery_1-3) ACDD defines attributes such as title, summary, keywords, Conventions, institution, history, source, references, date_created, geospatial_lat_min/max, geospatial_lon_min/max, time_coverage_start/end, and others, and it is widely used in THREDDS and other tools that extract metadata from netCDF files and export them to formats such as Dublin Core, DIF, FGDC, and ISO 19115.[10](https://wiki.esipfed.org/Attribute_Convention_for_Data_Discovery_1-3) For environmental-exposure datasets stored in netCDF, such as gridded pollution concentration fields or temperature reanalyses, ACDD provides a practical way to ensure that datasets carry sufficient discovery-level metadata to be indexed and understood by users and systems.

ACDD is a convention rather than a formal standard with a governing body, but it is maintained in community documentation and has become a de facto standard in earth science data management.[10](https://wiki.esipfed.org/Attribute_Convention_for_Data_Discovery_1-3) It operates at the dataset level, although some attributes relate to variables and geospatial dimensions, and its primary purpose is discovery rather than detailed provenance or variable semantics, which are addressed more by the CF metadata conventions and CF Standard Name Table.[10](https://wiki.esipfed.org/Attribute_Convention_for_Data_Discovery_1-3) The CF conventions define variable attributes such as standard_name, units, long_name, and coordinates, and the CF Standard Name Table provides controlled names for physical quantities, aiding semantic interoperability across netCDF datasets.[10](https://wiki.esipfed.org/Attribute_Convention_for_Data_Discovery_1-3)[https://cfconventions.org/](https://cfconventions.org/)

For environmental-exposure data, ACDD and CF together ensure that netCDF-based environmental datasets are discoverable and interpretable, and they can be leveraged in exposure metadata registries as part of variable-level semantics and dataset-level discovery metadata.[10](https://wiki.esipfed.org/Attribute_Convention_for_Data_Discovery_1-3) However, they do not capture detailed derivation of exposure values assigned to individuals or populations; their focus is on the environmental fields themselves rather than on exposure assessment algorithms. As such, ACDD is classified here as a general standard with dataset-level discovery focus, with CF serving as a controlled vocabulary and variable semantics framework more than a standalone metadata standard.

### 3.7 Frictionless Data Package and Croissant: lightweight dataset packaging

The Frictionless Data initiative, led by Open Knowledge Foundation, defines the Data Package specification as a simple container format for tabular datasets, consisting of a descriptor file (datapackage.json) that lists resources (data files) and provides metadata such as name, title, description, licenses, sources, and schema information for each resource, including field names, types, and constraints.[https://specs.frictionlessdata.io/data-package/](https://specs.frictionlessdata.io/data-package/) Data Packages are designed for lightweight portability and validation of datasets, with tooling across multiple programming languages, and they have been used in various open data contexts.[https://specs.frictionlessdata.io/data-package/](https://specs.frictionlessdata.io/data-package/) For environmental-exposure data stored in tabular formats, Data Packages can serve as structured descriptors that capture dataset-level and field-level metadata, including basic schema information, but they do not prescribe domain semantics or provenance beyond optional sources and licenses fields.

Croissant is a relatively recent specification developed under the MLCommons umbrella for describing machine learning datasets, aiming to support discovery, reuse, and automated data loading by defining metadata such as dataset name, description, modalities, features, licensing, and distribution information in a JSON-LD format.[https://mlcommons.org/en/croissant/](https://mlcommons.org/en/croissant/) Croissant is intended to serve as a standard representation of datasets for AI workflows and is designed to be machine-actionable, enabling tools to automatically ingest datasets based on their Croissant descriptors.[https://mlcommons.org/en/croissant/](https://mlcommons.org/en/croissant/) Environmental-exposure datasets that feed into machine-learning models, such as exposure prediction models or risk assessment models, could be described using Croissant, making them discoverable and usable in AI pipelines, although Croissant is currently general-purpose and does not contain exposure-specific semantics.

Both Data Package and Croissant are general structured formats rather than domain-specific standards, and they are governed by their respective communities (Frictionless Data and MLCommons).[https://specs.frictionlessdata.io/data-package/](https://specs.frictionlessdata.io/data-package/)[https://mlcommons.org/en/croissant/](https://mlcommons.org/en/croissant/) They operate at the dataset and file levels with some field-level schema detail, serving primarily discovery and automated ingestion purposes, and they contain limited support for provenance, relying on optional metadata fields rather than formal models. In an exposure metadata registry, they could be used as serialization formats or as complementary descriptors for datasets but would need to be augmented with domain-specific semantics and provenance models to support reproducibility.

### 3.8 RO-Crate and research objects: FAIR packaging of data and workflows

RO-Crate is a specification developed by the Research Object community that defines a method of organizing file-based data with associated metadata, using a JSON-LD–based “crate” that describes datasets, files, persons, institutions, software, and workflows, along with their relationships and provenance.[17](https://www.researchobject.org/ro-crate/specification/1.1/index.html) The RO-Crate 1.1 specification describes how to represent the content of a research investigation, including datasets, computational workflows, and their executions, in a machine-readable form grounded in schema.org terms and optionally extended with PROV-O and other vocabularies.[17](https://www.researchobject.org/ro-crate/specification/1.1/index.html) In environmental-exposure science, RO-Crate can be used to package exposure datasets together with the scripts or workflows used to compute exposure values, the environmental and health source datasets, and provenance metadata describing the derivation chain.

RO-Crate is governed by a community working group and has been adopted in several domains, including life sciences and earth sciences, as a means of implementing FAIR data principles by making data, code, and provenance discoverable and reusable together.[17](https://www.researchobject.org/ro-crate/specification/1.1/index.html) The unit of description in RO-Crate is the crate itself, which can encapsulate multiple datasets, files, and workflow executions, but individual entities within the crate can also be described with metadata including type, name, description, and relationships. Provenance support is strong, especially when RO-Crate is combined with PROV-O, enabling representation of which activities generated which entities and which sources they used.[17](https://www.researchobject.org/ro-crate/specification/1.1/index.html)

For instance-level reproducibility of exposure values, RO-Crate can serve as a packaging mechanism in which each exposure computation workflow execution is represented, including parameter settings, input files (e.g., netCDF environmental fields and cohort tables), and output exposure datasets, all linked through provenance relationships.[17](https://www.researchobject.org/ro-crate/specification/1.1/index.html) RO-Crate is general-purpose and not specific to environmental or health data, but its flexibility and strong provenance model make it a promising framework for exposure metadata registries, especially when combined with domain-specific standards for environmental and clinical data. In this review, RO-Crate is classified as a general standard with both dataset-level and workflow-level metadata capabilities, with potential support for instance-level provenance when appropriately used.

## 4. Geospatial, Earth-Observation, and Environmental-Monitoring Standards

### 4.1 ISO 19115-1 and ISO/TS 19139: geospatial metadata and XML encoding

ISO 19115-1:2014 is an international standard that defines the schema required for describing geographic information and services by means of metadata, providing information about identification, extent, quality, spatial and temporal aspects, content, spatial reference, portrayal, distribution, and other properties of digital geographic data and services.[7](https://www.iso.org/standard/53798.html) It is part of the ISO 19100 series of geographic information standards and provides a comprehensive model for geospatial metadata, including classes such as MD_Metadata, MD_DataIdentification, MD_Distribution, and MD_DataQuality, with elements for titles, abstracts, keywords, extents, resolutions, and lineage.[7](https://www.iso.org/standard/53798.html) Exposure datasets that are geospatial in nature, such as gridded air-pollution fields or spatially aggregated exposure indices, can be described using ISO 19115-1 to capture their spatial-temporal coverage and data quality characteristics.

ISO/TS 19139:2007 defines Geographic MetaData XML (gmd) encoding, providing an XML schema implementation derived from ISO 19115, which allows ISO 19115 metadata instances to be represented in XML and exchanged between systems.[8](https://www.iso.org/obp/ui/) The 19139 specification includes XML schema files corresponding to the ISO 19115 UML model and has been widely implemented in geospatial metadata catalogs and services, including OGC Catalogue Service for the Web (CSW).[8](https://www.iso.org/obp/ui/) For exposure metadata registries that rely on geospatial catalogs, ISO 19139 provides the standard encoding format for ISO 19115-based metadata records.

Governance of ISO 19115 and ISO/TS 19139 lies with ISO Technical Committee 211 (ISO/TC 211), and the standards are mature and widely adopted in geospatial data infrastructures.[7](https://www.iso.org/standard/53798.html)[8](https://www.iso.org/obp/ui/) The unit of description is the dataset or service, with capabilities to describe granules and features in certain profiles, and the primary purpose is discovery, interoperability, and data quality documentation for geospatial data. Provenance is supported through the lineage section of MD_DataQuality, which can record sources and processing steps that led to the creation of the dataset, although typically at a narrative dataset-level rather than instance-level.[7](https://www.iso.org/standard/53798.html) ISO 19115-1 is domain-specific to geospatial data but applicable to environmental datasets, making it an important standard for exposure metadata where environmental datasets and derived exposure maps are treated as geospatial resources.

### 4.2 Observations and Measurements (ISO 19156 / OGC O&M) and Sensor Observation Service

The Observations and Measurements (O&M) framework, standardized as ISO 19156 and as an OGC standard, defines a conceptual schema and XML encodings for observations and for features involved in sampling when making observations, providing document models for the exchange of information describing observation acts and their results.[9](http://www.opengeospatial.org/standards/om) The O&M standard specifies classes such as OM_Observation, which links a feature-of-interest, a phenomenon time, a result time, a procedure (sampling or measurement process), a result (value), and metadata about the observation, thereby enabling explicit representation of how environmental measurements are made.[9](http://www.opengeospatial.org/standards/om) ISO 19156/O&M is an essential dependency for OGC Sensor Observation Service (SOS) and other sensor-related standards, which allow querying observations, sensor metadata, and sampled features via web service interfaces.[9](http://www.opengeospatial.org/standards/om)

For environmental-exposure data, O&M is directly relevant because exposures often build on environmental observations such as pollutant concentrations measured at stations or by sensors. O&M can represent these observations with detailed metadata about sensors, locations, sampling procedures, and result values, and when extended to represent model-derived exposures, it could potentially capture exposure assignments as observations whose procedures are computational models rather than physical sensors.[9](http://www.opengeospatial.org/standards/om) Governance of O&M lies with both ISO/TC 211 (for ISO 19156) and OGC, and the standard is mature with implementations in several domains, including hydrology and atmospheric sciences.[9](http://www.opengeospatial.org/standards/om)

Units of description in O&M are individual observations, making it inherently instance-level rather than dataset-level. The primary purpose is to standardize representation and exchange of observations and sampling features, enabling interoperability across sensor systems and observational datasets.[9](http://www.opengeospatial.org/standards/om) Provenance is embedded in the observation model through explicit representation of the procedure (which can include links to sensor metadata, calibration, and methods) and the feature-of-interest, and O&M can be combined with PROV-O for more formal provenance.[9](http://www.opengeospatial.org/standards/om) As such, O&M is one of the few standards that naturally supports instance-level reproducibility for environmental observations and, by extension, could be adapted to represent instance-level exposure values if exposure assignments are treated as observational results.

### 4.3 GeoSPARQL: RDF representation and querying of geospatial data

OGC GeoSPARQL is a standard that defines a vocabulary for representing geospatial data in RDF and an extension to the SPARQL query language for processing geospatial data, enabling querying and representing spatial data on the Semantic Web using interoperable standards.[13](https://www.ogc.org/standards/geosparql/) The GeoSPARQL vocabulary includes classes such as geo:Feature and geo:Geometry and properties such as geo:hasGeometry, with support for serializing geometries as WKT or GML literals, and it defines SPARQL extension functions for spatial predicates (e.g., intersects, within) and relationships.[13](https://www.ogc.org/standards/geosparql/) For environmental-exposure metadata that are represented in RDF, GeoSPARQL provides the necessary tools to encode geospatial aspects of datasets, features (such as monitoring stations or population units), and exposure assignments, and to perform spatial queries across them.

GeoSPARQL is governed by OGC and is widely used in Linked Data and semantic web contexts where geospatial data are represented as RDF.[13](https://www.ogc.org/standards/geosparql/) Its unit of description is geospatial features and geometries, and its primary purpose is enabling interoperability and querying rather than describing datasets per se. Provenance is not a direct focus of GeoSPARQL, but when combined with RDF vocabularies such as PROV-O and DCAT, GeoSPARQL can situate geospatial features within dataset and provenance contexts.[13](https://www.ogc.org/standards/geosparql/)[https://www.w3.org/TR/prov-o/](https://www.w3.org/TR/prov-o/) In an exposure metadata registry built on RDF, GeoSPARQL would be crucial for representing locations and spatial extents of environmental datasets, health data, and exposure assignments, but it would need to be integrated with other standards to capture discovery and provenance.

### 4.4 SpatioTemporal Asset Catalog (STAC): cataloging spatiotemporal data assets

The SpatioTemporal Asset Catalog (STAC) specification is a community-developed standard that describes a standardized way to expose collections of spatiotemporal data, particularly earth observation imagery and related assets, using JSON-based catalogs that can be crawled and searched.[6](https://stacspec.org) STAC defines core objects such as Item (representing a single spatiotemporal asset like an image), Collection (grouping items), and Catalog, with fields for id, geometry, properties (including datetime), links, and assets, and it supports extension mechanisms for domain-specific metadata.[6](https://stacspec.org) For environmental-exposure data, STAC can be used to catalog environmental data products such as satellite-derived wildfire smoke layers, land-surface temperature images, or pollution concentration maps, making them discoverable and accessible as spatiotemporal assets that can be used in exposure modeling.

Governance of STAC is informal but coordinated through the STAC specification repository and community processes, and the specification has seen rapid adoption in earth observation platforms and cloud-based geospatial infrastructures.[6](https://stacspec.org) Units of description in STAC are items (assets) and collections, corresponding to individual files (e.g., GeoTIFF images) or series of images, and its primary purpose is discovery and access rather than detailed semantic description of physical variables or provenance.[6](https://stacspec.org) Provenance in STAC can be represented through properties such as created and links to source collections, and some extensions allow for recording processing levels and lineage, but STAC is not a full provenance model.

In exposure metadata registries, STAC can play a role in cataloging environmental datasets used as inputs to exposure models, particularly remote sensing and climate data, allowing exposure workflows to programmatically discover and access required assets.[6](https://stacspec.org) However, STAC itself does not describe how exposure values are derived from these assets; it is an asset catalog standard and thus primarily supports dataset- and file-level discovery.

### 4.5 Specialized metadata for air-quality monitoring and environmental health tracking

Beyond general geospatial standards, there are specialized metadata frameworks for air-quality monitoring and environmental public health tracking that, while not always formalized as standards, provide structured metadata for exposure-related data. A recent study on harmonized global air-quality monitoring metadata compiled a dataset of station metadata including station types and area characteristics, recognizing that existing metadata reporting practices were inconsistent and often insufficient for cross-network analysis.[2](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13022413/) The study developed a classifier to infer station types based on available metadata and proposed harmonized fields capturing aspects such as station siting (urban, suburban, rural), exposure context (traffic, industrial, background), and monitoring objectives, highlighting the importance of standardized metadata for air-quality exposure assessment.[2](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13022413/) While this work does not itself constitute a formally governed standard, it points to the need for air-quality–specific metadata schemas that could be codified as extensions to O&M or as profiles in DCAT or ISO 19115.

In the U.S. CDC Environmental Public Health Tracking Network, efforts have been undertaken to adopt a metadata standard, create an EPHT-specific metadata profile, and develop an open-source metadata application to improve data sharing and reuse.[1](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18849771/) The EPHT metadata profile likely builds on existing standards such as ISO 19115 and HL7 but adapts them to the specific needs of environmental public health data, including exposure measures, risk indicators, and health outcomes.[1](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18849771/) Governance of EPHT metadata lies within CDC and partner agencies, and while the profile may not be widely adopted outside EPHT, it is an example of an exposure-science–specific adaptation of general standards.

These specialized frameworks operate at dataset and variable levels, describing monitoring stations, exposure indicators, and public health measures, and their primary purpose is to support surveillance, analysis, and communication in environmental public health.[1](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18849771/)[2](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13022413/) Provenance in these contexts may include information on data sources and processing steps but is often limited; detailed instance-level provenance of exposure assignments is rarely standardized. As such, these frameworks are currently more akin to project-specific profiles and structured formats than to universally adopted standards, but they provide valuable guidance for designing exposure metadata schemas.

## 5. Clinical, Epidemiological, and Exposure-Science Data Models

### 5.1 OMOP Common Data Model and OHDSI GIS/exposure extensions

The Observational Medical Outcomes Partnership (OMOP) Common Data Model (CDM), developed and maintained by the OHDSI (Observational Health Data Sciences and Informatics) community, is an open community data standard designed to standardize the structure and content of observational healthcare databases to enable systematic analysis.[12](https://www.ohdsi.org/data-standardization/) The OMOP CDM transforms data from disparate sources—such as electronic health records, claims data, and registries—into a common format using standardized vocabularies and coding schemes, allowing analyses to be performed using a library of standard analytic routines.[12](https://www.ohdsi.org/data-standardization/) While the core OMOP CDM focuses on clinical and demographic data, the OHDSI GIS Working Group is extending the CDM to accommodate environmental exposures, social determinants of health, access to healthcare, and built environment characteristics.[14](https://www.ohdsi.org/web/wiki/doku.php?id=projects%3Aworkgroups%3Agis)

The GIS Working Group aims to define a CDM-compatible schema that accommodates four types of exposures—environmental toxins, social determinants of health, access to healthcare, and the built environment—by identifying or developing local and standard vocabularies and mappings that follow the CDM’s data model for healthcare concepts.[14](https://www.ohdsi.org/web/wiki/doku.php?id=projects%3Aworkgroups%3Agis) This effort includes designing tables and fields for storing exposure measures linked to individuals, including geocoded addresses and environmental exposure indices, within the OMOP CDM structure, thereby making environmental-exposure data computable alongside clinical data.[14](https://www.ohdsi.org/web/wiki/doku.php?id=projects%3Aworkgroups%3Agis) Governance lies with the OHDSI community, and while the exposure extensions are still under active development, they represent a significant move toward standardizing environmental-exposure metadata in a clinical data model.

Units of description in OMOP CDM include person-level records, condition occurrences, measurement records, and other clinical events, and the exposure extensions will add person-level exposure records, potentially capturing multiple dimensions such as exposure type, intensity, duration, and source.[12](https://www.ohdsi.org/data-standardization/)[14](https://www.ohdsi.org/web/wiki/doku.php?id=projects%3Aworkgroups%3Agis) The primary purpose is to enable standardized analysis and pooling of observational data, including exposure–outcome associations, across databases. Provenance in OMOP CDM is typically limited to recording source systems and mapping processes, but the exposure extensions may incorporate more detailed metadata about exposure derivation, such as the models and environmental datasets used, especially if combined with external provenance frameworks.[12](https://www.ohdsi.org/data-standardization/)

In terms of domain specificity, OMOP CDM is domain-specific to healthcare but is being extended to environmental exposures and social determinants, making it directly relevant to environmental-exposure metadata linked to health outcomes.[12](https://www.ohdsi.org/data-standardization/)[14](https://www.ohdsi.org/web/wiki/doku.php?id=projects%3Aworkgroups%3Agis) For instance-level reproducibility of exposure values, OMOP CDM exposure tables would need to include detailed metadata about exposure assignment methods, and this may require integration with geospatial standards and provenance models. As such, OMOP CDM is a general clinical standard with emerging exposure-science extensions, offering a pathway for standardized representation of exposure measures at the individual level but not yet fully covering provenance.

### 5.2 HL7 FHIR and public health exposure profiles

HL7 FHIR (Fast Healthcare Interoperability Resources) is a modern standard for exchanging healthcare information electronically, using RESTful APIs and standardized resources to represent clinical and administrative data.[https://www.hl7.org/fhir/](https://www.hl7.org/fhir/) Within FHIR, the Observation resource represents measurements and assertions about a patient or subject, including vital signs, laboratory results, and environmental exposures, and it includes fields such as code (what was observed), subject, effectiveDateTime (when), value, and related components.[https://www.hl7.org/fhir/observation.html](https://www.hl7.org/fhir/observation.html) The FHIR Provenance resource records information about entities, activities, and agents involved in producing a resource, providing a formal provenance model analogous to PROV-O but adapted to clinical contexts.[https://www.hl7.org/fhir/provenance.html](https://www.hl7.org/fhir/provenance.html)

The HL7 FHIR US Public Health Profiles Library implementation guide includes profiles for exposure-related observations, specifying how to represent types of exposure/contact using the Observation.code field, such as environmental, activity, event, location, person, or animal exposures.[15](https://build.fhir.org/ig/HL7/fhir-us-phpl/artifacts.html) In these profiles, the type of exposure/contact is encoded in Observation.code, and additional fields and extensions may capture details about exposure context, intensity, and duration, making FHIR a candidate standard for representing environmental exposures in clinical and public health systems.[15](https://build.fhir.org/ig/HL7/fhir-us-phpl/artifacts.html) Governance of FHIR resides with HL7, and the US Public Health Profiles Library is managed through HL7 implementation guide processes.

Units of description in FHIR are individual resources, with Observation representing instance-level measurements or exposures, and Provenance representing instance-level derivation metadata for those resources.[https://www.hl7.org/fhir/observation.html](https://www.hl7.org/fhir/observation.html)[https://www.hl7.org/fhir/provenance.html](https://www.hl7.org/fhir/provenance.html) The primary purpose of FHIR exposure profiles is to standardize representation and exchange of exposure data in clinical and public health settings, enabling interoperable surveillance and research. Provenance depth is potentially high, especially when Provenance resources are used to record details of data generation, including environmental data sources and algorithms used in exposure assignment.[15](https://build.fhir.org/ig/HL7/fhir-us-phpl/artifacts.html)

FHIR is domain-specific to healthcare but explicitly accommodates environmental exposures as a type of observation, making it a key standard for linking exposure metadata to health outcomes at the instance level.[15](https://build.fhir.org/ig/HL7/fhir-us-phpl/artifacts.html) For instance-level reproducibility, FHIR Observation plus Provenance can capture both the exposure value and its derivation, but this requires careful implementation and may need to be complemented by more generic provenance models or workflow packaging frameworks like RO-Crate to capture complex modeling chains.

### 5.3 DDI in epidemiological and exposure cohorts

As noted earlier, DDI standards are widely used to describe epidemiological cohort studies, surveys, and longitudinal data, including those involving environmental exposures.[16](https://ddialliance.org) In cohort studies where exposure variables are derived from environmental datasets, DDI metadata can describe how variables such as “daily mean \(\mathrm{PM_{2.5}}\) exposure” or “heat-wave exposure status” were constructed, including references to original environmental datasets, measurement protocols, and transformation algorithms.[16](https://ddialliance.org)[https://doi.org/10.5281/zenodo.3665575](https://doi.org/10.5281/zenodo.3665575) DDI’s variable-level documentation can include details such as codes, categories, derived variable definitions, and imputation methods, which are critical for interpreting exposure variables in meta-analysis.

In epidemiological contexts, DDI is typically used alongside clinical or health data models to document the study design and variable derivation, and it can incorporate references to environmental datasets described using geospatial standards, creating a multi-standard metadata environment.[16](https://ddialliance.org) Governance via the DDI Alliance ensures that DDI models remain consistent and extensible, and the standards are mature and widely used in social science data archives such as ICPSR and CESSDA.[16](https://ddialliance.org) Provenance support in DDI is moderate, with process metadata describing transformations, but not as formalized as in PROV-O or FHIR Provenance.

For environmental-exposure metadata registries, DDI can serve as the study-level and variable-level documentation framework, capturing human-side metadata such as cohort design, questionnaires, and variable derivations, but it must be connected to geospatial and environmental observational standards to fully represent the exposure assessment processes. DDI is thus classified as a domain-specific standard focused on social and health research, with variable-level but not instance-level exposure provenance.

### 5.4 Exposure science and personalized exposure assessment frameworks

Exposure science has developed conceptual frameworks and guidelines for exposure assessment that imply metadata requirements even when not formalized as data standards. For example, personalized exposure assessment approaches, as reviewed by Sexton et al., emphasize the use of personal monitoring devices, biomarkers, and integrative models to characterize individual exposure profiles over time, with detailed attention to human activity patterns and microenvironmental exposures.[20](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1257643/) These approaches require metadata about sensor characteristics, sampling schedules, data quality, and human behaviors, as well as about environmental concentrations and health outcomes, suggesting a need for metadata standards that span sensor, environmental, and clinical domains.[20](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1257643/)

While exposure-science frameworks such as the exposome and integrated exposure models have inspired checklists and minimal information guidelines for exposure assessment studies, formal metadata standards specific to exposure science are still emerging. Efforts like EPHT metadata profiles and OMOP exposure extensions can be seen as institutional and community responses to these conceptual needs.[1](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18849771/)[14](https://www.ohdsi.org/web/wiki/doku.php?id=projects%3Aworkgroups%3Agis) However, there remains a gap in standardized, exposure-specific metadata schemas that explicitly encode exposure assessment designs, microenvironmental contexts, personal monitoring devices, and integrative modeling chains in a reusable way.

In summary, clinical and epidemiological standards like OMOP CDM, FHIR, and DDI provide structures for linking environmental exposures to health outcomes and documenting cohort designs and exposures, but exposure science-specific metadata standards are still nascent and often embedded as profiles or extensions within these broader frameworks.[12](https://www.ohdsi.org/data-standardization/)[15](https://build.fhir.org/ig/HL7/fhir-us-phpl/artifacts.html)[16](https://ddialliance.org) This suggests that an exposure metadata registry must draw on multiple existing standards and possibly define new profiles or extensions to adequately capture exposure-specific metadata.

## 6. Provenance and Workflow-Level Metadata Standards

### 6.1 W3C PROV-O: a general provenance ontology

The W3C PROV-O specification defines an OWL2 ontology representing the PROV Data Model, which provides classes and properties for modeling provenance information, including entities (data and other artifacts), activities (processes that generate or use entities), and agents (people, organizations, software) and the relationships between them.[https://www.w3.org/TR/prov-o/](https://www.w3.org/TR/prov-o/) PROV-O includes core classes such as prov:Entity, prov:Activity, and prov:Agent and properties such as prov:wasGeneratedBy, prov:used, prov:wasDerivedFrom, prov:wasAttributedTo, and prov:wasAssociatedWith, allowing detailed narratives of data derivation to be expressed in RDF.[https://www.w3.org/TR/prov-o/](https://www.w3.org/TR/prov-o/) For environmental-exposure data, PROV-O can be used to describe how exposure datasets and individual exposure values were generated from environmental datasets, geocoded addresses, and computational models.

Governance of PROV-O resides with the W3C, and the specification is a stable recommendation that has been widely adopted in various domains requiring provenance tracking, including e-science, life sciences, and digital preservation.[https://www.w3.org/TR/prov-o/](https://www.w3.org/TR/prov-o/) Units of description in PROV-O are entities and activities, which can be at any granularity from entire datasets to individual values, and its primary purpose is to enable interoperable representation and exchange of provenance data, not to describe datasets themselves.[https://www.w3.org/TR/prov-o/](https://www.w3.org/TR/prov-o/) Provenance depth in PROV-O is, in principle, unlimited: one can record full derivation chains for each exposure value, including the specific environmental data files used, the algorithms and parameter settings, and the software and hardware environments, providing the strongest support for instance-level reproducibility among all standards discussed.

In practice, using PROV-O for exposure metadata requires integrating it with domain-specific schemas such as DCAT, ISO 19115, OGC O&M, OMOP, and FHIR, so that entities and activities are properly typed and linked to domain concepts.[9](http://www.opengeospatial.org/standards/om)[15](https://build.fhir.org/ig/HL7/fhir-us-phpl/artifacts.html) For example, exposure values could be modeled as prov:Entity instances derived from O&M observations and computed via prov:Activity instances representing exposure assignment algorithms, with agents representing researchers or institutions. PROV-O itself is general-purpose and does not prescribe domain semantics, but its flexibility and formalism make it a key component of any exposure metadata registry that aims to support reproducibility.

### 6.2 RO-Crate and PROV integration for exposure workflows

As discussed earlier, RO-Crate provides a packaging mechanism that can embed PROV-O provenance graphs within a crate describing data and workflows.[17](https://www.researchobject.org/ro-crate/specification/1.1/index.html) In the context of environmental-exposure workflows, RO-Crate can be used to capture the end-to-end process of exposure assessment, from ingesting environmental datasets (e.g., netCDF files described with ACDD and CF) and geocoded cohort data to executing computational workflows that assign exposures and generating output exposure datasets.[10](https://wiki.esipfed.org/Attribute_Convention_for_Data_Discovery_1-3)[17](https://www.researchobject.org/ro-crate/specification/1.1/index.html) Within the RO-Crate JSON-LD metadata, entities corresponding to environmental datasets, cohort tables, scripts, and output exposure files can be annotated with schema.org properties and PROV-O relations, thereby encoding both descriptive metadata and provenance in a unified structure.[17](https://www.researchobject.org/ro-crate/specification/1.1/index.html)

For instance-level reproducibility, RO-Crate can represent not only the workflows but also, if desired, the outputs at a granular level, although large-scale exposure assignments may make value-level provenance burdensome. Instead, a common approach is to record workflow-level provenance, including the parameters and software versions, and treat exposure values as results of those workflows without individual derivation records. However, in specific cases such as personal exposure monitoring studies, where each exposure value is critical, more detailed provenance could be recorded using PROV-O within RO-Crate, linking each exposure measurement to specific sensor readings and calibration events.[20](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1257643/)[17](https://www.researchobject.org/ro-crate/specification/1.1/index.html)

RO-Crate thus serves as a general standard for FAIR packaging of data and workflows, with strong potential to support exposure metadata registries by providing a container for both environmental and clinical datasets and for the workflows that connect them. Its governance by the Research Object community and alignment with schema.org and PROV-O make it interoperable with other standards discussed, and its flexibility allows it to incorporate domain-specific profiles, such as exposure metadata schemas or OMOP CDM representations.[17](https://www.researchobject.org/ro-crate/specification/1.1/index.html)[12](https://www.ohdsi.org/data-standardization/)

### 6.3 Domain-specific provenance in O&M, SensorML, and FHIR

Domain-specific standards often embed provenance concepts within their models. OGC Observations and Measurements, for instance, explicitly includes the procedure as part of an OM_Observation, which can reference sensor metadata described using standards like SensorML, including calibration histories and operating conditions.[9](http://www.opengeospatial.org/standards/om)[http://www.opengeospatial.org/standards/sensorml](http://www.opengeospatial.org/standards/sensorml) SensorML itself is an OGC standard that describes sensors and measurement processes, including physical and logical models of sensors, and can record provenance information such as sensor deployment history and processing chains.[http://www.opengeospatial.org/standards/sensorml](http://www.opengeospatial.org/standards/sensorml) For exposure data based on environmental sensors, these standards can capture provenance from the sensor to the observation, which is then used as input for exposure modeling.

In FHIR, the Provenance resource provides a way to record the origin of a resource, including who, what, and when, and it can be linked to Observation resources representing exposure measurements.[https://www.hl7.org/fhir/provenance.html](https://www.hl7.org/fhir/provenance.html)[15](https://build.fhir.org/ig/HL7/fhir-us-phpl/artifacts.html) Provenance attributes such as recorded, reason, activity, and agent can capture details about how an exposure measurement was obtained or computed, including references to external environmental datasets or algorithms. For example, an exposure Observation representing daily \(\mathrm{PM_{2.5}}\) exposure for a patient could be linked via Provenance to the environmental dataset from which concentrations were drawn and to a computation activity representing the exposure assignment procedure.[15](https://build.fhir.org/ig/HL7/fhir-us-phpl/artifacts.html)[https://www.hl7.org/fhir/observation.html](https://www.hl7.org/fhir/observation.html)

While these domain-specific provenance mechanisms are less general than PROV-O, they are tailored to their respective domains and may be easier to implement in domain-specific systems. O&M and SensorML are well suited to environmental sensor networks, capturing detailed provenance of environmental measurements, while FHIR Provenance is suited to clinical and public health systems, capturing provenance of exposure observations as part of the clinical record.[9](http://www.opengeospatial.org/standards/om)[http://www.opengeospatial.org/standards/sensorml](http://www.opengeospatial.org/standards/sensorml)[15](https://build.fhir.org/ig/HL7/fhir-us-phpl/artifacts.html) Integrating these domain-specific provenance models with general models like PROV-O and packaging frameworks like RO-Crate offers a path toward comprehensive exposure provenance that spans environmental and clinical domains.

### 6.4 Which standards support instance-level reproducibility?

Drawing on the analyses above, we can assess which standards and structured formats support instance-level reproducibility of environmental-exposure values and which are limited to dataset-level discovery and interpretation. Dataset-oriented standards such as DCAT, DCAT-AP, DataCite, Dublin Core, schema.org Dataset, DDI (in its dataset-level use), ISO 19115-1, ISO/TS 19139, ACDD, and STAC primarily provide dataset-level metadata; they may include lineage and source references but do not natively model individual exposure assignments or their derivation chains.[3](https://semiceu.github.io/DCAT-AP-reuse-guidelines/)[7](https://www.iso.org/standard/53798.html)[10](https://wiki.esipfed.org/Attribute_Convention_for_Data_Discovery_1-3)[11](https://schema.datacite.org)[6](https://stacspec.org) These standards are thus crucial for discovery and citation but insufficient for instance-level reproducibility.

Observation-level and clinical data standards such as OGC Observations and Measurements, SensorML, HL7 FHIR Observation/Provenance, and OMOP CDM with exposure extensions approach instance-level reproducibility by modeling individual observations or person-level exposure records with associated procedures and provenance.[9](http://www.opengeospatial.org/standards/om)[12](https://www.ohdsi.org/data-standardization/)[15](https://build.fhir.org/ig/HL7/fhir-us-phpl/artifacts.html) O&M and SensorML can capture how each environmental measurement was made, while FHIR can capture how each exposure observation was obtained or computed; OMOP CDM provides a standardized structure for storing exposure measures linked to individuals, though provenance details depend on implementation.[9](http://www.opengeospatial.org/standards/om)[12](https://www.ohdsi.org/data-standardization/)[15](https://build.fhir.org/ig/HL7/fhir-us-phpl/artifacts.html)

Provenance-focused standards such as W3C PROV-O and RO-Crate, when applied at sufficient granularity, can fully support instance-level reproducibility by representing derivation chains for each exposure value, including environmental inputs, computational activities, and output entities.[https://www.w3.org/TR/prov-o/](https://www.w3.org/TR/prov-o/)[17](https://www.researchobject.org/ro-crate/specification/1.1/index.html) However, using PROV-O at this level may be practically challenging for large datasets, and many implementations opt for workflow-level provenance rather than value-level. Pipeline sidecars, though not standardized, often contain detailed configuration and parameter information that could support instance-level reproducibility if mapped into formal provenance models; currently, they are typically limited to project-specific documentation, making reproducibility dependent on local knowledge.[10](https://wiki.esipfed.org/Attribute_Convention_for_Data_Discovery_1-3)

In sum, standards like OGC O&M, HL7 FHIR Observation/Provenance, W3C PROV-O, and RO-Crate provide the strongest basis for instance-level reproducibility of exposure values, while most catalog and dataset standards remain at higher levels of abstraction. A robust exposure metadata registry would need to combine these provenance-rich standards with dataset-level schemas to achieve both discovery and reproducibility.

## 7. Pipeline Sidecars and Their Relationship to Standards

### 7.1 Nature and role of pipeline sidecar metadata

Pipeline sidecars, as considered in this review, are structured metadata files or tables that accompany specific analytic workflows or pipelines, documenting inputs, parameters, intermediate outputs, and processing steps in ways that are tailored to the pipeline but not standardized across projects.[10](https://wiki.esipfed.org/Attribute_Convention_for_Data_Discovery_1-3) In environmental-exposure science, such sidecars are common in geocoding pipelines, where auxiliary files record the geocoding precision and source for each address, and in exposure assignment workflows, where configuration files specify which environmental datasets, interpolation methods, and temporal windows are used. These sidecars are often implemented as JSON, YAML, CSV, or XML files stored alongside code and data, and they are essential for reproducibility within a project, especially when combined with version-controlled code and environment management tools.

Unlike formal metadata standards, pipeline sidecars are usually ad hoc, created by project teams without broader governance or community consensus, and their schemas may change as pipelines evolve. They may reuse elements from general standards, such as ISO 19115 attributes or PROV-O relations, but often they use local naming and structures that are documented only in project documentation or code comments.[10](https://wiki.esipfed.org/Attribute_Convention_for_Data_Discovery_1-3) From the perspective of schema engineers building an exposure metadata registry, pipeline sidecars represent rich sources of provenance and configuration information that could be harvested and mapped into standardized schemas, but they also pose challenges because their lack of standardization and semantics complicates automated interpretation.

### 7.2 Typical structures in exposure pipelines

In exposure pipelines, sidecar structures commonly encode information about environmental datasets used as inputs, such as file paths, dataset identifiers, and temporal ranges, as well as geocoding details such as coordinate systems, geocoding services, match scores, and error flags. They may also record parameters for exposure assignment algorithms, such as buffer sizes, interpolation methods (e.g., nearest-neighbor, inverse distance weighting, kriging), lag windows, thresholds for defining events (e.g., heat waves), and modeling options for time-series regression. In some pipelines, sidecars include log entries for each run, noting start and end times, user or process IDs, and environment details such as software versions and library dependencies, which are critical for reproducibility but rarely captured in general metadata standards.[10](https://wiki.esipfed.org/Attribute_Convention_for_Data_Discovery_1-3)

Sidecars may also contain structured metadata for cohort data, such as the mapping between local ID fields and standardized person identifiers, inclusion and exclusion criteria applied during preprocessing, and transformations applied to raw data. These elements align with DDI’s process metadata and OMOP’s ETL (extract-transform-load) documentation practices, suggesting that pipeline sidecars could be partially standardized by adopting elements from these frameworks.[12](https://www.ohdsi.org/data-standardization/)[16](https://ddialliance.org) Furthermore, some pipelines may embed PROV-O or similar provenance information directly in sidecars, but this is currently uncommon and requires explicit design efforts.

### 7.3 Distinguishing pipeline sidecars from general standards

For the purposes of this review, pipeline sidecars are distinguished from general metadata standards in that they lack formal specifications, governance, and intent of broad applicability. While general standards like DCAT, ISO 19115, OGC O&M, OMOP CDM, FHIR, PROV-O, and RO-Crate are designed to be reusable across projects and producers, with documented schemas and implementation guidance, pipeline sidecars are designed primarily to serve the needs of specific workflows.[7](https://www.iso.org/standard/53798.html)[9](http://www.opengeospatial.org/standards/om)[12](https://www.ohdsi.org/data-standardization/)[15](https://build.fhir.org/ig/HL7/fhir-us-phpl/artifacts.html)[17](https://www.researchobject.org/ro-crate/specification/1.1/index.html) Even when pipeline sidecars adopt structures from general standards, their local modifications and incomplete implementations may limit interoperability.

Nonetheless, pipeline sidecars often capture the most detailed provenance information available for exposure assignments, including value-level derivation details that are rarely recorded elsewhere. As such, they are important targets for schema engineering: by analyzing patterns across sidecars and mapping their content into general provenance and metadata standards, one can enrich exposure metadata registries with detailed derivation information while gradually moving toward standardized representations. In this review, pipeline sidecars are treated as a separate class of structured formats—useful and rich but not standards—and they are not individually cataloged unless they have evolved into widely used formats with published specifications.

## 9. Synthesis and Recommendations for Exposure Metadata Registry Design

The landscape surveyed in this report reveals a rich but fragmented ecosystem of metadata standards and structured formats relevant to environmental-exposure data. General-purpose dataset and catalog standards such as DCAT, DCAT-AP, DataCite, Dublin Core, schema.org Dataset, and RSLP Collection Description provide robust frameworks for dataset-level discovery and citation, ensuring that exposure datasets are findable and citable.[3](https://semiceu.github.io/DCAT-AP-reuse-guidelines/)[4](https://schema.org/Dataset)[11](https://schema.datacite.org)[19](http://www.ukoln.ac.uk/metadata/rslp/schema/) Geospatial and earth-observation standards such as ISO 19115-1, ISO/TS 19139, OGC Observations and Measurements, GeoSPARQL, STAC, ACDD, and CF conventions enrich this picture by providing spatial-temporal description and variable-level semantics for environmental datasets that underpin exposure assessments.[7](https://www.iso.org/standard/53798.html)[8](https://www.iso.org/obp/ui/)[9](http://www.opengeospatial.org/standards/om)[10](https://wiki.esipfed.org/Attribute_Convention_for_Data_Discovery_1-3)[6](https://stacspec.org)[13](https://www.ogc.org/standards/geosparql/)

Clinical and epidemiological standards such as OMOP CDM, FHIR Observation/Provenance, and DDI offer structured models for linking exposure measures to health outcomes at the person level and documenting study designs and variable derivations.[12](https://www.ohdsi.org/data-standardization/)[15](https://build.fhir.org/ig/HL7/fhir-us-phpl/artifacts.html)[16](https://ddialliance.org) Provenance-focused standards like W3C PROV-O and packaging frameworks like RO-Crate provide formal tools to describe derivation chains and bundle data and workflows, enabling reproducibility when carefully implemented.[https://www.w3.org/TR/prov-o/](https://www.w3.org/TR/prov-o/)[17](https://www.researchobject.org/ro-crate/specification/1.1/index.html) Pipeline sidecars, while not standardized, capture detailed configuration and process information that could be harnessed to enrich provenance in exposure metadata registries.[10](https://wiki.esipfed.org/Attribute_Convention_for_Data_Discovery_1-3)

From the perspective of instance-level reproducibility of exposure values, the most promising standards are OGC Observations and Measurements (and related sensor standards), HL7 FHIR Observation with Provenance, W3C PROV-O, and RO-Crate, all of which can represent individual observations or entities and link them to procedures and provenance chains.[9](http://www.opengeospatial.org/standards/om)[15](https://build.fhir.org/ig/HL7/fhir-us-phpl/artifacts.html)[17](https://www.researchobject.org/ro-crate/specification/1.1/index.html)[https://www.w3.org/TR/prov-o/](https://www.w3.org/TR/prov-o/) Dataset-level standards remain essential for discovery and citation but must be combined with these more granular models to achieve full reproducibility. Exposure-science–specific profiles and extensions, such as EPHT metadata profiles and OMOP GIS extensions, illustrate how domain communities can adapt general standards to their needs, but they are still under development and not yet universally adopted.[1](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18849771/)[14](https://www.ohdsi.org/web/wiki/doku.php?id=projects%3Aworkgroups%3Agis)

For schema engineers designing an environmental-exposure metadata registry, several recommendations emerge. First, leverage general standards for dataset-level discovery and citation, particularly DCAT-AP, DataCite, schema.org Dataset (with science-on-schema.org guidance), and ISO 19115-1/19139, to ensure that exposure datasets and environmental inputs are well described and discoverable.[3](https://semiceu.github.io/DCAT-AP-reuse-guidelines/)[5](https://github.com/esipfed/science-on-schema.org)[7](https://www.iso.org/standard/53798.html)[11](https://schema.datacite.org) Second, adopt geospatial and observational standards like OGC O&M, ACDD, CF, GeoSPARQL, and STAC to describe environmental observations and assets, making exposure-related environmental data interoperable and queryable.[9](http://www.opengeospatial.org/standards/om)[10](https://wiki.esipfed.org/Attribute_Convention_for_Data_Discovery_1-3)[6](https://stacspec.org)[13](https://www.ogc.org/standards/geosparql/)

Third, integrate clinical and epidemiological standards such as OMOP CDM exposures and FHIR exposure profiles for representing exposures linked to individuals and health outcomes, ensuring compatibility with healthcare data infrastructures.[12](https://www.ohdsi.org/data-standardization/)[14](https://www.ohdsi.org/web/wiki/doku.php?id=projects%3Aworkgroups%3Agis)[15](https://build.fhir.org/ig/HL7/fhir-us-phpl/artifacts.html) Fourth, use provenance standards like W3C PROV-O and RO-Crate to capture derivation chains and package workflows, mapping pipeline sidecar contents into these formal models wherever possible.[https://www.w3.org/TR/prov-o/](https://www.w3.org/TR/prov-o/)[17](https://www.researchobject.org/ro-crate/specification/1.1/index.html) Fifth, treat controlled vocabularies and ontologies (such as QUDT, CF standard names, ENVO, GCMD Science Keywords, GeoNames, and CMECS) as value sets referenced within metadata fields rather than as metadata schemas themselves, ensuring semantic interoperability while preserving the distinction between schemas and vocabularies.[10](https://wiki.esipfed.org/Attribute_Convention_for_Data_Discovery_1-3)[18](https://www.qudt.org/2.1/catalog/qudt-catalog.html)

Finally, recognize that instance-level reproducibility of exposure values will require careful design decisions about provenance granularity and practicality. In many cases, workflow-level provenance—recording which environmental datasets, cohort data, and algorithms were used—is sufficient for reproducibility of exposure assignment procedures, while full value-level provenance may be reserved for critical subsets of data or for high-stakes analyses.[20](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1257643/)[17](https://www.researchobject.org/ro-crate/specification/1.1/index.html) By combining the strengths of existing standards and mapping pipeline sidecar content into them, an environmental-exposure metadata registry can achieve a balance between discoverability, interpretability, reproducibility, and feasibility, laying the groundwork for robust reuse and meta-analysis of exposure–health data across studies and domains.

## 10. Conclusion

The challenge of describing environmental-exposure data in ways that support reuse, reproducibility, and meta-analysis is multifaceted, reflecting the cross-domain nature of exposure science, which sits at the intersection of environmental monitoring, geospatial analysis, clinical data, and computational modeling.[20](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1257643/) This review has mapped the landscape of metadata standards and structured formats relevant to this challenge, identifying key general standards, domain-specific models, provenance frameworks, and pipeline-specific formats, and evaluating their suitability for different units of description and provenance depths. It has also clarified the distinction between metadata schemas and controlled vocabularies, ensuring that schema engineers focus on standards that define structure and semantics for metadata rather than on ontologies that supply terms.

The analysis shows that while many standards exist for dataset-level discovery and citation—such as DCAT, DataCite, schema.org Dataset, ISO 19115, ACDD, and DDI—only a subset offer the instance-level observational and provenance capabilities needed for full reproducibility of exposure values, notably OGC Observations and Measurements, HL7 FHIR Observation/Provenance, W3C PROV-O, and RO-Crate.[7](https://www.iso.org/standard/53798.html)[9](http://www.opengeospatial.org/standards/om)[15](https://build.fhir.org/ig/HL7/fhir-us-phpl/artifacts.html)[17](https://www.researchobject.org/ro-crate/specification/1.1/index.html)[https://www.w3.org/TR/prov-o/](https://www.w3.org/TR/prov-o/) Clinical models like OMOP CDM and emerging exposure extensions make it possible to store and analyze exposure data alongside health outcomes, while geospatial standards ensure that environmental datasets and exposure maps are well described and interoperable. Provenance frameworks and FAIR-packaging standards provide the glue to connect these pieces into coherent derivation narratives.

For schema engineers, the path forward involves selecting and combining these standards to build layered metadata models that capture dataset-level discovery, variable-level semantics, observation-level exposure assignments, and workflow-level provenance, tailored to the needs of environmental and health researchers. By doing so, and by systematically mapping rich but ad hoc pipeline sidecar metadata into these models, it will be possible to transform environmental-exposure data from isolated project artifacts into interoperable, reproducible resources that can be pooled and analyzed across studies and regions, advancing our understanding of environmental determinants of health and supporting evidence-based policy and intervention.[1](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18849771/)[2](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13022413/)[20](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1257643/)
